# The Dandelion Girl (1961)

**Medium:** Short Story
**Author:** Robert F. Young

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

A middle-aged lawyer named Mark Randolph escapes his demanding career for a quiet vacation in rural Pennsylvania, where he encounters a mysterious young woman with dandelion-colored hair on a hilltop overlooking the countryside. The girl, Julie Danvers, claims to be from the year 2201—240 years in the future—and explains that her father's time machine allows her to visit the past. As Mark spends his vacation days with Julie, their connection deepens into something unexpected and profound. What begins as an intriguing encounter with what seems like a delusional young woman evolves into a touching meditation on love, time, and the nature of destiny. This is gentle, hopeful science fiction that explores the possibility that love can transcend not just distance, but the boundaries of time itself.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

Mark Randolph is a 44-year-old lawyer who runs his own small law firm. Overworked and exhausted, he manages to take only two vacations per year. His second vacation of 1961 begins in September, and his wife Anne cannot accompany him—she's been called for jury duty. Mark settles into a cabin near a lake in a wooded area, with a hill overlooking a small city in the distance.

One day early in his vacation, Mark climbs the hill and encounters a young woman sitting there. She introduces herself as Julie Danvers—a 21-year-old with blue eyes and dandelion-colored hair, wearing a white dress. She tells Mark an extraordinary tale: she is from the year 2201, and her father invented a time machine that allows her to travel back to the 1960s. Mark doesn't fully believe her but plays along with her imaginative story.

They talk for hours, and Julie returns the next day, this time wearing a blue dress. She tells Mark more about her father—an invalid she cares for—and explains how she bends the laws of the "time police" to visit the past. She mentions the Novikov self-consistency principle, two decades before physicist Igor Novikov formally formulated it. Mark finds himself falling in love with Julie, but he's wracked with guilt due to his marriage to Anne. He hasn't ever looked at another woman since marrying her.

On their third meeting, Julie wears a yellow dress. They continue to bond, making plans to meet again. But when Mark returns the next day, Julie is nowhere to be found. He returns day after day, disappointed. On the fourth day of searching, he finally finds her—but this time she's wearing a black dress and is crying. Her father has died. She came back one more time to tell him that her father's personal time machine is breaking down. Without him, she cannot repair it, and she'll only be able to travel back in time once more before it fails completely. She confesses her love for Mark before leaving.

Mark returns home from his vacation, trying to hide his emotional transformation from Anne. He can sense that she knows something has changed about him. On Sunday afternoons, he drives to the hilltop in the country, but Julie never appears.

Then one rainy day in mid-November, Anne goes out to play bingo. Bored, Mark goes into the attic to find jigsaw puzzles. He accidentally knocks his wife's old suitcase off a shelf, and the rusted lock breaks open. Inside, he finds the same white dress that Julie had worn on their first meeting. The realization hits him like a thunderbolt: his wife Anne is actually Julie—or rather, Julianne. She had traveled back in time when Mark was in his twenties, assumed the name "Anne," and applied to work as his secretary.

Mark grabs a raincoat and runs outside to meet Anne, who is getting off the bus from her bingo night. He reaches out to touch her rain-wet cheek, and Anne knows that he finally understands. The fear she had carried in her eyes for twenty years disappears. Mark and Anne walk home together, hand in hand, in the rain.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

At its heart, "The Dandelion Girl" explores the nature of love and destiny. Young asks whether love can be so powerful that it transcends time itself—whether a person could love someone so deeply that they would travel centuries back in time to be with them, sacrificing everything to build a life together in the past. The twist ending reframes the entire narrative: what seemed like a brief, impossible summer romance was actually the foundation of a twenty-year marriage.

The story also examines the weight of secrets and the courage required to truly know another person. Anne/Julie carried her secret for two decades, watching her husband search for "Julie" on Sunday afternoons, never revealing the truth until he was ready to understand. Her patience represents a profound kind of love—the willingness to wait for someone to catch up to the truth.

Young also subtly explores the concept of time as cyclical rather than linear. By invoking the Novikov self-consistency principle (before it was formally named), Julie suggests that time travel doesn't create paradoxes—everything that happened was always meant to happen. Mark and Anne were always meant to be together, across all of time.

Unlike much of science fiction that focuses on technology, aliens, or dystopia, "The Dandelion Girl" is fundamentally a love story. It's optimistic and tender, suggesting that at the core of human existence, love is the most powerful force—even more powerful than time itself.
