# The Fantastic Adventures of Unico (1981)

## The Pitch

A young unicorn named Unico possesses a magical gift that angers the gods: he brings happiness to everyone around him simply by existing. The gods decree this power is theirs alone to wield and order the West Wind to take Unico to the Hill of Oblivion, where his memories will be erased forever. But the compassionate West Wind defies the gods and instead transports Unico through time and space to different locations, giving him chances to help those in need before being whisked away again. In this 1981 musical anime film, Unico befriends Chao, an abandoned black cat who dreams of becoming a witch's companion, and together they face the sinister Baron DeGhost, a villain who seeks to trap Chao in his demonic realm. Part fairy tale, part philosophical meditation on love and loss, this forgotten gem from Tezuka Productions offers a surprisingly dark and emotionally complex story wrapped in gorgeous hand-painted animation.

## The Truth

The film opens in ancient Greece where Unico first met Psyche, a mortal girl so beautiful that Venus, goddess of love, grew jealous. Venus commanded the West Wind to take Unico away to the Hill of Oblivion so that Psyche would forget her happiness and lose her beauty. The West Wind, however, took pity on Unico and defied the gods, transporting him instead to a forest where he would be hidden from divine scrutiny.

In the present day, Unico finds himself alone in a mystical forest where he encounters Beezle, the son of the Demon of Solitude. Beezle is a mischievous imp who tricks Unico into playing dangerous games, ultimately causing Unico to fall into the sea. As Unico drowns, Beezle realizes the depths of his loneliness and uses his own horn to save Unico, transforming him into a Mighty Unicorn—a powerful winged form. In this moment of friendship, Unico grants Beezle his own horn before the West Wind returns to spirit Unico away.

Unico is then transported to another forest where he meets Chao (named Katy in the English dub), an abandoned black cat who spends her days searching for a witch to make her their familiar. Chao believes that having a witch master would give her purpose and dignity. Unico and Chao become fast friends, and they take shelter with an old woman whom Chao mistakenly believes is a witch.

Unico secretly grants Chao her wish by transforming her into a human girl, but only temporarily each day. During one of these transformations, Chao encounters the enigmatic Baron DeGhost, a handsome but sinister lord who invites her to his manor. Unico grows suspicious and follows her, discovering that Baron is actually a demon who plans to trap Chao forever.

During the climax, Baron transforms into his true demonic form and attacks Unico. In the ensuing battle, Unico is killed and his horn is broken. Chao's desperate tears of love and grief revive Unico, transforming him once again into the Mighty Unicorn. Unico defeats Baron by sending him into the underworld, but the cost is devastating—the West Wind arrives to take Unico away, erasing his memories of Chao forever. The film ends with Chao, now a cat again, calling out Unico's name as the West Wind carries her friend away to another time and place.

## The Core

The Fantastic Adventures of Unico is a profound meditation on impermanence, love, and the cost of happiness. Unico can bring joy to others, but he can never stay—the very act of loving others means he must eventually leave them. This creates a poignant theme about the transient nature of happiness and connection: true love sometimes means accepting loss. The gods in this story represent a cruel kind of perfectionism—their jealousy of Unico's ability to spread happiness reflects how societies often punish those who give freely without seeking power or control.

Chao's journey represents the universal search for belonging and identity. As an abandoned cat, she is willing to become something other than herself (a human, a witch's familiar) just to feel valued. Her transformation into a human girl is both a wish granted and a loss of self—a theme Tezuka explores with surprising depth for a children's film. The villain Baron DeGhost embodies the emptiness of superficial beauty and the danger of manipulation; he presents himself as charming but ultimately preys on vulnerability.

Visually, the film is a masterpiece of hand-painted cel animation featuring lush backgrounds and emotionally expressive character designs. The musical numbers, performed by narrator Iruka, give the film a fairy-tale quality that contrasts sharply with its dark themes of abandonment, death, and eternal separation. The film asks whether it's better to have loved and lost than never to have loved at all—and suggests that the pain of loss is precisely what makes love meaningful.
