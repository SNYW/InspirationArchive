# Dreamweb (1994)

**Medium:** Video Game

**Creator/Author:** Creative Reality (Developer) / Empire Interactive (Publisher)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Dreamweb is a 1994 point-and-click cyberpunk adventure game developed by the small UK studio Creative Reality and published by Empire Interactive. Set in a dystopian near-future England, the game puts players in the role of Ryan, a bartender who has been plagued by strange dreams of an entity known as the Dreamweb—a mystical force that recruits humans to eliminate "seven evils" threatening to break loose and plunge humanity into chaos. What makes Dreamweb distinctive is its unflinching embrace of mature themes: violence, brief full frontal nudity, and an overall tone that refuses to soften its edges. The game draws heavy visual and thematic influence from Ridley Scott's Blade Runner, while its narrative structure—centered on the concept of seven deadly sins—owes a debt to the 1986 film Highlander. Originally released for MS-DOS and Amiga, Dreamweb was controversial enough to be refused classification in Australia upon initial release due to its explicit content. The game has since faded into obscurity, remembered primarily by dedicated adventure game enthusiasts and those who appreciate the darker corners of 1990s gaming.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The game opens with protagonist Ryan waking from another recurring dream of the Dreamweb—a mystical web-like entity that exists between dimensions. In these dreams, the "master monk" of the Keepers appears to Ryan, charging him with a mission: kill seven individuals known as the "seven evils" who are on the verge of uniting and breaking the Dreamweb, which would send humanity into irreversible chaos.

Ryan begins his journey in his everyday life as a bartender in a grim, rain-soaked dystopian city that appears to be somewhere in England (the use of British English terms like "lift" and "bonnet" confirms the UK setting). His first target is David Crane, a rock star staying at a local hotel. After obtaining a gun through his friend Louis, Ryan infiltrates the hotel, kills Crane and two bodyguards, and is transported to the Dreamweb where he learns of his second target: a military general named Sterling.

Ryan's journey continues as he tracks down and eliminates each of the seven evils. General Sterling is killed in a television studio when Ryan drops a heavy crate on him during a live talk show broadcast. The third target is Sartain, the owner of the company where Ryan's girlfriend Eden works—Ryan pursues him to a rooftop where he kills him with a hover car. The fourth target is Juliet Chappel, whom Ryan discovers in a destroyed house, though he initially believes her dead before learning her location leads to a church.

Ryan's fifth target is Diane Underwood, found at a heavily guarded beach house. Ryan sabotages the security system by dousing the control box in water, causing the security turret to go haywire and kill Underwood. The sixth target is Father O'Rourke, found dead in the secret underground passages beneath a church—leaving only one final evil: Dr. Beckett.

In the climax, Ryan locates Beckett in the subway system, linked to the church's underground passages. As Beckett tries to flee, he is run over by a train. After completing all seven kills, Ryan returns to the Dreamweb one last time, where the master monk thanks him—but also reveals Ryan's fate. Returning to the real world for the final time, Ryan is shot dead by police for his numerous murders. His last vision is of his soul entering the Dreamweb, suggesting either transcendence or descent into madness—the game intentionally leaves this ambiguous.

A prequel diary included with the game, "Diary of a (Mad?) Man," strongly implies that Ryan may have fabricated the entire Dreamweb scenario as a symptom of psychosis—suggesting the entire game may represent a psychotic break rather than actual supernatural events.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Ambiguity of Reality and Madness:** Dreamweb presents a fundamental question about the nature of Ryan's mission: is the Dreamweb a real supernatural entity, or is Ryan simply descending into madness? The included "Diary of a (Mad?) Man" deliberately undermines the player assumptions, suggesting the entire adventure may be a psychotic episode. This ambiguity places the game in dialogue with films like Fight Club and Jacob's Ladder, where the line between reality and delusion blurs. The thematic choice to leave this unresolved creates a disturbing undercurrent: even as players complete the "heroic" mission, they must confront the possibility that they have been murders committed by an complicit in multiple unreliable narrator.

**Violence as Catharsis and Commentary:** Unlike most adventure games of the era, Dreamweb places no moral weight on killing. Ryan murders seven people throughout the game, often in graphic ways—shooting bodyguards, dropping a man to his death on live television, destroying a hover car with its occupant inside. The game neither celebrates nor condemns this violence; it simply presents it. This approach was groundbreaking (and controversial) in 1994, presaging later games that would explore the psychological cost of violence. The designers seemed to be asking: what does it mean to be an instrument of cosmic "justice" when that justice requires becoming a murderer?

**Cyberpunk Aesthetics and British Identity:** While Dreamweb clearly owes debts to Blade Runner's visual language—similar typography, the white-on-black credits, the rain-soaked dystopian city—it grounds these familiar cyberpunk tropes in a specifically British context. The use of UK English, the grey urban environments, and the localized references all serve to translate the American cyberpunk aesthetic into a British framework. This made Dreamweb a unique entry in the cyberpunk gaming canon—neither a Hollywood production nor a Japanese import, but a distinctly British take on the genre.

**The Self-Destructive Quest:** The game's ending—where Ryan completes his mission only to be killed by police—embodies a tragic structure. The "hero" achieves his cosmic purpose but is destroyed by the mundane consequences of his actions. This mirrors the Highlander influence, where "there can be only one" leads to pyrrhic victories. Ryan's final fate suggests that fulfilling a cosmic mission does not guarantee redemption or survival; sometimes doing the "right" thing still leads to destruction.
