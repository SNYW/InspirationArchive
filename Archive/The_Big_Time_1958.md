# The Big Time (1958)

**Tags:** 🌍 USA | 🎭 Cerebral | 📚 Hard-SF, Time-Travel | 🧬 Time-Travel, Exploration

**Medium:** Novella
**Creator/Author:** Fritz Leiber

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a pocket of spacetime outside the universe, a small group of soldiers from different eras of human history—and beyond—recuperate between battles in an endless temporal war. Greta, a young woman from the early 20th century, serves as an "Entertainer" at this waystation, tending to the psychological needs of warriors plucked from ancient Rome, World War II, and even alien futures. The catch: no one truly knows who their enemies are, why they're fighting, or what the ultimate goal of the Change War even is. When tensions rise and an atomic bomb enters the mix, Greta must navigate loyalties, secrets, and the existential weight of being a tiny cog in a cosmic machine. It's time travel as you've never seen it—not action-packed heroics, but a meditative, almost theatrical exploration of conflict, fate, and the strange dignity of playing a role you never auditioned for.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The story unfolds entirely within the Place, a Recuperation Station that exists in an artificial bubble of spacetime called the Big Time, separate from the normal universe. This station serves as a rest and recovery point for soldiers fighting in the Change War—a vast temporal conflict between two mysterious factions known only as the Spiders and the Snakes. Both sides have mastered time travel and fight by altering historical events throughout time, recruiting soldiers from every era of human history and beyond.

Our narrator is Greta, a young woman from the early 20th century who was recruited into the Spiders and now works as an "Entertainer" at the Place—a role that blends prostitute, nurse, and psychotherapist. Her colleagues include Sidney Lessingham (the Place's manager), Lillian Foster (another Entertainer), Doc Beauvis (an alcoholic physician), and Maud (a weary nurse).

The station receives a group of soldiers: Bruce Marchant (a British RAF pilot), Erich von Emmelmann (a German Wehrmacht officer), Kaby (a Cretan Amazon), Mark (a Roman soldier), Sevensee (a Venusian), and Illy (a Lunan). These soldiers have brought an atomic bomb into the Place, intending to use it in a future battle.

As the soldiers rest and interact, the narrative explores the nature of the Change War. The Spiders seem to be losing ground, and Bruce begins to question the entire conflict. He attempts to convince his fellow soldiers to abandon the war and sue for peace—a radical proposal that would require the soldiers to remain in the pocket universe indefinitely, hiding from their respective timelines.

Erich strongly opposes this idea, and tension escalates when he primes the atomic bomb, threatening to kill everyone unless they're returned to the main timeline. Meanwhile, Lillian has hidden the Maintainer—a crucial machine that keeps the Place situated within the Big Time—in the art gallery, disguised as a sculpture.

Greta discovers the hidden Maintainer and restores it. Bruce manages to disarm the bomb. The group agrees to keep silent about Bruce's proposed mutiny, as each character has something to hide from the incident.

In the aftermath, Greta discusses the future of the Change War with Illy, the Lunan. Illy concludes that the Change War represents the next phase in cosmic evolution—a transformation as significant as when animals evolved from plants. The story ends with this unsettling, almost nihilistic observation: the war isn't about winning or losing in any traditional sense, but is somehow fundamental to the universe's progression.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Fritz Leiber's "The Big Time" is a masterclass in doing the most with the least. Rather than depicting grand battles across history, Leiber confines his story to a single room and uses it to explore profound questions about conflict, identity, and purpose.

The Change War itself is Leiber's metaphor for the human condition. The Spiders and Snakes never explain their ideologies or goals—because there may not be any. They're fighting simply because fighting is what they do. This mirrors how nations and groups throughout history have clashed over abstractions—freedom, power, revenge—without truly understanding why.

The characters represent humanity's diversity: warriors from Rome, Nazis, Allied soldiers, even alien futures fighting side by side or against each other based purely on which faction recruited them. This arbitrary sorting exposes the absurdity of tribalism. Erich and Bruce mortal enemies in 1944, now comrades—highlighting how quickly "enemy" and "ally" can flip when the context changes.

Greta's role as an Entertainer is equally significant. She's providing comfort and meaning to soldiers who may die in the next battle or be erased from existence by temporal changes. It's a poignant meditation on how we find purpose in meaningless circumstances—by creating connection, even when the larger context is absurd.

Leiber also explores free will versus determinism. These soldiers have been plucked from their timelines and forced to fight in a war they don't understand. Yet they form bonds, make choices, and find meaning. Perhaps free will isn't about grand significance but about the small dignities we create within systems we can't control.

The ending—that the Change War is evolution itself—is both terrifying and strangely comforting. It suggests that even cosmic futility might be part of something larger. We're all participants in the Big Time, whether we understand it or not.
