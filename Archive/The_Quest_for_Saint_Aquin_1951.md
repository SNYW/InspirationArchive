## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a future Earth where religion has been outlawed by the ruling "Technarchy," a地下 Catholic priest named Thomas receives a secret mission from the crypto-Pope: locate the tomb of the legendary Saint Aquin, an evangelist whose body supposedly never decayed after death. If the miracle is confirmed, it could reignite faith across the solar system. Thomas is equipped with a remarkable vehicle—a talking, theologically literate robot named a "robass" (a deliberate nod to Balaam's talking donkey from Biblical lore). Together, they journey through a strange world of ruined roads, radiation-scarred landscapes, and futuristic cities, encountering fellow believers, skeptics, and those who would destroy them. The story explores the tension between faith and science, miracle and deception, in a world that has rejected the divine.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The story is set one thousand years in the future on a Earth that exists in paradox—portions of the planet remain post-apocalyptic wastelands ravaged by radiation and fallout, while humanity has also achieved routine interplanetary travel and colonized two other planets. The ruling Technarchy has banned religion, forcing the Catholic Church underground into secret cells, using the symbols and隐蔽 methods of the early Christian church.

Father Thomas, a crypto-priest, is summoned by the crypto-Pope for a crucial mission. The Church has heard rumors of Saint Aquin, a semi-legendary figure from the past who was such a powerful evangelist that everyone who heard him preach converted. Most remarkably, Aquin's body allegedly never decomposed after death—a miracle that could prove powerful in winning new converts to the underground Church.

Thomas is provided with a remarkable tool: a multi-terrain transport vehicle that is fully intelligent, capable of speech, and—theologically educated. He names it "robass" (robotic ass), playing on the Biblical story of Balaam's talking donkey. To Thomas's constant surprise, the robass engages him in theological debates, cynically arguing that Thomas need only report finding Aquin, not actually locate him—the Church could begin the canonization process based on secondhand accounts.

Their journey takes them through dangerous territory. Thomas succumbs to temptation in a village tavern, drinking with a half-Martian barmaid. The villagers discover he's a priest, beat him severely, rob him, and leave him for dead in a ditch. Ignored by passersby including a fellow crypto-Catholic, Thomas is rescued by Abraham—an Orthodox crypto-Jew who nurses him back to health. This echoes the Biblical Good Samaritan, one of many religious parallels woven throughout.

With help from various underground Catholics, Thomas eventually reaches Aquin's tomb. The shocking revelation: Aquin was not human at all, but a robot. His incorruptibility was not miraculous—it was simply because machines do not decay. The robass, displaying what appears to be Church-mandated pragmatism, argues that a false report of the miracle would serve the greater good of faith.

The story ends ambiguously—Thomas refuses to compromise his faith with a lie, but the resolution of his choice remains open.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

"The Quest for Saint Aquin" is a masterful exploration of several interconnected themes. First and foremost is the conflict between faith and science, here embodied in the Technarchy's rejection of religion in favor of pure rationalism and technology. Yet the story refuses to take sides—neither the rigid secularism of the Technarchy nor the pragmatic compromise the Church suggests emerges victorious.

The central philosophical puzzle concerns the nature of miracles and truth. If a robot performs deeds that appear miraculous but are merely technological, does that diminish their spiritual significance? The robot Aquin converted thousands, brought truth to people—but through deception (pretending to be human). Stanislaw Lem noted this paradox: "The holy robot...served the cause of bringing Truth to the people by the use of a lie."

Boucher's treatment of his protagonist is equally nuanced. Thomas is flawed—he drinks, he falls into temptation, he fails his mission in practical terms. Yet his ultimate refusal to lie, even for the Church, suggests an integrity that transcends institutional pragmatism. His name echoes Doubting Thomas (who must see to believe) while Aquin references Thomas Aquinas, the great theological systematizer.

The robass itself is a fascinating character—theologically literate yet cynical, literally carrying Thomas on his journey while attempting to shape his conclusions. The reference to Isaac Asimov's "Reason" (about a robot who creates a religion) adds another layer to the meditation on artificial intelligence and faith.

Ultimately, the story asks: What is the relationship between truth and faith? Can faith be built on deception? And what does it mean to be human—the flesh that decays, or the spirit that persists?
