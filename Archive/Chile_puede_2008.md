## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Chile puede (Spanish for "Chile can do it") is a 2008 Chilean science fiction comedy film that tells the story of an illegal private space venture orchestrated by a wealthy tycoon in Chile. Directed by Ricardo Larraín and written by Boris Quercia, the film takes a satirical look at national ambition, scientific endeavor, and the chaos that ensues when amateurism meets the complexities of space travel. Set against the backdrop of Chile's arid north, where real-life astronomical observatories dominate the landscape, the film blends absurdist humor with a surprisingly heartfelt story about national pride and human ingenuity. The film was released in Chile in January 2008 and earned approximately $365,966 at the box office.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film follows a group of Chilean scientists working in the northern desert region of Chile, preparing for what would be the country's first-ever space mission. The project is financed by Patricio, a wealthy and eccentric millionaire who serves as the visionary (and somewhat unhinged) mind behind the venture. The team includes engineers and scientists who have been working tirelessly to get the mission off the ground.

However, just before the scheduled launch, an industrial dispute erupts between the scientists and engineers over wages and working conditions. The workers walk out of the ground control center in protest, leaving the project director in a precarious position. With no other options, the director decides to proceed with the launch anyway, accompanied only by Guillermo, a Spanish high school teacher who has been selected as the astronaut.

As the mission progresses, the director realizes to his horror that he does not actually know how to bring Guillermo back to Earth alive. The spacecraft successfully reaches orbit, but the team has no plan for re-entry or safe return. Guillermo is left stranded, orbiting the Earth with no way to come home.

The situation becomes increasingly desperate until an old Russian scientist enters the picture. This scientist, who previously worked on the Soviet Sputnik program, now lives in Chile in retirement. Together with a cosmonaut who happens to be aboard the aging MIR space station, they devise an improvised solution to save Guillermo: using the MIR to give the Chilean spacecraft a push back toward Earth's orbit. Through this unlikely international cooperation between a Chilean mission, a retired Russian scientist, and a MIR cosmonaut, the spacecraft is guided back to Earth for a dramatic (if unorthodox) return.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**National Ambition and Postcolonial Identity:** The film's title, "Chile puede" (Chile can do it), directly references the famous "Yes, we can" motivational phrase, but applied to a national context. It satirizes the desire of developing nations to prove themselves on the global stage, particularly in prestigious scientific and technological endeavors traditionally dominated by superpowers. The film questions whether ambition alone is enough to compete with established space programs.

**Scientific amateurism and Hubris:** The film critiques the dangers of undertaking highly complex scientific missions without proper planning, funding, or expertise. The characters' lack of knowledge about basic aspects of space travel (like how to return safely) serves as a comedic but pointed commentary on the risks of scientific hubris and inadequate preparation.

**Labor and Class Dynamics:** The labor dispute that precipitates the crisis highlights the often-overlooked labor tensions in scientific and technological projects. The film suggests that scientific achievement doesn't happen in a vacuum—economic and social realities always impact technical endeavors.

**International Cooperation:** The resolution of the film hinges on unexpected international collaboration—a retired Russian scientist and an astronaut on the MIR space station. This outcome emphasizes that space exploration, despite nationalistic motivations, ultimately requires global cooperation and shared knowledge.

**The Absurdity of Heroism:** Guillermo, the "astronaut," is not a trained space explorer but a high school teacher. The film plays with the idea of the hero, subverting traditional sci-fi tropes by having an ordinary person stumble into an extraordinary situation through circumstances beyond their control.
