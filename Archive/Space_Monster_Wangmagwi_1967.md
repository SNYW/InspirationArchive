## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Space Monster Wangmagwi is a 1967 South Korean kaiju film and the oldest surviving Korean kaiju film, notable as the first all-Korean science fiction film ever made. Directed by Kwon Hyeok-jin and inspired by Japanese and American monster movies such as Godzilla and King Kong, the film tells the story of an alien invasion of Earth using a giant monster to attack South Korea. The plot centers on air force pilot Oh Jeong-hwan and his betrothed Ahn-hee, whose wedding day is interrupted by the arrival of the space monster Wangmagwi. The film is remarkable for its unique blend of science fiction action, comedic vignettes, and subtle political commentary referencing the Korean War, making it a fascinating artifact of 1960s Korean cinema.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens with preparations for the wedding of South Korean air force pilot Oh Jeong-hwan and his bride-to-be Ahn-hee, scheduled for the next day. Oh Jeong-hwan is called away to his air force base due to an unspecified emergency, presumed to be related to an incoming typhoon.

Meanwhile, in space, aliens from the Gamma star system approach Earth with plans for invasion. They decide to destroy humanity using a monster, choosing South Korea as their first target. The monster is initially human-sized but grows enormous upon reaching Earth due to the planet's lower gravity.

Seoul begins evacuating as the monster approaches, but Ahn-her and her mother linger, determined to go through with their planned wedding. They evacuate too late, and the monster kidnaps Ahn-hee. The South Korean air force attempts to fight the monster in Seoul, but the creature is unstoppable and destroys much of the city.

During the rampage, the monster encounters a homeless boy named Squirrel, who had been looting Ahn-hee's home earlier. The boy attempts to fight the monster by climbing onto it and stabbing its inner-ear, ultimately falling and being captured alongside Ahn-hee.

The air force changes tactics and attempts to lead the monster away from the city. During the battle, the monster destroys Oh Jeong-hwan's jet fighter, but he ejects safely and manages to rescue both Ahn-hee and Squirrel. One of the jet fighters crashes into the monster, and the aliens ultimately decide that humans are too dangerous to conquer. They kill their own monster by detonating an explosive device they had affixed to it.

Much of the film is composed of comedic vignettes that take place during the monster's rampage, including sketches about gamblers betting on bravery, a man who needs to defecate during the chaos, and various other humorous interludes that have little impact on the main story.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Space Monster Wangmagwi carries subtle but significant political commentary despite its genre trappings. Film scholars Sueng-hoon Jeong and John Goodrich note that the film contains many allusions to the Korean War, even though the monster attacks from alien forces rather than any human aggressor. The monster appears to arrive in Korea near the Joint Security Area and proceeds south, mimicking the trajectory of a North Korean invasion. The separation of Ahn-hee from her family may symbolize the division of North and South Korea, while American involvement on behalf of South Korea is implied through the air force's jet fighters. The monster's spray of a napalm-like liquid from its head evokes the bombing campaigns conducted during the Korean War.

The film also presents a vision of modernizing Korean society. Ahn-hee, depicted in Western-style clothing with a modern attitude, represents the emergence of a new nuclear family model. Her and Oh Jeong-hwan's decision to adopt the orphaned Squirrel marks a significant shift from traditional Korean values where blood-relation was historically crucial.

As a piece of cinema history, Space Monster Wangmagwi represents a pivotal moment in Korean film. It is the oldest surviving Korean kaiju film and the first entirely Korean-produced science fiction film, demonstrating an early attempt to engage with international genre conventions while addressing distinctly Korean concerns. The film remained extremely rare until 2022, with the Korean Film Archive holding the only copies and occasionally screening it before finally making it available digitally and granting North American distribution rights.
