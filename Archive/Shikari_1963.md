# Shikari (1963)

**Medium:** Film
**Raw Score:** 3.0/5
**Total Ratings:** 80
**Creator/Author:** Mohammed Hussain (Director)

## The Pitch

Shikari is a 1963 Indian Hindi-language science fiction adventure film loosely based on Hollywood films Dr. Cyclops and King Kong. When a struggling circus owner and his partner embark on an expedition to the jungles of the Malay Peninsula to capture a legendary giant ape known as Otango, they encounter something far more dangerous: a mad scientist living in the jungle who is conducting horrifying experiments to turn humans into gorillas. The film combines adventure, horror, and sci-fi elements in a uniquely Indian package, featuring popular music and the iconic villain performance by K.N. Singh as Dr. Cyclops.

## The Truth

Kapoor and Jagdish are partners in a circus that is running at a loss. Desperate to save their business, they organize an expedition to the jungles of Malay to capture a giant ape known as Otango, believed to be worth a fortune. Accompanying them are Professor Sharma, his daughter Rita, and Chandu the circus clown.

During the expedition, the group encounters a devastating scene—a village destroyed by Otango. They soon discover a jungle laboratory run by the sinister Dr. Cyclops, a mad scientist living with his daughter Shobha and his henchmen. Dr. Cyclops has been experimenting on humans, attempting to turn them into gorillas using his chemical serums.

Dr. Cyclops schemes to marry Rita and forces Jagdish to help him. When Dr. Cyclops takes Rita to a native village for the wedding, Shobha helps the others escape through a secret passage. A chaotic fight ensues, during which Shobha is killed. Otango then attacks the village, causing widespread destruction. The heroes flee across a trestle bridge over burning lava and manage to cut the ropes, causing Otango to fall to its death in the gorge below.

## The Core

Shikari represents an interesting case of cross-cultural influence in 1960s Indian cinema, where Hollywood B-movie tropes were adapted for local audiences. The film borrows heavily from the "mad scientist" archetype seen in films like Dr. Cyclops (1940) and combines it with the giant ape spectacle of King Kong, creating a unique Indian adventure that incorporates sci-fi elements into traditional formulaic storytelling.

The character of Dr. Cyclops serves as a warni about scientific experimentation without ethical boundaries—a theme that would become more prominent in later science fiction. His laboratory experiments to transform humans into animals reflects early Cold War anxieties about science running amok, filtered through Indian cinematic conventions.

The film is notable for its villain performance by K.N. Singh, who plays Dr. Cyclops with memorable menace, and for its ambitious-for-the-time jungle sequences and special effects. While not as celebrated as later Indian sci-fi like Mr. India or Koi... Mil Gaya, Shikari stands as an early example of Indian filmmakers engaging with international science fiction genres and tropes.
