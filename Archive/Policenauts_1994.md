# Policenauts (1994)

**Medium:** Video Game

**Total Ratings:** 180
**Creator/Author:** Konami / Hideo Kojima

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Policenauts is a graphic adventure game developed and published by Konami, written and directed by Hideo Kojima. Originally released for the PC-9821 in Japan in 1994, it later came to 3DO, PlayStation, and Sega Saturn. Set in the mid-21st century, it follows Jonathan Ingram, an astronaut who was presumed dead after an accident at a space colony sent him drifting into space for 25 years in cryosleep. Recovered and revived, Ingram returns to Earth only to find his ex-wife has remarried—and then is murdered. Now a private investigator in Los Angeles, he travels to the space colony "Beyond Coast" to investigate her murder, uncovering a dark conspiracy involving illegal organ trafficking. The game is notable for its cinematic production values, blending anime-style cel animation with a narrative that combines hard science fiction with buddy-cop and noir detective tropes. Kojima conceived the game during development of Snatcher (1988) and wanted to explore how living in space might affect human society—medically, socially, and politically.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

In 2013, Jonathan Ingram was one of five "Policenauts"—police officers who received astronaut training to protect Beyond Coast, humanity's first fully self-supporting space colony. During a routine space suit test, an accident sends Jonathan drifting into space, where he remains in cryogenic survival for 25 years before being recovered and revived in 2040.

Upon returning to Earth, Jonathan discovers that his ex-wife Lorraine Hojo has remarried to Kenzo Hojo, a researcher at Tokugawa Pharmaceuticals. Lorraine visits Jonathan, asking for help solving her husband's disappearance—the only clues being a torn leaf, a set of capsules, and the word "Plato." Before Jonathan can fully investigate, Lorraine is murdered by a man in a black motorcycle suit. Driven to find her killer, Jonathan travels to Beyond Coast to continue the investigation.

On the space colony, Jonathan is reunited with his former LAPD partner Ed Brown, now chief of the Beyond Coast Police Department (BCPD) Vice Division. Together with Vice Unit members Meryl Silverburgh and Dave Forrest, Jonathan investigates the disappearance. They discover that Kenzo Hojo worked at Tokugawa Pharmaceuticals, which is researching solutions to health problems caused by living in space.

As the investigation deepens, Jonathan encounters former colleagues Gates Becker and Joseph Tokugawa—the latter suspected of involvement. A informant is killed and used to lure Jonathan and Ed into a trap. Eventually, Hojo's body is found, and Jonathan is framed for the killings. The truth emerges: Becker and Tokugawa have been running an illegal organ trafficking ring, exploiting the medical vulnerabilities of space colonists. The drugs they develop counteract the negative effects of long-term space habitation, but they need a constant supply of organs from donors—some willing, some not.

Kenzo Hojo became involved in the conspiracy to save his daughter Karen from terminal bone cancer, but eventually tried to break away and was murdered. With help from Ed and Meryl, Jonathan storms Tokugawa headquarters, confronts the assassin, and tricks Becker into revealing the entire scheme through a live video feed—exposing the scandal to all of Beyond Coast. Ed kills Becker, while Meryl and the police arrest Tokugawa. In the end, Jonathan discovers he is Karen's biological father and donates his bone marrow to save her.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Hard Science Fiction and Realism:** Policenauts represents Kojima's attempt at hard science fiction—a genre rarely explored in video games at the time. The game grounding its speculative elements in real scientific discourse about the effects of space travel on the human body, drawing from NASA research and contemporary debates about extended space habitation. By setting the story in a near-future that could realistically occur, Kojima distinguishes Policenauts from more fantastical science fiction.

**Organ Transplantation and Bioethics:** The game's central plot revolves around organ trafficking, reflecting real-world debates about organ transplantation, brain death, and bioethics that were particularly prominent in Japan in the early 1990s. The narrative explores how medical necessity can become a rationale for criminality, and how the desperation of the ill can be exploited by those in power.

**Isolation and Identity:** Jonathan Ingram's 25-year isolation in cryosleep creates a profound meditation on time, loss, and identity. He returns to a world that has moved on without him—his wife has remarried, his career has ended, his entire life has been rearranged. The space colony setting amplifies this theme: colonists live in isolation from Earth, developing their own society and culture. The game explores how isolation transforms not just individuals but entire civilizations.

**Noir and Buddy-Cop Fusion:** Policenauts blends hardboiled detective fiction with science fiction, creating a unique tonal hybrid. The buddy-cop dynamic between Jonathan and Ed mirrors American television shows like L.A. Law and Hill Street Blues, while the conspiracy plot and moral ambiguity draw from noir traditions. This genre fusion would become a Kojima trademark.

**Anti-Japanese Sentiment and Cultural Identity:** Kojima explicitly cited the 1993 film Rising Sun and the anti-Japanese sentiment it sparked in America as an influence on Policenauts. The game addresses questions of Japanese identity in an international context, with the space colony representing a new beginning free from Earth's national tensions—yet still plagued by human greed and corruption.

Policenauts remains significant as one of the earliest examples of narrative-driven adventure games tackling hard science fiction themes, predating the widespread adoption of such approaches in the gaming industry. Its themes of medical ethics, isolation, and identity resonate with contemporary debates about biotechnology and space exploration.
