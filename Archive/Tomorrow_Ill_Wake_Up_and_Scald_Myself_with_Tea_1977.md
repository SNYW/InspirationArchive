## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Tomorrow I'll Wake Up and Scald Myself with Tea is a 1977 Czechoslovak science fiction comedy that blends time travel paradoxes with absurd humor and political satire. Directed by Jindřich Polák (who also made the acclaimed Ikarie XB-1), the film is based on a short story by Josef Nesvadba. Set in a near-future Czechoslovakia where commercial time travel has become reality, the story follows identical twins Karel and Jan Bureš—one a corrupt pilot who works for Nazis planning to change history, the other an ordinary man thrust into an extraordinary situation when his twin dies unexpectedly. The film is notable for its uniquely Czech blend of surreal comedy, social commentary on totalitarianism (both historical and contemporary), and its surprisingly thoughtful handling of time travel logic. With production design that captures a wonderfully kitschy late-1970s Eastern Bloc vision of the future, this film has developed a cult following among fans of obscure European science fiction.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

In the near future, time travel technology has been developed and commercialized in Czechoslovakia, allowing citizens to travel back to witness historical events. The system is run by a company called Time, with departures announced in period-appropriate costumes. Anti-aging pills have also been developed, allowing users to remain youthful indefinitely.

A group of aging Nazis, led by Klaus Abard (Jiří Sovák), have survived through these anti-aging treatments. They plot to travel back in time and supply Adolf Hitler with a hydrogen bomb to help Germany win World War II. They bribe Karel Bureš (Petr Kostka), the pilot of their time machine, to assist them in their scheme.

On the day of the scheduled mission, Karel chokes to death on a breadroll (kifli) in his apartment and dies. His identical twin brother Jan (also played by Petr Kostka), a designer of the rocket-ship time machine, impulsively decides to assume Karel's identity to protect Karel's fiancée Eva from the truth. However, Jan is soon mistaken for Karel by the Nazis, who pressure him to continue with their plot.

Jan finds himself drawn into the Nazis' scheme, piloting the time machine back to Hitler's bunker in 1945. The film reaches its comedic climax in an extended sequence set in Hitler's bunker, where Jan triggers several time paradoxes while attempting to prevent the Nazis' success. Through a series of mishaps involving green-faced time travelers, suitcase switcheroos, and tourist groups visiting historical events, Jan ultimately manages to defeat the Nazis and resolve the consequences of his twin's death.

The film ends with Jan returning to his own time, having saved history while also coming to terms with his brother's death and his unexpected role as a hero.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Time Travel as Political Commentary:** The film uses time travel as a vehicle for exploring Czechoslovakia's complicated relationship with its World War II history and the ongoing presence of former Nazis in society. The fact that the villains are literally Nazi time travelers attempting to rewrite history adds a layer of dark comedy to the film's anti-fascist message.

**Satire of Totalitarianism:** Beyond the Nazi plot, the film satirizes the bureaucratic nature of communist Czechoslovakia. The commercialized time travel industry, the period-costumed announcers, and the mundane way citizens treat historical atrocities as tourist attractions all comment on how societies process difficult histories through commodification and routine.

**Identity and Duality:** The twin premise allows the film to explore questions of identity—what makes someone who they are? Jan must impersonate his brother, navigating both the Nazis' scheme and his own relationship with Eva, raising questions about the performative nature of identity.

**Eastern Bloc Futourism:** The production design presents a uniquely Czechoslovak vision of the future, mixing retro-futuristic technology with period fashion and communist-era aesthetics. This kitschy vision contrasts sharply with both Western sci-fi and Soviet realism, creating a distinctive visual style that has become part of the film's cult appeal.

**Comedy as Resistance:** The film's absurdist humor—particularly the green-faced time travelers, the tourist groups, and the comedy Hitler portrayal—represents a form of cultural resistance. By making fun of totalitarianism (both Nazi and communist), the film embodies the Czech tradition of humor as survival mechanism under oppressive regimes.

The film remains a cult classic precisely because it manages to be simultaneously a silly time-travel comedy, a serious political statement, and a uniquely Czech artifact of late-1970s cinema—demonstrating that Eastern Bloc science fiction could be as inventive and satirical as its Western counterparts.
