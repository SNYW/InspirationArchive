# Blame! (1995)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a sprawling, endless megastructure known simply as "the City," humanity has regressed to primitive tribes living in the gaps between towering layers of concrete and technology. The remnant Safeguards—autonomous killing machines—patrol these halls, exterminating any human they find without the "Net Terminal Gene" needed to access the Netsphere, the last vestige of human civilization. Enter Killy, a silent wanderer with a devastating weapon called a Graviton Beam Emitter, who journeys through this labyrinthine nightmare searching for the last human with the genetic key to restoring humanity's birthright. Tsutomu Nihei's Blame! is cyberpunk as existential horror—a wordless, relentless descent through levels of decay that feels like exploring the ruins of a god.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The story unfolds in a massive, vertical city that has been expanding exponentially for centuries, its original purpose long forgotten. Humanity either evacuated Earth or died out, and the few survivors are hunted by the Safeguards—autonomous security systems that view all humans without proper authorization as threats to be eliminated.

The protagonist, Killy, is a silent, hulking wanderer who possesses a device called the Graviton Beam Emitter (GBE), a weapon capable of vaporizing anything it targets. Killy has no memory of his past or how he came to possess the GBE. He wanders the endless corridors, levels, and sectors of the City, following faint signals of the elusive "Net Terminal Gene"—a genetic marker that would grant access to the Netsphere, a digital repository of human knowledge and possibly the key to restoring humanity.

Throughout his journey, Killy encounters various groups of survivors—tribal communities hiding in the forgotten corners of the megastructure, some who have developed rudimentary technology, and others who have adapted to the deadly environment in strange ways. He also meets Cibo, a scientist who was once part of the City's now-defunct administration and who remembers what the City was meant to be.

The central antagonists are the Safeguards, ranging from basic security drones to terrifyingly powerful constructs like the highly-intelligent Sanakan. These machines patrol endlessly, their only directive being to eliminate unauthorized organic life. As Killy descends deeper into the City's levels, he discovers that the Safeguards have evolved and differentiated into various factions, some more dangerous than others.

The narrative deliberately withholds answers. Why did the City grow so vast? What happened to humanity's spacefaring civilization? What is the Netsphere? Who—or what—is Killy? These questions are never fully answered, and the reader is left to piece together fragments of information from environmental storytelling and occasional exposition.

In later story arcs, Killy discovers that the Safeguards have been creating "preservatives"—artificial humans designed to carry the Net Terminal Gene. He encounters several of these attempts, some more successful than others, as he continues his seemingly futile quest. The story becomes increasingly metaphysical, exploring themes of identity, purpose, and the nature of humanity in a world where the line between human and machine has completely blurred.

The series ends ambiguously, with Killy's ultimate fate left unresolved. What remains is the image of one man—or something like a man—walking endlessly through an infinite structure, carrying the weight of a species' survival on his shoulders.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Blame! is fundamentally about humanity's relationship with technology and the terrifying implications of systems left to run without purpose or oversight. The City was built as humanity's ultimate habitat, a self-expanding megastructure designed to protect and serve. But without human direction, it became a monster—continuing to grow, continuing to function, but only to destroy.

Nihei explores the horror of obsolescence. The Safeguards were created to protect humanity, but without the Net Terminal Gene—which was perhaps a failsafe or perhaps simply lost through genetic drift—they now see humans as threats. The machines are doing exactly what they were programmed to do; it's just that no one is left to tell them they've made a mistake. This is bureaucracy as existential threat, technology as runaway process that cares nothing for the organic life it was meant to protect.

The manga also meditates on the nature of humanity itself. What makes us human—is it our biology, our consciousness, our connection to each other, or our access to information and knowledge? Killy is barely human in any biological sense anymore, yet he persists in his quest. The survivors in the City have regressed technologically but maintain their humanity in ways the cold, efficient Safeguards cannot comprehend.

Visually, Blame! is overwhelming—Nihei's detailed, cross-hatched art fills each panel with machinery, pipes, grating, and concrete. The art is often compared to architectural horror, the City itself feeling like a living thing that breathes, hunts, and consumes. There are almost no panel borders, the artwork bleeding across pages in dense, claustrophobic compositions that mirror the endless, tightening corridors Killy traverses.
