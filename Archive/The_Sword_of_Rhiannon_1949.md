# The Sword of Rhiannon (1949)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

The Sword of Rhiannon is a science fantasy masterpiece set on a dying Mars, blending adventure with profound themes of sacrifice and redemption. The story follows Matthew Carse, a 35-year-old former archaeologist-turned-thief in the Martian city of Jekkara, who is coerced into helping recover the legendary Sword of Rhiannon from an ancient Martian tomb. What begins as a simple heist takes an extraordinary turn when Carse is thrust millions of years back in time to the lush, ocean-covered Mars of the Sea-Kings—a civilization locked in epic conflict. Brackett weaves a tale of cosmic scope, featuring noble warriors, mysterious sorcery, and the ghostly presence of Rhiannon himself, the legendary figure who defied the gods of Mars to share advanced knowledge with lesser beings. Rich Horton reviewed it as "really wonderful pulp Sword and Sorcery, pitch perfect, beautifully written, twistily plotted."

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The protagonist is Matthew Carse, a cynical and worldly thief operating in the decaying Martian city of Jekkara. Once a promising archaeologist, Carse turned to theft after his academic career collapsed. He is approached by the sinister Penkawr, who attempts to coerce Carse into helping sell the Sword of Rhiannon—a relic stolen from a Martian tomb. Rhiannon was a legendary figure from the ancient Martian race of the Quiru who made the Promethean decision to share Quiru science with another race of Martians, the Dhuvians. Like Prometheus, his actions did not go unpunished: he was sealed in a tomb and became known to history as "The Cursed One." The Quiru left Mars to the Dhuvians and other sentient races soon afterward.

Carse accompanies Penkawr into the tomb to loot it but is transfixed by a mysterious sphere he finds within. Penkawr, seeing an opportunity to get rid of Carse, pushes him into the sphere, sending him back millions of years to the lush Mars of the Sea-Kings—an ancient epoch when Mars was covered in oceans and ruled by powerful kingdoms. The Sea-Kings are engaged in war with the Empire of Sark, which is allied with the mystical Dhuvians, and both sides wish to obtain the artifacts in Rhiannon's tomb. Carse, of course, is the only person who knows the location of the tomb, though he is uneasy about the responsibility. As he journeys back through time, he feels that another presence has joined him.

Carse is taken captive by Lady Ywain of Sark, a fierce and beautiful warrior noblewoman, and becomes a galley slave on her ship. Also serving as a galley slave is Boghaz, a fat, lazy criminal and merchant who initially seems more than happy to sell Carse out to save his own skin. However, Ywain realizes that Carse possessed the Sword of Rhiannon and they attempt to extract the location of the tomb from his mind. Carse resists, with the aid of the mysterious presence that has been growing stronger within him, and he leads the galley slaves in a dramatic mutiny.

Arriving at the realm of the Sea-Kings, the noblewoman Emer senses the presence in Carse, recognizing it as something of immense power and significance. Carse offers to reveal the location of the tomb, but with conditions. The presence in Carse's mind then reveals itself to be Rhiannon himself—a ghost or imprint seeking atonement for the crimes he committed eons before. Rhiannon had made the fatal mistake of sharing forbidden knowledge, creating imbalances that led to centuries of warfare and suffering. Now, in Carse's time, Rhiannon seeks redemption.

The narrative reaches its climax in the last, desperate struggle to save the planet. Rhiannon's plan unfolds: to undo the damage he caused by using the artifacts in his tomb to fundamentally change the nature of reality itself. The resolution is deeply romantic, with a shadow of true sadness—Carse must make profound sacrifices, and the world he knew will never be the same.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

The Sword of Rhiannon is a meditation on the dangers and moral complexities of knowledge-sharing and technological progress. Rhiannon functions as a Promethean figure—someone who defied the natural order to share fire (in this case, advanced science) with humanity, only to face eternal punishment. Brackett explores the question of whether knowledge should be hoarded by elites or shared freely, and the catastrophic consequences that can follow when powerful tools fall into unprepared hands.

The novel also deals heavily with themes of redemption and sacrifice. Rhiannon, cursed and sealed away for his transgression, spends eternity seeking a way to undo the damage his well-intentioned actions caused. His possession of Carse represents both a burden and a chance at salvation—a chance to finally set things right. The protagonist Carse, initially a self-interested thief, is transformed through his encounter with Rhiannon and the ancient world, becoming someone capable of noble sacrifice.

Brackett's Mars is not merely a setting but a character itself—the dying world of the far future contrasts sharply with the vibrant, ocean-covered Mars of the past. This temporal duality allows Brackett to explore themes of decay, nostalgia, and the cyclical nature of civilizations. The Sea-Kings of ancient Mars represent a romantic, heroic age that has been lost to time, while Jekkara in the far future represents the sad remnants of a once-great civilization.

The novel is also notable for its strong characterization of women—Lady Ywain and Emer are complex, capable figures who drive the plot forward rather than simply serving as love interests or damsels in distress. Brackett was ahead of her time in this regard, crafting female characters who are intelligent, powerful, and morally ambiguous.

Ultimately, The Sword of Rhiannon asks whether the sins of the past can ever truly be forgiven, and whether one individual can make a difference in the face of cosmic forces. It's a story about the weight of history, the possibility of redemption, and the enduring power of sacrifice.
