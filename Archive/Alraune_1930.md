## The Pitch

Alraune is a 1930 German science fiction horror film directed by Richard Oswald, based on the controversial novel by Hanns Heinz Ewers. The film stars Brigitte Helm reprising her role from the 1928 version, playing a woman created through a bizarre scientific experiment—a prostitute impregnated with the semen of a hanged murderer. This "unnatural" conception results in a daughter, Alraune, who lacks any concept of love and possesses obsessive sexuality and perverse relationships throughout her life.

The film aims for greater realism than its predecessor while remaining faithful to the original German myth. It represents an early example of speculative fiction exploring the ethics of artificial life and heredity experimentation, predating modern discussions of cloning by decades. The story follows Professor Jakob ten Brinken as he raises his creation and witnesses her destructive nature when she learns of her origins.

## The Truth

The film centers on Professor Jakob ten Brinken, a scientist obsessed with the laws of heredity, who conducts a radical experiment in his laboratory. He impregnates a prostitute with the semen of a hanged murderer, hoping to study the results of such an unnatural conception. The woman gives birth to a daughter whom the professor adopts and names Alraune.

From childhood, Alraune displays an alarming lack of moral compass and emotional connection—she has no concept of love and is driven by purely instinctual desires. As she grows into adulthood, she becomes involved in a series of destructive relationships, using her beauty to manipulate and destroy those around her.

When Alraune eventually discovers the truth of her unnatural origins—that she was conceived from a murderer and a prostitute—she turns her rage against her adoptive father. The film concludes with Alraune seeking revenge against Professor ten Brinken for creating her as a monster without love or conscience.

## The Core

**Heredity and Nature vs. Nurture:** The film directly engages with early 20th-century anxieties about genetics and inherited traits. Ten Brinken's experiment tests whether criminality and morality are inherited—his creation proves that without love or moral guidance, human nature can become monstrous. This echoes contemporaneous debates about eugenics and the responsibilities of parenthood.

**The Uncanny and the Artificial:** Alraune represents the "artificial human" archetype that would recur throughout science fiction—beings created outside natural reproduction. The film's horror stems from the creature's lack of soul or conscience, suggesting that human consciousness requires more than biological material.

**Gender and Monstrosity:** Brigitte Helm's performance portrays Alraune as both alluring and terrifying—a woman whose sexuality is weaponized because she lacks emotional connection. This reflects period anxieties about female sexuality and independence.

**Scientific Hubris:** The film warns against unchecked scientific ambition, presenting Ten Brinken as a scientist whose curiosity leads to tragedy. His creation destroys not only herself but everyone around her, including her creator.

Alraune remains significant as an early example of German Expressionist science fiction, exploring themes of artificial life and genetic experimentation that resonate throughout science fiction history.
