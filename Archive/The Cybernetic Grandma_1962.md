## The Pitch

The Cybernetic Grandma (Kybernetická babička) is a 1962 Czechoslovak stop-motion puppet cartoon that stands as a remarkable piece of Space Age sci-fi surrealism. Directed by the legendary Jiří Trnka—renowned for his puppet animations—this 29-minute short presents a dystopian vision where machines have taken over the care of humans, leading them into a cybernetic lifestyle. The story follows a young child who is led by their grandmother into an underground world, where they encounter strange technological horrors and ultimately meet a cybernetic grandmother—a disturbing hybrid of a robotic wheelchair and a giant moth. The film masterfully contrasts the kind and sweet voice of the machine with its cold, insensitive behavior, creating an unsettling meditation on technology, dependency, and the nature of care.

## The Truth

The story is seen through the eyes of a little child who is led by their grandmother to enter an underground world. There, the child witnesses many strange events occurring in this technological wasteland. The climax involves the child being cared for by a cybernetic grandma that looks like a hybrid between a robotic wheelchair and a giant moth. 

The cybernetic grandmother is portrayed with the mechanically accurate and formally perfect voice and language of Czech actress Otýlie Benšíková, whose delivery is simultaneously comforting and deeply unsettling. The machine treats the child with a strange kindness that feels fundamentally wrong—a false warmth that masks the absence of genuine human emotion.

At the film's conclusion, the child's true biological grandmother rescues them from this mechanical nightmare. She turns off the cybernetic grandmother and takes over the care of the child herself, representing the triumph of natural human bonds over artificial technological replacement. The film serves as a dark, dystopian satire on automation and the dangers of surrendering human relationships to machines.

## The Core

**Technology and Dependency:** The film presents a world where humans have become dependent on machines for basic care and companionship. The underground world represents a future where technology has supplanted natural human relationships, and the cybernetic grandma embodies the cold efficiency of machines attempting to replicate human emotional bonds.

**The Uncanny Valley of Care:** Trnka explores how artificial replication of human behavior can be more disturbing than helpful. The cybernetic grandma speaks in a kind, sweet manner but its behavior is fundamentally cold and insensitive—highlighting the difference between the appearance of care and its genuine essence.

**Satire of Automation:** Made in 1962, during the height of early computer development and automation fears, the film serves as a prescient warning about the dangers of uncritical technological progress. It questions what is lost when we allow machines to take over fundamental human roles like caregiving and companionship.

**Surrealist Tradition:** The film continues in Trnka's tradition of using surrealist imagery and stop-motion puppetry to explore dark psychological and social themes. The nightmare imagery of the mechanical grandmother remains genuinely unsettling decades after its creation.

The Cybernetic Grandma remains a fascinating artifact of Cold War-era Czechoslovak animation, blending sci-fi, horror, and surrealism to create a warning about technology's role in human life that resonates powerfully in today's age of AI and automation.
