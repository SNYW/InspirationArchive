# Domu: A Child's Dream (1980)

**Tags:** 🌍 Japan | 🎭 Pessimistic | 📚 Horror, Science-Fantasy | 🧬 Sentience, Isolation

**Medium:** Manga
**Raw Score:** 3.92/5
**Total Ratings:** 393
**Creator/Author:** Katsuhiro Otomo

## The Pitch

Domu is a psychological horror manga by Katsuhiro Otomo, creator of Akira, set in a sprawling Tokyo public housing complex called the Tsutsumi Housing Complex. When a series of mysterious suicides and violent deaths rocks the apartment block, two detectives investigate and discover a terrifying truth: an elderly man named Old Cho, a senile resident with powerful psychic abilities, is responsible. As the death toll rises, a young girl named Etsuko moves into the complex—and it becomes clear she is the only one with powers strong enough to stop Old Cho. First serialized in 1980-1981, Domu was the first manga to win the prestigious Nihon SF Taisho Award (Japan Science Fiction Grand Prix) and won the Seiun Award for Best Comic. It predates Otomo's more famous Akira and showcases his talent for blending urban horror with themes of generational conflict and psychic warfare.

## The Truth

The story begins with Mr. Ueno, a tenant at the Tsutsumi Housing Complex, jumping to his death from a building roof—the latest in a string of thirty-two mysterious deaths over just three years. Inspectors Yamagawa and Takayama investigate, initially dismissed as suicides, but soon discover something far more sinister. The case takes an even darker turn when a patrolman at the complex suddenly jumps to his death, this time with his service pistol missing from his belt.

As Yamagawa delves deeper, he interviews the complex manager and learns about several troubled residents: Mrs. Tezuka, sent to a mental hospital after a miscarriage; Little Yo, a mentally disabled man falsely accused of child molestation; and Old Cho, a senile old man living alone. When Yamagawa returns to the complex to investigate further, he experiences a terrifying encounter—his pager explodes, and he is pursued by an unseen force with psychic powers. He is found dead on the ground below the building.

The arrival of young Etsuko and her family changes everything. When Old Cho attempts to drop a baby from a balcony, Etsuko instinctively uses her own powerful psychic abilities to save the child. She realizes that Old Cho is behind all the deaths—he has been using his telekinetic powers to manipulate victims into killing themselves and collecting trophies from each one. Old Cho, once a quiet old man, has become a dangerous psychic predator, using his powers to exact twisted justice on residents he deems "sinful."

A brutal game of cat and mouse ensues between Old Cho and Etsuko across the apartment complex. Old Cho possesses various victims, including a young man who slits his own throat in front of Etsuko, and an abusive alcoholic whom he arms with the dead patrolman's pistol. Etsuko must use all her courage and psychic strength to protect the other residents and ultimately confront Old Cho in a climactic psychic battle.

## The Core

**Psychic Powers and Generational Conflict:** At its heart, Domu explores the conflict between generations through the lens of psychic ability. Old Cho represents the bitter, resentful elderly—marginalized, forgotten, and seething with accumulated grudges against a society that has dismissed him. His psychic powers, awakened or intensified by his senility, become a weapon of revenge against a world he perceives as having wronged him. Etsuko, by contrast, represents innocent youth, her powers emerging naturally and used defensively to protect life rather than destroy it. The clash between them is not just physical but philosophical: what happens when the powerless gain extraordinary power?

**Urban Isolation and Alienation:** Otomo sets the horror within a massive anonymous apartment complex, a perfect metaphor for modern urban isolation. Residents live side by side yet remain strangers; the elderly especially are forgotten, their suffering unnoticed until violence erupts. The Tsutsumi Housing Complex becomes a character in itself—a labyrinthine setting of corridors and shared walls where privacy is nonexistent but genuine connection is rare. This mirrors Otomo's own inspiration: the apartment complex he lived in when first moving to Tokyo, and news reports of suicides at similar housing developments.

**Horror and the Mundane:** Domu stands out for grounding its supernatural horror in everyday details. The police investigations, welfare checks, neighbor gossip, and domestic scenes of children playing create a stark contrast with the psychic violence. The horror doesn't occur in some fantastical otherworld but in recognizable spaces—a rooftop, a courtyard, a child's bedroom. This approach, which Otomo would later refine in Akira, treats the extraordinary with the same observational detail as the mundane.

**Precursor to Akira:** Reading Domu immediately before Akira, one sees Otomo developing many themes that would define his later masterpiece: the apocalyptic potential of psychic powers, the tension between individual ability and collective responsibility, and above all, Tokyo as a site of both technological modernity and social decay. But where Akira would become a sprawling epic of generational and political warfare, Domu remains focused and intimate—a contained horror story that achieves its devastating effects through restraint and psychological precision.
