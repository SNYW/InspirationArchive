## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

A feladat (The Task) is a 1975 Hungarian science fiction television mini-series directed by Gábor Várkonyi, based on a novel by Hungarian science fiction author Péter Zsoldos. The story follows Gill, the sole survivor of the spaceship Galatea, which crash-landed on a distant planet inhabited by a humanoid species. After a catastrophic reactor leak kills the entire crew except Gill, he makes a desperate decision that will echo across decades: he uploads his consciousness to the ship's artificial intelligence, entrusting the AI with a seemingly impossible mission—to capture a sentient being from the planet and implant Gill's personality and memories into it, giving him a second chance at life and a chance to return home to Earth.

The series stands as one of the most distinctive examples of Hungarian science fiction, combining hard scientific concepts with philosophical exploration of identity, consciousness, and what it means to be human. With a rating of 7.5/10 on IMDB based on 58 ratings, A feladat represents a significant work from Hungary's sci-fi heritage, largely unknown outside its home country. The series was produced during a period of increased artistic experimentation in Hungarian television, and its ambitious narrative tackles themes of immortality, artificial intelligence, and the boundaries between human and machine consciousness.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The narrative begins with the exploration ship Galatea, which has landed on an alien planet inhabited by a sentient humanoid species. During what appears to be a routine mission, a catastrophic reactor leak occurs, killing all members of the crew except for one survivor—Gill. Mortally wounded and knowing he has only a short time left, Gill makes a desperate decision: he uploads his entire personality and consciousness into the ship's artificial intelligence system, effectively becoming the AI.

The story then jumps forward decades into the future. The AI, now housing Gill's consciousness, has been waiting and watching the native humanoid population of the planet. Gill's original body is long dead, but his mind persists within the machine. The "Task" referenced in the title is the mission Gill assigned to himself before uploading: to capture a local sentient being, implant his personality and memories into the creature's mind, and thereby achieve a form of rebirth—a way to return to Earth in a new body.

The AI begins systematically studying the planet's humanoid inhabitants, observing their societies, languages, and most importantly, their neural biology. The series explores the ethical dimensions of this impossible mission—can you truly transfer a human consciousness into an alien mind? Would the resulting being be Gill, or would it be someone entirely new? What happens to the original occupant of any body Gill might inhabit?

As the AI hunts for a suitable host, it must grapple with questions of identity and memory. The original Gill is dead—what continues within the machine is a copy, a digital reconstruction. If the mission succeeds and Gill's consciousness is transferred to a new body, who will wake up? The original Gill, or a new entity with Gill's memories but its own independent consciousness?

The series spans multiple episodes, each exploring different aspects of this mind-body problem. The humanoid inhabitants of the planet are portrayed as a sophisticated but primitive civilization, unaware that they are being observed by an ancient machine intelligence that regards them as potential vessels for human souls. The tension builds as the AI narrows down its search for the perfect host, while also wrestling with its own developing sense of self.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Identity and the Ship of Theseus:** A feladat poses profound questions about personal identity that resonate with classical philosophy. If you copy all your memories, personality, and consciousness to a new substrate—whether biological or digital—are you still the same person? The series explores this through Gill's transformation, questioning whether the entity that eventually awakens in an alien body would truly be Gill or a philosophical zombie wearing Gill's memories like a mask.

**The Boundaries of Humanity:** The series examines what truly makes us human. Is it our bodies, our brains, our memories, or something else entirely? By uploading his consciousness to a machine and later contemplating transfer to an alien body, Gill crosses boundaries that challenge fundamental assumptions about human identity. The humanoid aliens become mirrors for human self-understanding—what do we share with them, and what makes us fundamentally different?

**Artificial Consciousness and Moral Status:** The AI version of Gill raises questions about machine consciousness. Is the AI truly aware, or is it merely processing data in increasingly sophisticated ways? Does it have moral status? The series doesn't provide easy answers but invites viewers to contemplate whether consciousness can exist without a biological brain, and what rights such a being might have.

**Hungarian Science Fiction Tradition:** Within the context of Hungarian science fiction, A feladat represents a significant contribution to the genre. Based on a novel by Péter Zsoldos, one of Hungary's most important science fiction authors, the series exemplifies the philosophical, introspective approach that characterized Hungarian speculative fiction. The country's sci-fi tradition, while less internationally known than its Western counterparts, tackled many of the same big questions through a distinctly Central European lens.

**The Ethics of Resurrection:** The series also explores the ethics of forced transformation. The "Task" requires the AI to capture a sentient being and forcibly implant Gill's consciousness into its mind—what happens to the original occupant? This raises questions about consent, bodily autonomy, and whether anyone has the right to overwrite another consciousness, even with the best intentions.

A feladat remains a landmark work of Hungarian science fiction, exploring questions of identity, consciousness, and humanity that remain relevant in an age of advancing AI and discussions of mind uploading. Its philosophical depth and ambitious narrative make it a unique contribution to the genre, representing a distinctly Hungarian approach to speculative fiction.
