# Golem (1979)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Golem is a 1979 Polish science fiction film directed and co-written by Piotr Szulkin in his directorial debut. Inspired by the titular being in Jewish folklore as well as Gustav Meyrink's 1915 novel "The Golem," Szulkin transposes the ancient legend into a nightmarish 21st-century dystopia where the golem is reimagined as a genetic clone—a subject of government eugenics experiments designed to create a superior human species in the aftermath of nuclear war. The film presents a haunting, surreal vision of post-apocalyptic society where bureaucratic absurdity meets existential horror, creating what critics describe as "Kafkaesque claustrophobia meets surrealism in a sci-fi retelling."

Shot through with striking visual style—using green, yellow, and red color filters to create an otherworldly atmosphere—the film follows its protagonist through a labyrinthine apartment building and a series of surreal encounters that blur the line between reality and nightmare. Szulkin's debut established him as a distinctive voice in Polish cinema, later earning recognition alongside other Eastern Bloc science fiction works like Stalker and Andrei Tarkovsky's filmography.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens with our protagonist, Pernat, at a police station being interrogated about the murder of a doctor who lived in his apartment building. Confused about much of his past and with no evidence against him, the police release Pernat—but the mysterious circumstances of the murder set the tone for everything that follows.

When Pernat returns to his apartment building to collect his belongings, he is mistakenly given someone else's hat and coat by an unconcerned clerk—a small bureaucratic error that exemplifies the film's atmosphere of systemic indifference. The apartment building itself becomes a character: a shabby, claustrophobic structure where each floor houses eccentrics and outcasts, all of whom seem to exist on the margins of society.

The narrative unfolds through Pernat's encounters with his eccentric neighbors, each encounter more surreal than the last. These meetings are interrupted periodically by scenes of scientists in sterile laboratories discussing their failed eugenics project—the creation of "golems," genetically engineered humans meant to be superior to ordinary people. These scientific discussions reveal that Pernat himself is one of these clones, a being created through government experiments to continue the human species after nuclear devastation.

As Pernat navigates this nightmarish world, the line between human and monster becomes increasingly blurred. The film poses questions about identity, humanity, and what it means to be alive when one's existence is the product of scientific experimentation. The "golem" of the title is not a supernatural creature of clay brought to life through magic, but a modern Frankenstein—a human created through genetic manipulation, more human than the humans around him yet treated as an outsider.

One particularly memorable sequence involves Pernat being invited to a cinema by the father of a young woman he meets—what the father calls the "Church of Transfiguration." Inside, Pernat witnesses bizarre commercials advertising sleeping pills and plastic surgery, while the father dozes off and later removes his own face in the toilet. This surreal sequence exemplifies Szulkin's vision: a world where consumer culture, bureaucracy, and identity have become unmoored from any recognizable reality.

The film builds toward an enigmatic conclusion that leaves many questions unanswered—a hallmark of Szulkin's approach. The ending credits feature a sequence that may reference Citizen Kane, suggesting further layers of meaning for attentive viewers.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Modern Golem:** Szulkin reimagines the traditional Jewish golem legend for the atomic age. Where the original golem was a protective figure created through mystical means to defend a community, Szulkin's golem is a product of state power—a clone created by government scientists pursuing eugenics. This transformation reflects anxieties about technology, state control, and the potential for scientific advancement to become a tool of oppression rather than liberation.

**Identity and Humanity:** The central question of Golem is "what makes you human?" Pernat is revealed to be one of the failed subjects of the eugenics program—more human in his soul than the "normal" humans around him, yet treated as suspect and other. The film asks whether identity is determined by origin, by appearance, or by actions and compassion.

**Kafkaesque Bureaucracy:** The film draws heavily from Kafka's The Trial, presenting a world where bureaucratic systems operate with indifferent absurdity. The police interrogation that opens the film, the clerical error with the hat and coat, the scientists discussing their failed experiments in detached clinical terms—all create a sense of individuals trapped in systems they cannot understand or escape.

**Post-Apocalyptic Dystopia:** The nuclear war that provides the backstory for the eugenics program creates a world of scarcity, decay, and desperation. The film's visual style—dark orange hues, sepia tinting, color filters—evokes a world that has been damaged, contaminated, transformed into something other.

**Surrealism and Social Commentary:** Szulkin uses surrealist techniques to critique Polish society under communist rule. The absurdities of daily life, the faceless bureaucracy, the manipulation of citizens by the state—all are filtered through a dreamlike aesthetic that makes the commentary more potent precisely because it is indirect.

**Tarkovsky's Influence:** Critics have noted Szulkin's admiration for Andrei Tarkovsky in the film's visual approach—the contemplative pacing, the striking imagery, the philosophical depth. However, Szulkin adds his own distinct sensibility: dark humor, satirical edge, and a more overtly political critique than Tarkovsky's often mystical approach.

Golem remains a landmark of Polish science fiction cinema, representing a distinctive voice in the Eastern Bloc tradition of dystopian filmmaking. Its combination of surrealist visuals, philosophical questioning, and political commentary make it a rewarding watch for those interested in how science fiction can serve as a vehicle for profound cultural critique.
