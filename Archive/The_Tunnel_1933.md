# The Tunnel (1933)

## The Pitch

The Tunnel is a 1933 German science fiction film adapted from Bernhard Kellermann's 1913 novel, depicting the ambitious engineering project to construct a transatlantic tunnel connecting Europe and America. Directed by Curtis Bernhardt and starring Paul Hartmann, Attila Hörbiger, and Gustaf Gründgens, the film presents an early vision of global connectivity and the technological optimism of the early 20th century—before the shadow of World War II would darken such visions of progress.

The story follows Mac Allen, the chief engineer leading the massive undertaking to bore a tunnel beneath the Atlantic Ocean. Facing financial crises, political opposition, and the immense technical challenges of such an unprecedented project, Allen and his team press forward despite mounting obstacles. The film explores the tension between visionary ambition and the practical, political, and financial forces that threaten to derail great endeavors.

## The Truth

The narrative centers on the construction of the transatlantic tunnel, following the engineering team led by Mac Allen as they attempt to complete the monumental project. The tunnel syndicate, headed by Mr. Woolf (played by Gustaf Gründgens), provides the financial backing, but the project faces constant threats from financiers who question the returns, from the elements themselves, and from those who oppose such a radical transformation of the world order.

The film depicts the technical challenges of boring through the ocean floor, the political machinations surrounding funding and international agreements, and the personal dramas of the engineers and financiers involved. A separate French version was also produced, and in 1935 Britain would remake the film as "Transatlantic Tunnel," released in the U.S. under that same title.

The cast includes notable actors of the German cinema of the era, including Max Schreck in a supporting role, adding another connection to the Expressionist horror tradition that defined much of German filmmaking in the 1920s and early 1930s.

## The Core

The Tunnel represents a fascinating artifact of early 1930s German cinema, capturing the technological optimism of the era while also reflecting the anxieties of the period. The transatlantic tunnel serves as a metaphor for human ambition and the desire to connect distant peoples—themes that resonate particularly strongly in the context of 1930s Europe, where both the promise and peril of large-scale engineering projects were very much in evidence.

The film can be seen as part of a tradition of German speculative fiction that includes Metropolis (1927), exploring humanity's relationship with technology and large-scale projects. Like Metropolis, The Tunnel presents technology as both transformative and potentially dangerous, asking whether the benefits of grand engineering projects justify the costs—financial, human, and political.

The 1933 release date is significant: this was a transitional moment in German history, and the film's themes of ambitious projects, financial intrigue, and the struggle between visionaries and conservative forces take on added resonance in this context. The film's production at Bavaria Film and its subsequent international success (including the French version and British remake) also speak to the transnational nature of early science fiction cinema.

Today, The Tunnel remains an important early entry in the science fiction film genre, demonstrating that the genre's fascination with megaprojects and technological ambition was alive and well even in the early days of the form.
