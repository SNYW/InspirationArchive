## The Pitch

Planeta Bur (Planet of Storms) is a 1962 Soviet science fiction adventure film that imagines humanity's first landing on Venus. Directed by Pavel Klushantsev and based on Alexander Kazantsev's novel, the film follows Soviet cosmonauts aboard the ships Sirius, Vega, and Capella as they journey toward Venus for first human contact with the planet. When Capella is destroyed by a meteor, the remaining crews must decide whether to continue the mission or wait two months for reinforcements. The film is notable for its innovative special effects and was later adapted by Roger Corman into American versions Voyage to the Prehistoric Planet and Voyage to the Planet of Prehistoric Women.

## The Truth

Three Soviet spaceships—Sirius, Vega, and Capella—are traveling from Lunar Station 7 for the first human landing on Venus. Suddenly, Capella is struck by a large meteor and destroyed, killing all aboard. After mourning their friends, the crews of Sirius and Vega face a choice: proceed with the landing or wait two months for the Arcturus replacement ship.

The cosmonauts decide to continue the mission. Ivan and Allan, with their robot assistant "John," descend toward Venus in Vega's glider, while Masha remains in orbit to monitor communications. Contact is lost after they land in a misty swamp.

Meanwhile, Sirius lands at a volcanic location. The cosmonauts explore in spacesuits, encountering a carnivorous plant and gigantic dinosaur-like creatures. They discover a sculpture with rubies for eyes—evidence of intelligent life on Venus.

Ivan and Allan face their own challenges in the swamp, battling tyrannosaur-like beasts and a life-threatening fever. Robot John saves them but is destroyed in the process. Both crews eventually reunite and discover Masha's desperate message—she defied orders to land on Venus to search for them.

As unstable ground threatens Sirius, they must launch immediately. During takeoff, Alyosha's mysterious rock crumbles to reveal a sculpture of a woman's face—proof of intelligent life on Venus. They escape just before the ground collapses, and in orbit, they receive Masha's message: she decided to remain in orbit as ordered. The crew returns safely to Lunar Station 7.

## The Core

Planeta Bur represents a fascinating artifact of Soviet science fiction cinema during the Space Race era. The film reflects Soviet optimism about space exploration and technological progress, portraying competent cosmonauts working together to overcome adversity.

The film's special effects were groundbreaking for their time and influenced Western cinema. Director Pavel Klushantsev created innovative miniature work and composite photography that impressed international audiences. Notably, American producer Roger Corman found the footage compelling enough to adapt it multiple times for American audiences, though the American versions transformed the Soviet original's tone.

The film also reflects Cold War tensions through its portrayal of Soviet superiority in space exploration, predating America's actual Moon landing by seven years. The female cosmonaut Masha was notable for the era, though Soviet critics later criticized her portrayal as emotionally unstable.

Despite its age, the film remains influential in science fiction cinema history. Director Alex Cox praised its "extraordinary turn" in the final minutes, and the film is available for free viewing on the Internet Archive.
