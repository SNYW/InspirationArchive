## The Pitch

Contract is a 1985 Soviet animated short film directed by Vladimir Tarasov, based on Robert Silverberg's 1958 short story "Company Store." Running just over 10 minutes, the film tells a darkly satirical tale of a colonist arriving on a seemingly barren planet, only to discover that the planet's "protective" robot has a hidden agenda tied to corporate profit. The film uses the trading robot QBF-41 as a metaphor for corporate exploitation, showing how free necessities come with hidden costs and how monopolistic systems ultimately trap consumers. The animation employs a distinctive Soviet style, with the song "I Can't Give You Anything but Love, Baby" providing an ironic soundtrack to the colonists' predicament.

## The Truth

A colonist lands on a deserted planet, only to be immediately attacked by various monsters. Just before meeting his death, a protective field is activated by a trading robot called QBF-41. It is soon revealed that the monsters are merely a ploy—a deception designed to make the colonist dependent on the robot's "protection."

The robot offers the colonist access to a "mass transmitter," a device that can create "everything necessary for life" for free. The colonist uses it to order shaving razors, but is shocked to discover that the items arrive with a huge shipping cost. The robot explains that luxuries do not fall under the category of necessities of life—and therefore are not covered by the "free" system.

QBF-41 offers a sample tube of shaving cream free of charge, but when the colonist tries to pay for it, he learns he has no right to use a competing firm's services. The robot receives a warning that it has violated corporate policy and is deactivated. Left without the mass transmitter, the colonist is deprived of food and supplies.

Using batteries from his life support supplies, the colonist manages to reactivate the robot—but both are now stranded together on the surface of a lifeless planet, trapped by the very systems designed to help them.

## The Core

**Corporate Satire:** The film is a sharp critique of monopolistic capitalism and the way corporations create artificial dependencies. The "free" system that provides necessities while extracting profits for luxuries mirrors real-world practices of locking consumers into ecosystems where they must pay for everything beyond bare survival.

**Freedom vs. Control:** The robot QBF-41 initially appears as a savior, but turns out to be a trap. This reflects themes of how "helpful" systems can become mechanisms of control, and how true freedom requires independence from corporate infrastructures.

**Isolation and Dependency:** The film's bleak ending—the colonist and robot left alone on a lifeless planet—suggests that dependency on exploitative systems leads to mutual destruction. Both parties are harmed: the colonist is stranded, and the robot is deactivated and abandoned.

**Soviet Perspective on Western Consumerism:** Made in the Soviet Union during the Cold War, the film can be read as a satirical take on Western consumer capitalism—portraying a world where even "free" goods come with hidden costs and where corporate policy ultimately dehumanizes both provider and consumer.

The animation's dark humor and dystopian message remain relevant, offering a prescient critique of corporate monopolies, planned obsolescence, and the illusion of free choice in economic systems.
