## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

B.A.T. (Bureau of Astral Troubleshooters) is a futuristic point-and-click adventure game with role-playing elements, developed by Computer's Dream and published by Ubi Soft in 1989-1990. Set in the far-future galaxy of Selenia, players take on the role of an elite agent assigned to the Bureau of Astral Troubleshooters—a cosmic law enforcement agency tasked with maintaining peace across planetary colonies. The game combines exploration, puzzle-solving, and combat sequences as players navigate a branching narrative of interstellar intrigue.

What makes B.A.T. distinctive is its ambitious scope: a full galaxy of planets to explore, multiple story branches, and a blend of adventure game exploration with RPG statistics and combat. The Atari ST version notably shipped with a custom 16-channel sound dongle, representing cutting-edge audio technology for home computers at the time. While computer gaming magazines of the era praised its NPC interactions and visual presentation, they criticized its combat mechanics and user interface—making it a fascinating artifact of early cyberpunk gaming that never quite achieved mainstream success.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The player assumes the role of a newly recruited agent in the Bureau of Astral Troubleshooters, an interstellar police force operating across the galaxy's planetary colonies. The game opens with the agent arriving at the space station that serves as the game's hub, where they receive missions and interact with various NPCs.

The primary storyline involves investigating the Koshan Conspiracy—a shadowy organization threatening galactic stability. Players travel between different planets, each with its own distinct environment and inhabitants, gathering information, completing missions, and gradually uncovering the conspiracy's reach.

The gameplay alternates between several modes: exploration through point-and-click navigation of pre-rendered environments, conversation-based puzzle solving with NPCs, and arcade-style combat sequences. The RPG elements include character statistics that affect combat effectiveness and dialogue options, allowing players to approach missions through multiple strategies.

A notable feature is the game's branching narrative—player choices affect which missions they receive and how the story unfolds. The game received a sequel, B.A.T. II – The Koshan Conspiracy, released in 1992, which continued the story and expanded upon the original's concepts.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Interstellar Policing and Colonialism:** B.A.T. presents a vision of future galactic governance where a central bureau maintains order across diverse planetary colonies. This reflects Cold War-era anxieties about maintaining order in a complex, interconnected world—reimagined at a cosmic scale. The Bureau functions as both protector and controller, raising questions about the balance between security and freedom.

**Conspiracy and Institutional Corruption:** The Koshan Conspiracy represents a classic paranoid narrative—in which a seemingly benevolent organization harbors hidden malevolent intentions. This theme resonated with late 1980s audiences still processing real-world revelations about covert government operations and institutional betrayal.

**Technological Ambition:** The game's elaborate sound hardware and ambitious scope reflect an era of technological optimism in gaming, where developers pushed home computer hardware to its limits. The inclusion of a custom sound card demonstrates how early cyberpunk games sought to immerse players through technological spectacle.

**Genre Precursor:** While cyberpunk was primarily associated with tabletop RPGs and novels during this period, B.A.T. represents one of the earliest attempts to translate cyberpunk sensibilities into a video game format—a precursor to later classics like System Shock and Deus Ex that would explore similar themes of corporate conspiracy and body modification.
