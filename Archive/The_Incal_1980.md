# The Incal (1980)

**Medium:** Graphic Novel / Bande Dessinée
**Raw Score:** 4.04/5
**Total Ratings:** 1315
**Creator/Author:** Alejandro Jodorowsky (Writer), Jean Giraud/Mœbius (Original Artist), Zoran Janjetov (Before the Incal), José Ladrönn (Final Incal)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Set in a dystopian galactic empire on the insignificant planet Ter-21, The Incal follows John Difool, a Class R private investigator living in the depths of the great pit-city. When a dying Berg (featherless alien) hands him the Light Incal—a crystal of enormous and infinite powers—Difool becomes the most wanted being in the universe. Multiple factions seek to possess the Incal: the Berg aliens, the corrupt government, the rebel group Amok, and the sinister Techno-Technopriests who worship the Dark Incal.

What begins as a noir tale of urban corruption transforms into an epic space opera spanning galaxies. Joined by Animah (keeper of the Light Incal), the legendary Metabaron bounty hunter, and other unlikely allies, Difool must journey across worlds to prevent the Technopriests from launching the sun-eating Dark Egg that would consume the universe. The story is part of Jodorowsky's larger "Jodoverse" that includes the celebrated Metabarons saga.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The Incal unfolds in six original volumes, beginning in medias res with Difool being thrown from Suicide Alley into the acid lake below by masked attackers, only to be saved by a police cruiser. During questioning, he denies receiving the Light Incal from a dying Berg—but the crystal has already chosen him as its guardian.

The story unfolds across multiple plot threads:

**Volume 1-2 (The Black Incal, The Luminous Incal):** Difool is pursued by multiple factions seeking the Light Incal. He encounters Animah, the psychic keeper of the Light Incal, and learns that a powerful entity called the Dark Incal exists. The Technopriests, a technocratic cult, worship the Dark Incal and plan to destroy the universe with their sun-eating device.

**Volume 3-4 (What Lies Beneath, What Is Above):** The rebellion led by Tanatah (Animah's sister) enters the picture. The greatest bounty hunter in the universe—the Metabaron—is hired to kill Difool and retrieve the Incal. However, the Metabaron betrays Tanatah, knowing she would betray him. Difool and his companions—including the loyal concrete seagull Deepo who gains speech from the Incal—journey through increasingly strange worlds.

**Volume 5-6 (The Fifth Essence):** The full scope of the cosmic conflict emerges. The Technopriests launch the Dark Egg (a sun-eating weapon) into space. The universe faces annihilation. Difool encounters Orh, a fatherlike cosmic deity, who shows him the truth of existence. In the climax, the darkness is overcome, and Difool must remember what he witnessed—as the story closes with him falling again, mirroring the opening.

The prequel series "Before the Incal" (1988-1995) reveals Difool's origin as the son of a prostitute, his early career as a detective, and his first encounter with the mysterious powers of the universe.

The sequel "Final Incal" (2008-2014) shows a universe threatened by a metallic virus. The Prezident, cloned in a metallic body, unleashes the Biophage 13-X virus. Four different versions of John Difool from different realities are brought together to find love and save humanity from becoming unfeeling machines.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

The Incal operates on multiple symbolic levels, exploring the spiritual journey of an unwilling hero who must transform from a self-interested hedonist into someone capable of saving the universe.

**The Fool's Journey:** John Difool's name puns on "John, the Fool"—the zero card in the Tarot. His arc mirrors the Fool's journey through the Major Arcana: innocence, encounter with new worlds, trials, transformation, and enlightenment. The circular structure (beginning and ending with Difool falling) suggests an eternal return and the recursive nature of spiritual growth.

**Technology vs. Humanity:** The Technopriests represent technological dehumanization—the worship of dead machinery over living consciousness. The story argues that while technology can enable us ("glasses enable you to see"), it cannot provide meaning or happiness. The contrast between the organic Incal (living light) and the Dark Egg (mechanical destruction) embodies this theme.

**Class and Power:** The stratified society of Ter-21—a panopticon dystopia where the wealthy live above and the poor in the pits below—serves as a critique of class division. The mutants living in the garbage represent society's marginalized, while the techno-elites seek to control all existence.

**Dualism and Unity:** The Light/Dark Incal, the two galaxies (human and Berg), the conflict between Animah and Tanatah—everything in The Incal reflects binary opposition. Yet the resolution comes through integration and love, not victory of one side over the other. The Final Incal explicitly calls for collective consciousness and unity.

**Spiritual Transformation:** Jodorowsky's Buddhist and mystical influences permeate the work. The Incal is fundamentally about awakening—Difool must forget his ego, his fear, and his desire to return to ignorance. He must accept his role in the cosmic drama, transcending individual existence for the benefit of all consciousness.

The work's influence is vast—Blade Runner, The Matrix, and countless anime and films have borrowed from its visual and thematic vocabulary. Moebius's artwork, blending De Stijl minimalism with cosmic vision, remains some of the most beautiful in comic art history.
