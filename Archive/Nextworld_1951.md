## The Pitch

Nextworld is a Japanese science fiction manga series written and illustrated by Osamu Tezuka in 1951. It is the third and final work in Tezuka's early science fiction trilogy, following Lost World (1948) and Metropolis (1949). The story focuses on atomic tests that create a race of mutant animals known as Fumoon—intelligent beings with psychic powers who formulate a plan to evacuate hundreds of animals and a small group of people off planet Earth. Meanwhile, two superpowers (representing the USA and USSR) draw closer to nuclear confrontation. Created during the early Cold War, Nextworld serves as a satirical allegory for the tense geopolitical climate of the era.

## The Truth

The story is set in a near-future Earth where nuclear testing has led to unexpected consequences. Atomic tests conducted by the world's superpowers create a race of mutant animals called Fumoon—intelligent creatures with psychic powers that surpass human capabilities. These evolved animals possess advanced intelligence and begin planning for their own survival as a large toxic cloud approaches Earth, threatening to wipe out all life.

As the Fumoon develop their evacuation plans, the two rival superpowers—the "Nation of Stars" (representing the USA) and the "Uran Federation" (representing the USSR)—draw ever closer to nuclear confrontation. The narrative explores the absurdity of Cold War tensions through the lens of this alien emergence, showing how humanity's own conflicts threaten their survival while a new form of life emerges.

The Fumoon, led by their psychic abilities, must decide whether to save humanity or let the self-destructive civilization destroy itself. The story culminates in the Fumoon's decision to take only those humans they deem worthy of survival, while leaving behind the warring nations that have brought Earth to the brink of annihilation.

## The Core

**Cold War Allegory:** Nextworld is fundamentally a satirical commentary on the Cold War era. Tezuka uses the fictional superpowers to critique nuclear proliferation and the arms race, showing how humanity's own divisions and conflicts could lead to their replacement by a superior species.

**Evolution and Mutation:** The manga explores themes of accelerated evolution through radiation, presenting the Fumoon as the next step in Earth's biological development. This connects to mid-century anxieties about nuclear testing and its effects on living organisms.

**Parental Figures and Protection:** As with other Tezuka works, there are themes of protective figures guiding younger generations—whether the Fumoon protecting their species or the question of which humans deserve salvation.

**Science vs. Humanity:** The story questions whether scientific advancement without moral progress leads to self-destruction. The superpowers have developed powerful weapons but lack the wisdom to use them responsibly.

Nextworld remains significant as an early example of Japanese science fiction manga and as a period piece capturing Cold War anxieties through the lens of speculative evolution.
