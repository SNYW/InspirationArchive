# The Congress (2013)

**Medium:** Animated Film
**Raw Score:** 3.2/5
**Total Ratings:** 6000

**Tags:** 🌍 France | 🎭 Melancholic | 📚 Science-Fantasy | 🧬 Identity, Virtual Reality, Corporate Conspiracy, Memory

**Creator/Author:** Ari Folman (Director), based on Stanisław Lem's "The Futurological Congress"

## The Pitch

In a world where movie studios can digitize actors' likenesses for eternal use, aging actress Robin Wright agrees to let Miramount Studios create a digital clone of her—on one condition: she can never act again. Twenty years later, she travels to the "Futurological Congress," an animated utopia where anyone can become any avatar they imagine, but must use hallucinogenic drugs to enter. What begins as a surreal business trip becomes a journey through reality itself, questioning what it means to be human when identity can be copied, sold, and transformed at will. Part live-action, part breathtaking animation, The Congress is a visionary meditation on fame, mortality, and the price of escaping reality.

## The Truth

Robin Wright plays herself—a Hollywood actress whose career has faltered due to her reputation for being difficult. Her young son Aaron suffers from Usher syndrome, a degenerative condition that's slowly robbing him of his sight and hearing. Desperate for money to help her son, Robin meets with Jeff Green, CEO of Miramount Studios, who offers her an extraordinary deal: for a substantial sum, she'll allow the studio to scan her body and create a computer-animated version of herself that can star in any film they choose. The catch: she must sign away her likeness forever and promise never to act again. With few options, Robin agrees.

Twenty years pass. Robin travels to "Abrahama City" to speak at the Futurological Congress, Miramount's annual entertainment industry conference. What she finds is a fantastical animated world created from the collective imagination of humanity—anyone can transform into any avatar they desire, but they must take hallucinogenic drugs to enter this mutable realm. Robin discovers that in her absence, her digital persona has become the star of a successful film franchise called "Rebel Robot Robin," making her—alongside Tom Cruise—one of the last "real" movie stars.

During the Congress, Robin learns that Miramount has developed technology allowing anyone to transform into her likeness. Troubled by this, she publicly denounces the technology at the Congress, declaring that no one should be turned into a product. She's arrested and seemingly executed by Jeff as punishment for her rebellion.

But the execution was a hallucination. Robin's rescuers were actually Miramount security. Doctors determine she's become so intoxicated by the hallucinogens that she must be frozen until a treatment can be found.

Twenty years later, Robin is revived. She reunites with Dylan Truliner, Miramount's former lead animator who had worked on her films. He explains that the hallucinogenic technology has become ubiquitous—people can now become anyone or anything they wish, and as a result, many of humanity's negative aspects have vanished. The two fall in love and journey through spectacular animated worlds.

But Robin still wants to return to reality to be with her son. Dylan gives her a "soberty capsule"—the only way to exit the animated world—but it's essentially a suicide pill in this realm. Robin takes it and returns to the real world, only to find it devastated. Most people have abandoned reality for the animated realm. A tiny elite floats above ruined cities in airships. Aaron, now blind and deaf, entered the animated world six months earlier and has created a new identity there—impossible to find.

Dr. Barker gives Robin an inhalation ampoule that will let her return to the animated world, though her hallucinations will be different this time. She takes the drug and discovers Aaron in an animated desert—a final, transcendent reunion.

## The Core

The Congress is a profound exploration of identity in the digital age. Ari Folman transforms Stanisław Lem's 1971 allegory about communist dictatorship into a searing critique of the entertainment industry and digital technology. The film asks: when our bodies, faces, and voices can be replicated and owned by corporations, what remains of the self?

The live-action/animated hybrid structure brilliantly embodies the film's themes—the "real" world of the first act is cold, desperate, and financially desperate, while the animated realm is vibrant, limitless, and escapist. Robin's journey represents humanity's eternal tension between facing harsh reality and retreating into fantasy.

Particularly prescient is the film's treatment of AI and digital likeness rights—a theme that became urgent during the 2023 SAG-AFTRA strikes. The Congress predicted with eerie accuracy the Hollywood debates over whether studios should own actors' digital scans for perpetuity. The film's vision of people abandoning a ruined real world for customizable虚拟 realities feels increasingly relevant in our age of metaverse speculation and social media addiction.

The animation itself is breathtaking—six studios worldwide contributed to a style that shifts from psychedelic color to stark minimalism depending on the narrative needs. Max Richter's soundtrack, featuring Robin Wright's haunting renditions of "Forever Young" and Leonard Cohen's "If It Be Your Will," anchors the film in themes of mortality and transcendence.

The Congress is ultimately a melancholic meditation on what makes us human: is it our physical bodies, our memories, our relationships, or our capacity for choice? When Robin finally reunites with her son in the animated desert, the film suggests that love transcends the boundary between reality and simulation—but at the cost of acknowledging that the "real" world may not be worth saving.
