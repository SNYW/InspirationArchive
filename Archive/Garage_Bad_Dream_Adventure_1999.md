## The Pitch

Garage: Bad Dream Adventure is a Japanese surrealist point-and-click adventure game developed by Kinotrope and published by Toshiba-EMI in 1999. Set inside the mind of a man named Yang, the player enters a therapy machine called "Garage" that creates a dystopian world inside the user's psyche—visually inspired by the labyrinthine Kowloon Walled City. The player controls a small bio-machine tasked with finding its "shadow," a mysterious entity reflecting another facet of the character, while navigating a dark society of biomechanical beings trapped on a web of tracks. The game combines surreal visuals, psychological themes inspired by Carl Jung's theories, and resource management mechanics involving Ego and Fuel depletion. Only 3,000 copies were originally released in Japan, making it one of the rarest and most sought-after cult classic games.

## The Truth

The player begins inside the "Garage"—a therapy machine that projects the user's consciousness into a surreal internal landscape. This dystopian city resembles the densely packed Kowloon Walled City, but rendered as a nightmarish realm of biomechanical entities trapped on interconnected tracks, endlessly repeating their days in a dark, oppressive society.

Taking control of a small bio-machine—a creature with an organic head on a mechanical body—the player must explore this strange world to find their "shadow," an entity that seems to represent another facet of the player's own psyche. The journey involves meeting other machines inhabiting this world, solving surreal puzzles, fishing for frogs, and constantly managing two dwindling resources: Ego and Fuel.

As the player progresses, they uncover more about the nature of this world—it's literally the interior landscape of Yang's mind, processed through the Garage therapy machine. Each biomechanical character represents aspects of the human psyche, and the player's journey through the city is an attempt at psychological integration and escape.

The game's narrative is intentionally cryptic and dreamlike, with the story emerging through environmental storytelling, character interactions, and the player's own interpretation of the surreal imagery. The ending involves the bio-machine finally confronting and integrating with its shadow, allowing escape from the循环 of the mind-city.

## The Core

**Carl Jung and Psychological Symbolism:** The entire game is built on Jungian concepts—the shadow self, the collective unconscious, and psychological integration. The bio-machine's quest to find its shadow directly mirrors Jung's therapeutic process of shadow integration. The Garage itself functions as a Jungian therapy machine, projecting the psyche into a visual form where the ego must confront repressed aspects of self.

**The Biomechanical Uncanny:** All characters in the game are bio-machines—organic heads on mechanical bodies—a design choice that creates persistent unease. These beings are neither fully human nor fully machine, trapped in a liminal state between biological and technological existence. This anticipates themes of transhumanism and cyborg identity that would become central to later cyberpunk works.

**Isolation and Internal Landscape:** The game presents a powerful metaphor for mental isolation—Yang is literally trapped inside his own mind, and the player experiences this as a solitary journey through a city of strangers who cannot truly connect. The biomechanical entities are alone despite their numbers, each trapped on their own track, repeating the same cycles.

**Rarity and Cult Status:** With only 3,000 original copies and a original price reaching 300,000 yen on secondary markets, Garage represents extreme rarity in gaming. Its preservation by 4chan's /vr/ community and eventual English translation patch in 2020 brought this obscure work to Western audiences, demonstrating how internet communities can rescue lost media from obscurity.

**Surrealist Game Design:** Unlike most games that prioritize clarity and progression, Garage embraces confusion and atmosphere as design elements. The puzzles are often abstract, the logic dreamlike, and the visual experience prioritizes emotional resonance over conventional gameplay satisfaction—a approach more common in art games than mainstream adventure titles.
