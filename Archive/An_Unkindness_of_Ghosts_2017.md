# An Unkindness of Ghosts (2017)

**Tags:** 🌍 USA | 🎭 Gritty | 📚 Hard-SF, Dystopia | 🧬 Rebellion, Memory, Poverty

**Medium:** Novel
**Creator/Author:** Rivers Solomon

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

On the generation ship Matilda—a vessel named for the Clotilda, the last slave ship to bring enslaved Africans to America—society has stratified along racial lines. Those with dark skin are relegated to the lower decks, living lives of servitude under harsh behavioral restrictions, while the white passengers occupy the upper decks, hoarding wealth, heat, and comfort. Aster Gray, a neuroatypical healer on the lower decks, possesses medical knowledge passed down from her mother's journals. When she discovers secrets connecting her mother's suicide decades ago to the mysterious illness of the current Sovereign, Aster must navigate the ship's rigid hierarchy to uncover the truth. This debut novel by Rivers Solomon is a stunning work of Afrofuturism that explores structural racism, Queerness, and the legacy of slavery transposed to the stars.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

Aster Gray lives on the lower decks of the generation ship Matilda, a vessel that has been traveling through space for 325 years in search of a Promised Land. Born after her mother Lune's suicide when Aster was an infant, she was raised by others on the lower decks. Aster is neuroatypical—she struggles with figurative language and deception, insisting on literal, unambiguous communication—which makes her an outsider among her bunkmates who mock her and among lowdeckers who find her confusing.

Despite her differences, Aster is respected for her extensive medical knowledge, gleaned from her dead mother's journals. She maintains a secret greenhouse she calls her botanarium, where she grows plants and creates medicines to help the lower deckers survive the harsh conditions.

Aster forms an unlikely friendship with Theo, the Surgeon General, a light-skinned bastard son of the former Sovereign. Theo is almost as much of an anomaly as Aster—a member of the upper deck who actually cares about the lower deckers and uses his position to help them. With Theo's support, Aster navigates Matilda's horrors, bringing healing where she can.

The ship Matilda is organized like an antebellum plantation in space. The black and brown people on the lower decks perform the hard labor that enables the white upper deckers to hoard wealth, heat, and comfort. The upper deckers also hoard religion—their Sovereignty claims that every sin committed by lowdeckers widens the gulf between Matilda and the Promised Land they've been searching for for centuries.

As the current Sovereign falls ill, something seems wrong with Matilda's power source. Aster begins decoding her mother's journals and discovers a connection between Lune's death and the secrets at Matilda's heart. With the help of Giselle, a complicated friend and sometimes-antagonist from the lower decks, Aster traces the mystery of her mother's death.

The truth Aster uncovers is devastating: the ship's power system, the hierarchical structure, and the very foundations of the Sovereignty are built on lies and exploitation. The Promised Land may never have existed, and the ship's leadership has maintained control through manipulation and violence.

The novel culminates in Aster confronting the system's corruption and finding her place in the struggle for truth and liberation. Though not a hopeful ending in the traditional sense, it offers a fierce kind of resistance and community.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

An Unkindness of Ghosts is a profound exploration of several interconnected themes:

**Structural Racism and Systemic Oppression:** The novel transposes the plantation system to space, examining how racial hierarchies persist even in a closed society supposedly starting fresh. The lower deckers are kept in conditions resembling antebellum slavery, while upper deckers profit from their labor. Solomon explores how these systems perpetuate themselves through religion, control of information, and violent enforcement.

**The Legacy of Slavery:** The ship Matilda being named for the Clotilda is deliberate—Solomon drags up the "falsehoods and broken memories of a nightmare past" to interrogate them. The novel vivisects pervasive stereotypes and examines how trauma from slavery echoes through generations.

**Afrofuturism:** The novel centers Black and Brown experiences in a sci-fi setting, reimagining who gets to tell space stories and who gets to be the heroes. It asks: what would happen if the descendants of enslaved people built a future among the stars.

**Queerness and Neurodiversity:** All central characters are gender-variant and neurodivergent. Aster's inability to understand figurative language becomes a strength—she cannot be gaslit or manipulated through doublespeak. The novel disrupts typical SF adventure tropes by centering characters who don't fit binary categories.

**Resistance and Community:** Despite the darkness, Aster maintains "fierce, unrelenting kindness" to those she loves. The novel shows how community forms in oppressive conditions and how resistance happens even when liberation seems impossible.

**Language and Communication:** The way each deck has its own dialect, and how lowdeckers navigate these differences, shows how language both divides and unites. Aster's literalness becomes a form of truth-telling in a society built on lies.

Solomon's prose is described as "clear and precise... like drinking cool water on an empty stomach." The novel is not comfortable reading—it should not be. As Amal El-Mohtar wrote, it is "an antidote to poison," "inoculation against pervasive, enduring disease." It arms readers from the inside out, forcing confrontation with uncomfortable truths about American history and contemporary oppression.
