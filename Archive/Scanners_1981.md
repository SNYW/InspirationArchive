# Scanners (1981)

**Medium:** Film
**Raw Score:** N/A
**Total Ratings:** 0

**Creator/Author:** Written and directed by David Cronenberg

## The Pitch

In a near-future where corporations hunt for psychic humans called "scanners," a homeless man named Cameron Vale discovers he possesses devastating telepathic and telekinetic powers. Recruited by Dr. Paul Ruth to stop the rogue scanner Darryl Revok—who plans to wage war against the world using an army of newly-born psychic infants—Cameron must master his abilities and confront his own monstrous brother. The film features one of cinema's most iconic head-explosion scenes and explores themes of evolution, corporate control, and the price of power.

## The Truth

Cameron Vale (Stephen Lack) is a vagrant living on the streets, plagued by voices manifesting in his head. After accidentally causing a woman to have a seizure with his telepathic abilities, he is captured and brought to Dr. Paul Ruth (Patrick McGoohan), who explains that Cameron is one of 237 super-powered individuals known as "scanners," capable of psychic powers including telepathy and psychokinesis. Dr. Ruth injects Cameron with a drug called ephemerol, which temporarily inhibits his scanning abilities and restores his sanity, then teaches him to control his powers.

Meanwhile, Darryl Revok (Michael Ironside), a powerful scanner and former mental patient driven insane by uncontrollable streams of thoughts, volunteers at a ConSec (a private military corporation) marketing event. During a demonstration of scanning abilities, Revok psychically explodes the head of another ConSec scanner, establishing his villainous intent. Revok seeks to convince other scanners to join his quest for domination and to eliminate opposing scanners.

ConSec's new security head Braedon Keller advocates shutting down the company's scanner research program after the tragic incident. Dr. Ruth disagrees, believing scanners represent the next stage of human evolution. He convinces Cameron to infiltrate Revok's group.

Unknown to Ruth, Keller is actually working for Revok and informs him of the infiltration plan. Revok dispatches assassins to kill unaffiliated scanners, including Benjamin Pierce, a successful sculptor who copes with his abilities through art. Cameron defeats the assassins but reads Pierce's dying thoughts, learning of another group of scanners led by Kim Obrist (Jennifer O'Neill) who oppose Revok.

Cameron tracks down Obrist and attends a meeting, but Revok's assassins strike again—only Cameron and Obrist survive. They discover a pharmaceutical company, Biocarbon Amalgamate, manufactures ephemerol and that Revok is distributing the drug using a computerized ConSec plan called "Ripe."

At ConSec, Dr. Ruth admits he founded Biocarbon Amalgamate but claims no knowledge of Revok's involvement. He encourages Cameron to cyberpathically scan the computer system to find more information. Keller kills Dr. Ruth, while Cameron and Obrist escape. Cameron hacks the computer network using a telephone booth and downloads ephemerol shipment information directly into his mind. Keller is killed when the computer explodes during his attempt to intercept Cameron.

Cameron and Obrist visit Dr. Frame, someone receiving the drug. Obrist discovers a pregnant woman whose fetus has scanned her—a shocking revelation about Revok's plan. Cameron confronts Dr. Frame and learns that Revok's plan involves prescribing ephemerol to pregnant women, turning their children into scanners. Revok's group captures Cameron and Obrist and takes them to the Biocarbon Amalgamate plant.

Revok reveals that he and Cameron are both sons of Dr. Ruth, who developed ephemerol as a sedative for pregnant women. During his wife's pregnancies, Ruth discovered the drug's side effect—he made his sons the most powerful scanners in the world by administering a prototype dosage before abandoning them. Revok plans to create and lead a new generation of scanners to take over the world. Cameron refuses to join him and accuses him of acting like their father, which enrages Revok.

The brothers engage in a telepathic duel that incinerates Cameron's body. However, when Obrist encounters Revok, she discovers that Cameron has somehow managed to swap minds with Revok—Revok dies in Cameron's body during the duel, while Cameron now inhabits Revok's powerful form.

## The Core

Scanners operates on multiple thematic levels, functioning as both a horror film and a sharp social allegory. At its surface, the film explores the horror of uncontrolled psychic powers and the corruption that comes with absolute power. But Cronenberg layers deeper meanings throughout.

The scanners can be read as allegories for 1960s political radicals and counterculture hippies—individuals with transformative potential who become dangerous when they either suppress their abilities or use them for domination. The film can be seen as "an oblique reflection on what might happen when the counterculture becomes the dominant culture." At the same time, the film rejects the conservative nostalgia for the 1950s present in contemporary science-fiction films like E.T. and Back to the Future.

The fictional drug ephemerol mirrors the real-life thalidomide scandal, where a popular medication caused severe birth defects in children—a pointed commentary on corporate experimentation and pharmaceutical negligence. ConSec, the private military corporation, represents the dangers of unchecked corporate power controlling human evolution itself.

The father-son dynamic between Dr. Ruth and his two sons explores questions of genetic engineering, parental responsibility, and whether humanity can responsibly guide its own evolution. Revok's desire to rule reflects his trauma; his father's abandonment created a monster. Cameron's victory comes not through domination but through transcendence—literally abandoning his physical form to inhabit his brother's body represents a kind of evolution beyond the conflict itself.

Cronenberg's body horror aesthetic, particularly the iconic exploding head scene, visualizes the film's themes about internal pressures becoming external violence—the scanners' psychic powers making literal the psychological violence of human consciousness.
