## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Armitage III is a 1995 cyberpunk OVA series directed by Hiroyuki Ochi and produced by Anime International Company (AIC). Set in 2046 on a colonized Mars grappling with overpopulation, the story follows police officer Ross Sylibus who transfers from Chicago to the Martian city of Saint Lowell, where he partners with Naomi Armitage—a seemingly human detective who is actually a highly advanced "Third Type" android. The series was later edited into a 1997 film, Armitage III: Poly-Matrix, and followed by a 2002 sequel, Armitage: Dual-Matrix.

The series explores themes of android identity, the nature of consciousness, and what it means to be human in a world where the line between human and machine has blurred. It was influenced by H.P. Lovecraft's "The Dunwich Horror" and features the Pinocchio motif—a robot yearning for love and acceptance as a person. While not as widely known as other cyberpunk anime, it gained a cult following and was praised for its complex narrative and themes.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

In 2046, Earth has overpopulated and begun colonizing Mars. The city of Saint Lowell has been built for terraforming efforts, populated by humans and "Second type" robots. Ross Sylibus, a middle-aged policeman from Chicago, transfers to the Martian Police Department to escape trauma from a previous partner killed by a robot. His new partner is Naomi Armitage, a skilled officer who is secretly a "Third Type" android—indistinguishable from humans.

During an investigation, Ross and Armitage encounter a murdered robot revealed to be a Third Type, leading to the revelation that Third Types have been living among humans undetected. A terrorist named René D'anclaude broadcasts evidence of their existence, causing panic and riots against the Third Types. Armitage herself is revealed to be a Third Type and goes into hiding with Ross.

The investigation leads them to discover that Third Types were originally created as "assassinroids"—infiltrators with organic components—though the project was repurposed to address Mars' low birth rate. Earth discovers the project and demands the Thirds be exterminated. Armitage and Ross must fight against both the military and their own origins as they uncover the truth.

In the sequel film Armitage III: Poly-Matrix (1997), several years later, Armitage and Ross live under assumed identities on Mars with their daughter Yoko. When a new effort to restore the Third project emerges, Armitage travels to Earth to investigate, encountering a corporate conspiracy to recreate the Thirds for labor and military purposes.

The second sequel, Armitage: Dual-Matrix (2002), set years after Poly-Matrix, continues exploring themes of android identity and family as Armitage and Ross protect their daughter who may be the first naturally conceived Third Type.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Pinocchio Motif:** At its heart, Armitage III is a reworking of the Pinocchio story—a robot who wants to be a real person. Armitage struggles with questions of identity: Is she merely a machine performing humanity, or does her capacity for love and sacrifice make her genuinely human? The series poses these questions without easy answers, letting the tension between her programming and her emotions drive the narrative.

**Android Rights and Personhood:** The Third Types represent a new form of life—beings that are physically indistinguishable from humans but created rather than born. The series explores whether creation negates personhood, and whether rights should be granted based on appearance or essence. This theme resonates with contemporary debates about AI consciousness and robot rights.

**Colonialism and Population Control:** Mars represents both escape and exploitation—a new world that mirrors Earth's problems. The series critiques how societies address overpopulation through control rather than liberation, and how those in power use fear of the "other" (Third Types) to maintain authority. Earth's demand for Third Type extermination reflects xenophobic colonial attitudes.

**Identity and Memory:** Third Types have programmed memories but also develop genuine experiences. The series examines whether identity is constructed from memories (programmed or lived) or from choices and relationships. Characters like Armitage must reconcile their manufactured origins with authentic emotional development.

**Love and Reproduction:** The series culminates in the possibility of Third Types reproducing naturally—suggesting that love, not programming, defines humanity. This is embodied in Armitage's pregnancy, which represents both the ultimate expression of android humanity and a threat to those who view Thirds as mere property.
