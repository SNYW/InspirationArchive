## The Pitch

A Monstrous Corpse (괴시, Goeshi) is a 1981 South Korean zombie film and one of the earliest examples of the genre in Korean cinema. The film is notable as South Korea's first zombie movie, though it is primarily remembered today as an unofficial remake of the 1974 Spanish-Italian cult classic "Let Sleeping Corpses Die." Directed by Beom-gu Kang, the film follows a young woman and her companion who stumble upon a rural community devastated by zombies resurrected by experimental ultrasonic technology. While lacking the gory spectacle of its inspiration, the film stands as a fascinating artifact of early Korean horror and demonstrates how international genre conventions were adapted for local audiences during a period of limited film production.

## The Truth

The film opens with Su-ji, a young woman, picking up a hitchhiker named Kang Myung on the road. Su-ji wants to visit her brother-in-law, but when they arrive at his home, they find him dead. Meanwhile, in a nearby laboratory, scientists are conducting experiments with a powerful radio transmitter designed to kill harmful insects and protect crops. When the experimental ultrasonic machine is activated, its signals have an unintended and catastrophic effect: the radio waves revive the dead buried in local cemeteries, unleashing a horde of bloodthirsty zombies upon the countryside.

As the undead attack, Su-ji and Kang Myung find themselves trapped in an abandoned house, surrounded by the reanimated corpses. The local police, initially skeptical of their claims, eventually realize the severity of the situation but struggle to contain the outbreak. The film culminates in a chaotic hospital setting where the undead have gathered, leading to a tense final confrontation.

The zombie effects are notably low-budget, with the undead portrayed by actors in silver face paint—a stark contrast to the more elaborate prosthetics of Western zombie films. The violence is bloodless, with the film relying on atmosphere and tension rather than graphic gore. Detective Jee becomes an unlikely hero, though his investigations primarily hinder rather than help the survivors.

## The Core

A Monstrous Corpse represents an intriguing case study in cultural translation and the global spread of zombie cinema. While clearly inspired by Jorge Grau's "Let Sleeping Corpses Die," the film adapts its predecessor's premise to Korean cinematic traditions and production constraints of the early 1980s. The film's existence speaks to the international reach of horror conventions even during Korea's more isolated period.

The ultrasonic cause of the zombie outbreak is thematically interesting, tying the horror to technological advancement gone wrong—a common Cold War-era anxiety reflected in science fiction. The experimental transmitter represents scientific progress that spirals beyond human control, echoing similar themes in atomic age Western science fiction.

Despite its limitations, the film has gained a small cult following among zombie enthusiasts who appreciate its historical significance and its earnest, if underfunded, attempt at genre filmmaking. For scholars of Korean cinema, it represents an important early experiment in a genre that would later flourish in the country, leading to productions like "The Host" (2006) and "Train to Busan" (2016). The film's relative obscurity today makes it a rewarding discovery for dedicated genre fans seeking to trace the global evolution of zombie cinema.
