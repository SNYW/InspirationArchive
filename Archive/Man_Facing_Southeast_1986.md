# Man Facing Southeast (1986)

Tags: 🌍 Argentina | 🎭 Melancholic | 📚 Science-Fantasy | 🧬 Aliens, Religion, Isolation

**Medium:** Film
**Raw Score:** 3.8/5
**Total Ratings:** 5000
**Creator/Author:** Eliseo Subiela (Director)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Man Facing Southeast (Hombre mirando al sudeste) is a 1986 Argentine science fiction drama that stands as one of Latin America's most thought-provoking entries into the genre. Written and directed by Eliseo Subiela, the film tells the story of Dr. Julio Denis, a psychiatrist at a Buenos Aires mental hospital who encounters a mysterious patient named Rantés—who claims to be an extraterrestrial being from another planet.

The film received wide acclaim upon its video release and was selected as Argentina's entry for Best Foreign Language Film at the 60th Academy Awards. Remarkably, it served as the uncredited inspiration for the 2001 American film K-PAX, which led to a plagiarism lawsuit. The film reached #10 in a survey of the 100 greatest films of Argentine cinema, with later surveys confirming its enduring critical reputation.

What makes Man Facing Southeast remarkable is its delicate balance between science fiction and psychological drama. The film never definitively answers whether Rantés is truly an alien or simply a delusional patient—leaving both the protagonist and the audience in a state of beautiful uncertainty. This ambiguity, combined with Subiela's poetic direction and Pedro Aznar's haunting score, creates an experience that lingers long after the credits roll.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film begins in summer 1985 at the José Borda Psychiatric Hospital in Buenos Aires. Dr. Julio Denis, a staff psychiatrist, is surprised to discover an unidentified patient in the ward for non-violent delusional cases. The patient introduces himself as Rantés—an exotic-sounding name in Argentina—and claims to be a holographic projection from another planet.

Dr. Denis initially speculates that Rantés might be a fugitive hiding from the law, but is intrigued by the man's unusual presence. Rantés demonstrates extraordinary abilities: he plays the chapel organ with virtuosic skill and has psychokinetic powers that allow him to move objects without touching them. The doctor amuse d by Rantés' extraterrestrial claims suspects the man might be a genius using his elaborate tale as a charade.

Denis is a lonely man whose recent divorce has left him emotionally jaded and disconnected from life. Rantés becomes the first patient in a long time who genuinely interests him. The doctor notes the resemblance between Rantés' claim of being a projection and Adolfo Bioy Casares' classic novel "Morel's Invention," concluding that Rantés must be well-read.

Denis begins taking Rantés on outings outside the hospital, including a visit to a touring Moscow State Circus performance. During these excursions, Rantés demonstrates his psychokinetic abilities by exploring the city on his own. He also spends hours standing motionless in one of the asylum's courtyards, facing southeast. He claims to do this to receive transmissions from his planet and implies that he may actually be Denis's own hallucination.

Denis secures Rantés a job in the pathology department of the hospital. Rantés quickly earns the respect and loyalty of both the other patients and Denis through his kindness and mysterious wisdom. The doctor becomes aware that Rantés has been leaving the hospital without permission and has avoided taking his medication, but continues to advocate for him.

Rantés is visited by a young woman named Beatriz, whom Denis is immediately attracted to. She tells Denis that she met Rantés while working for an evangelical mission in a slum, where he devoted himself to helping children—particularly one with superior musical abilities. Beatriz describes Rantés as "a very good man" whom she knows only casually.

Beatriz invites Denis and Rantés to an outdoor classical concert. During the performance of Beethoven's Ninth Symphony, Rantés becomes entranced. He persuades the conductor to let him take the baton for the iconic "Ode to Joy." The audience is initially amused but becomes increasingly moved as Rantés demonstrates extraordinary conducting ability. Meanwhile, back at the asylum, the patients become agitated and excited, parading through the town toward the concert. Rantés finishes conducting but is arrested by police.

Confronted by the hospital's angry Director, Denis is more concerned for his friend than for his own job. The Director orders Rantés closely monitored and strictly medicated. Affected by the medication, Rantés becomes catatonic and rebellious. Denis believes he has become disillusioned with humankind.

Denis convinces Beatriz to meet him at his home, where they become intimate. Afterward, she confesses to Denis that she is also an alien projection, like Rantés, but assures him that she now feels emotions and can love him. Denis angrily throws her out, accusing her of being "like Rantés"—a lunatic.

When Rantés starts becoming catatonic, the Director decides to give him electroshock treatment without notifying Denis. Rantés dies from a heart attack during the procedure, not having received anesthesia. The other patients refuse to believe he is dead, instead hoping he has returned to his ship. They continue waiting for Rantés to return and take them to his planet. Meanwhile, Denis—filled with doubts and regrets—waits for Beatriz, who has departed indefinitely, to return to him.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Man Facing Southeast operates on multiple levels, weaving together science fiction, psychological drama, and spiritual allegory into a tapestry that invites endless interpretation.

**The Nature of Reality and Perception:** Subiela deliberately maintains ambiguity about whether Rantés is truly an extraterrestrial being or a deeply disturbed patient. This uncertainty serves the film's central philosophical inquiry: What is reality? The film suggests that the question itself may be less important than how we respond to those who perceive the world differently. Whether Rantés is alien or insane, his impact on the people around him is transformative and genuine.

**Alienation and Connection:** Dr. Denis represents modern man's emotional isolation—divorced, jaded, and going through the motions of life. Rantés enters his world like a spiritual awakener, forcing Denis to question his assumptions and reconnect with his capacity for wonder, compassion, and love. The film suggests that true connection requires vulnerability; Denis's ultimate rejection of Beatriz reflects his inability to accept mystery and embrace transformation.

**Christ Figure and Spiritual allegory:** Critics have noted clear parallels between Rantés and Christ. Rantés arrives at a mental institution (a kind of modern purgatory), performs miracles (healing, telekinesis), shows particular devotion to children and the poor, criticizes human hypocrisy, and ultimately subjects himself to torture (the electroshock treatment) that kills him. Like Christ, he returns—or is hoped to return. The southeast direction he faces during his meditations may reference the Argentine phrase "del otro lado" (from the other side).

**The Power of Art and Music:** Music plays a transformative role in the film. Rantés's organ playing and his conducting of Beethoven's Ninth Symphony represent art's power to transcend boundaries—whether between nations, species, or states of consciousness. The patient's synchronized reaction to the music suggests a form of collective spiritual experience that defies rational explanation.

**Critique of Institutional Authority:** The film implicitly critiques psychiatric institutions and the medical establishment. The hospital Director represents rigid, institutional thinking that cannot accommodate mystery or difference. Rantés's death under electroshock treatment—a procedure once widely used but now recognized as often harmful—symbolizes society's historical violence against those deemed "different" or "difficult to understand."

Man Facing Southeast endures because it asks questions rather than providing answers. It invites viewers to consider their own assumptions about reality, sanity, and what it means to be human. In Rantés, Subiela created one of cinema's most enigmatic and compassionate portraits of "the other"—a being whose humanity, whether real or imagined, illuminates our own.
