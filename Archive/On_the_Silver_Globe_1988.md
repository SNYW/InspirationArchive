# On the Silver Globe (1988)

**Medium:** Film

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

On the Silver Globe is a 1988 Polish epic surrealist science fiction arthouse film that defies easy categorization. Directed by Andrzej Żuławski (known for Possession and The Devil), this ambitious project was adapted from "The Lunar Trilogy" written by his grand-uncle Jerzy Żuławski around 1900. The film tells the story of a group of dissident astronauts who escape from a dystopian Earth and crash land on an unnamed planet, where they give rise to a new human civilization. Many years later, a second astronaut arrives and becomes entangled in religious prophecy and conflict with the planet's indigenous inhabitants. Production on this epic was plagued by political interference—it began in 1976-1977 but was shut down by Polish authorities, with the reels ordered destroyed. Żuławski eventually completed the film in 1988 using preserved footage plus voice-over commentary to fill narrative gaps. The result is a haunting, philosophical meditation on civilization, religion, colonialism, and the cyclical nature of human history.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens in the far future, where two researchers explore a derelict palace inhabited by a tribe of indigenous people. A horse rider brings them a technological artifact—a "Small Transmission Module"—that fell from the sky in flames. In their underground laboratory, the researchers discover it contains the video diary of an astronaut whose spacecraft crashed at least 50-60 years ago.

The bulk of the film consists of this video diary's contents. A group of dissident astronauts have escaped from a degraded, dystopian Earth and crash land on an unnamed Earth-like planet. One astronaut dies from injuries shortly after the crash. Only three survivors remain: a woman named Marta and two men, Piotr and Jerzy. Communication with Earth is lost, and the survivors decide to settle on the planet's seashore, giving rise to a new human race.

After Marta gives birth to their first son, she notices the child is growing far faster than human children would on Earth. Later, an unseen enemy kills Piotr, and Marta dies in childbirth. Decades pass. Jerzy grows old but ages far slower than the new humans, who have become a tribe of several dozen partially feral people who can no longer communicate with him. They view "The Old Man" as a demigod. Before Jerzy dies, he sends a video diary back to Earth containing recordings from handheld video cameras.

Many years later, a planetary scientist named Marek is sent to the planet. When he arrives, the ruling priests believe him to be the returning Old Man and declare him the messiah who, according to prophecy, must free humans from the telepathic bird-like humanoids called "Sherns"—the planet's native inhabitants. Marek accepts this role and leads a military campaign against the Sherns.

A dark revelation emerges: Marek was sent to the planet because his actress girlfriend was sleeping with a fellow officer named Jacek, and they wanted to eliminate him so they could continue their affair without interference. Initially, the humans succeed and capture the Shern leader Avius, but their subsequent landing in the Shern city ends in disaster. The priests then conclude that Marek was an outcast from Earth rather than the prophesied messiah. Marek is stoned and crucified.

The film is presented in an unconventional structure: approximately 5-6 hours of footage was shot, but only about 2 hours survive in the final 1988 edit. Żuławski added voice-over narration describing missing scenes to create a coherent narrative from the preserved footage.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

On the Silver Globe is a profound meditation on multiple interconnected themes:

**Cyclical History and Civilization's Rise and Fall:** The film suggests that human civilization is doomed to repeat itself. The astronauts flee a decaying Earth only to recreate the same patterns of religion, warfare, and hierarchy on the new planet. The Sherns represent the indigenous peoples historically displaced by colonizers, while the humans become the new colonizers.

**Colonialism and Displacement:** The narrative can be read as an allegory for European colonialism. The astronauts arrive on an inhabited planet and displace the Sherns, mirroring historical patterns of colonization. The priests' manipulation of religious prophecy to gain power mirrors colonial powers' use of religion to justify conquest.

**The Nature of Faith and Prophecy:** Marek is initially a skeptical scientist who becomes a religious figurehead almost accidentally. The film questions whether prophecy shapes events or whether events are retroactively interpreted to fit prophecy. His crucifixion echoes the Christian narrative, suggesting that messianic figures are often martyrs to their own circumstances.

**Time, Memory, and Documentary Truth:** The film's unconventional structure—video diaries within a frame story—raises questions about how history is recorded and remembered. The fragmented footage with voice-over commentary mirrors how we construct narratives from incomplete evidence.

**Isolation and Otherness:** Both Jerzy (who ages slower than the new humans) and the Sherns (who are telepathic and fundamentally different) are others in their own society. The film explores how difference creates alienation and power imbalances.

The production history itself reflects the film's themes—the Polish authorities attempted to destroy the film, but members of the cast and crew preserved the materials. This act of preservation mirrors the film's narrative of fighting against erasure and loss.
