# The Book of Strange New Things (2014)

**Medium:** Novel
**Author:** Michel Faber

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Peter Leigh is a humble pastor living on Earth when he receives a mysterious summons from a religious organization called the Oasis Project. He's chosen to travel to a distant planet—known only as the Planet of the New Sun—to minister to its alien inhabitants, a species who have never known human contact. The twist: these aliens have developed an obsessive fascination with a human Bible, and they've specifically requested a pastor to help them understand its teachings.

But Peter soon discovers that his ministry is complicated by the fact that his messages back to Earth are increasingly cryptic, and the colonists he's leaving behind on a dying planet seem to be facing their own spiritual crises. As Peter immerses himself in the alien culture, teaching them about a God they desperately want to believe in, the gap between his old life and his new one widens into something unbridgeable.

The novel is a haunting meditation on faith, isolation, and what it means to be human in the face of the truly alien.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The novel opens with Peter Leigh, a former carpenter turned pastor, living a quiet life in contemporary England. His wife, Bea, has been his rock throughout his spiritual journey, but their marriage is tested when Peter receives an invitation to join the Oasis Project—a mysterious organization that's established contact with aliens on a distant world.

The aliens, called the Oasans, have encountered human technology and religious texts, and they've become particularly obsessed with the Christian Bible. They've requested a pastor to help them understand scripture. Despite Bea's protests and the obvious strain it will put on their marriage, Peter feels called to accept the mission.

On the Planet of the New Sun (later referred to as the Planet), Peter finds a harsh but beautiful alien world. The Oasans are strange creatures—they communicate through a complex system of clicks and gestures, and their biology is utterly foreign to human understanding. They're gentle, curious, and desperate to learn about the Christian God.

Peter begins his ministry, translating the Bible for the Oasans and answering their endless questions about scripture. The aliens are particularly fascinated by the Garden of Eden, sin, and redemption. They grapple with concepts like salvation and damnation with an intensity that both excites and disturbs Peter.

Meanwhile, communications with Earth become increasingly difficult. Messages from Bea grow more frantic—she describes a world in ecological collapse, with mass migrations, resource wars, and societal breakdown. The Oasis Project on Earth is falling apart, and Bea is struggling to survive.

As months pass, Peter becomes more deeply entrenched in his work. The Oasans begin to exhibit signs of religious devotion—they build churches, conduct ceremonies, and develop their own interpretations of scripture. But Peter starts to notice troubling patterns. The Oasans seem to be cherry-picking biblical passages, focusing heavily on apocalypse and redemption while ignoring other teachings.

The central tension of the novel emerges when Peter realizes the Oasans may have their own agenda. They've been selectively amplifying certain biblical prophecies that speak of a "New Sun" and a "chosen people"—concepts that align uncomfortably with their own messianic beliefs. Peter begins to question whether he's truly spreading the word of God or simply projecting human religious frameworks onto a species that doesn't share human biology, psychology, or worldview.

 Bea's messages become increasingly fragmented and desperate. She tells Peter about the total collapse of civilization on Earth—the seas are dying, governments have fallen, and she's surviving in a makeshift community. Her final transmissions suggest she may not survive much longer.

Peter receives a message that a rescue ship will be sent to bring him home, but he faces an impossible choice: return to a dying Earth to be with his wife, or stay with the Oasans who now depend on him spiritually. The novel ends ambiguously—Peter's ultimate decision remains uncertain, but it's clear that his faith has been fundamentally transformed by his experience.

The Oasans, in their own way, have created a new religion—a synthesis of Christianity and their own alien worldview. Whether this is a genuine spiritual evolution or a profound misunderstanding of human theology remains one of the novel's central mysteries.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

The Book of Strange New Things is ultimately about the impossibility of truly communicating across profound difference. Peter tries to translate Christian theology for beings who don't share human biology, culture, or conceptual frameworks—and the results are inevitably distorted. The novel asks whether faith is universal or whether it's deeply tied to human experience.

The alien Oasans represent the ultimate Other—beings so different from us that even our most sacred texts become subject to radical reinterpretation. Their embrace of certain biblical concepts (apocalypse, chosen people, divine intervention) while ignoring others (love, forgiveness, grace) suggests that any contact with the truly alien will transform both parties.

Faber also explores the fragility of human civilization. Earth in the novel is dying—ecologically collapsed, violence-ridden, its institutions crumbling. This isn't science fiction prediction but contemporary anxiety transposed onto a speculative framework. The contrast between Peter's spiritual work among the aliens and Bea's desperate survival on Earth creates a poignant meditation on what matters most.

The novel resists easy answers. Is Peter's ministry a success? Has he brought the Oasans to God, or has he accidentally created a dangerous new cult? Has his faith grown or been shattered by his experiences? Faber leaves these questions deliberately unresolved, trusting readers to sit with the discomfort of uncertainty.

What makes this significant is its approach to first contact—it isn't about invasion, technology, or war, but about the deepest human question: what do we believe, and can we share it with those who are fundamentally unlike us?
