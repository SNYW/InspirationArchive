## The Pitch

The Pass (Pereval) is a 1988 Soviet animated short film from Soyuzmultfilm, directed by Vladimir Tarasov and written by Kir Bulychov. Based on the first chapter of Bulychov's novel "The Settlement" (translated as "Those Who Survive"), this 30-minute animated feature tells the story of Earth's first interstellar expedition gone tragically wrong. When a scientific spacecraft crash-lands on an uninhabited planet, the surviving crew must abandon their ship and establish a primitive settlement. Seventeen years later, a new generation—born after the crash and knowing nothing but primitive survival—embarks on a perilous journey back to the abandoned spacecraft to retrieve essential supplies and potentially send a rescue signal to Earth.

## The Truth

The scientific spacecraft Pole makes an emergency landing on a distant, uninhabited planet after a radiation leak during descent. The crew survives but is forced to evacuate quickly, leaving behind critical supplies including food and weapons. Over the next 17 years, the survivors adapt to primitive living conditions, establishing a settlement far from the crash site and using basic weapons to defend against local predators.

The protagonist, Oleg, was an infant during the crash and remembers nothing of Earth or the spacecraft—his entire world is the settlement. When a new expedition is planned to retrieve supplies from the abandoned Pole, Oleg volunteers alongside his friends Dick and Mariana. They journey under the guidance of their elder, Thomas, who remembers the crash.

Along the way, the young explorers encounter remnants of previous failed expeditions and face numerous dangerous obstacles. Thomas makes the ultimate sacrifice to save Oleg, leaving the young man to lead the trio to their destination. Upon reaching the spacecraft, they discover preserved supplies, functional blasters, and Earth food for the first time in their lives. Oleg also finds his father's blaster, which he uses to defend his friends from a giant lizard.

Inside the ship, Oleg stumbles upon the radio room and accidentally activates a signal. This moment reveals the possibility of rescue. The film ends with the trio returning to the settlement with supplies and books, planning to learn how to operate the communication equipment to send a rescue signal to Earth—along with a domesticated goat that has now produced offspring.

## The Core

The Pass stands as a testament to Soviet science fiction animation, combining hard science fiction elements with coming-of-age themes. The film explores humanity's resilience and adaptability when thrust into alien environments, examining how civilization rebuilds from scratch and how subsequent generations inherit both the trauma and the hope of their predecessors.

The story's coming-of-age narrative follows Oleg as he discovers his heritage and identity through confronting the remnants of the old world. Unlike typical hero journeys, Oleg doesn't seek adventure—he stumbles into it, learning about his father's legacy and his own potential in the process. His discovery of the radio room and accidental activation of the signal represents both literal hope for rescue and a metaphorical reconnection with human civilization.

The film also touches on themes of generational divide—Thomas and the other survivors carry memories of Earth and the crash, while their children know only the settlement. This tension between remembering and forgetting, between looking back and moving forward, gives the story its emotional depth. The sacrifice of Thomas to save Oleg echoes classic themes of generational sacrifice and the passing of responsibility to the next generation.

Visually, The Pass showcases the distinctive style of Soviet animation at Soyuzmultfilm, blending detailed character work with imaginative alien landscapes. The film's depiction of the alien planet and its creatures reflects the Soviet tradition of creating imaginative, often surreal visual worlds within animated storytelling.

The Pass remains a relatively obscure but significant work in the canon of Soviet science fiction animation, representing a unique blend of Western-style hard science fiction storytelling with distinctly Soviet animation traditions.
