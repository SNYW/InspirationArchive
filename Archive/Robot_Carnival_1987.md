# Robot Carnival (1987)

**Medium:** OVA (Original Video Animation) / Anime

**Creator/Author:** Multiple Directors (Katsuhiro Otomo, Koji Morimoto, Hidetoshi Ōmori, Yasuomi Umetsu, Takashi Nakamura, Hiroyuki Kitakubo, Hiroyuki Kitazume, Mao Lamdo)

## The Pitch

Robot Carnival is a groundbreaking 1987 Japanese anthology OVA featuring nine distinct short films by different animators, each exploring robots and humanity's relationship with machines. From the surreal to the comedic to the deeply melancholic, this collection showcases emerging talent who would later become legends in the anime industry—including Katsuhiro Otomo (director of Akira). The film ranges from a mad scientist accidentally killing himself while teaching his creation to dance, to a post-apocalyptic android's journey to rescue its human companion, to a surreal tale of a traveling carnival of robots destroying a desert village. With music by the legendary Joe Hisaishi, Robot Carnival is a time capsule of late-80s Japanese animation creativity—a must-see for anyone interested in the evolution of anime as an art form.

## The Truth

**Opening/Ending:** A boy discovers a "coming soon" poster for the Robot Carnival in a desert wasteland. A gigantic rusted machine containing countless performing robots grinds through his village, destroying it. At the end, the Carnival gets stuck on a dune, breaks apart, and years later a family discovers a music box among the ruins—its dancing robot explodes, leaving only "END" in letters.

**Franken's Gears:** A mad scientist attempts to bring his robot to life using lightning, just like Frankenstein. When the robot is successfully animated, it mimics the scientist's movements. Overjoyed, the scientist dances gleefully, trips, falls, and dies. The robot, copying its creator, trips and falls on him, killing them both.

**Deprive:** An alien invasion of robot foot soldiers attacks a city, kidnapping people including a young girl. Her damaged android companion retains her locket and is later upgraded into a powerful combat android with a human disguise. The android defeats the alien forces and rescues the girl, who recognizes him by the locket he still carries.

**Presence:** A man secretly builds an automaton in his shed to compensate for emotional isolation from his wife and family. When the automaton develops its own personality, he smashes her in panic. Years later, he has visions of her, and eventually returns to take her hand and walk into the distance, vanishing before his shocked wife.

**Star Light Angel:** Two teenage friends visit a robot-themed amusement park. When one girl's boyfriend dumps her for her friend, she runs away and enters a virtual reality ride. Her dark emotions summon a giant laser-breathing mecha, but a park employee in a robot costume helps her confront her feelings and move forward.

**Cloud:** A robot walks through time, witnessing human evolution and eventual self-destruction. Done in a scratchboard/etching style, the robot experiences the rise and fall of civilization before being granted humanity by an angel who mourns human mortality.

**Strange Tales of Meiji Machine Culture: Westerner's Invasion:** Set in 19th century Japan, two giant wooden robots—one operated by Westerners attempting to colonize, one by Japanese locals—battle in the style of vintage movie serials. A comedic take on cultural conflict and technological modernization.

**Chicken Man and Red Neck:** In Tokyo, a robotic magician named Red Neck brings all machines to life for one night of revelry. Only a drunken "Chicken Man" witnesses the chaos. When dawn breaks, the robots are embedded high in skyscrapers while ordinary citizens continue their lives below, oblivious.

## The Core

Robot Carnival is fundamentally about humanity's complex, often contradictory relationship with technology. Each segment explores different aspects of this relationship: fear and fascination, creation and destruction, isolation and connection.

The anthology format allows multiple perspectives on the same theme. Some segments view robots as threats (Deprive, Chicken Man and Red Neck), others as companions (Star Light Angel), and still others as metaphors for human loneliness and the desire for connection (Presence). The recurring motif of robots as both entertainers and destroyers reflects anxieties about technology's dual nature.

Notably, many segments feature creators destroyed by their own creations (Franken's Gears), reflecting the Frankenstein complex—the fear that creating artificial life leads to the creator's downfall. Yet others show humans and robots forming genuine bonds (Deprive, Presence), suggesting hope for coexistence.

The film also showcases the diversity of anime as an art form. Each segment has a completely different visual style, from traditional anime to experimental scratchboard animation to retro serial styles. This variety was intentional—the directors were given near-complete creative freedom, and many were animators with no prior directing experience. The result is a unique document of late-80s Japanese animation creativity, a snapshot of artists who would go on to shape the industry.
