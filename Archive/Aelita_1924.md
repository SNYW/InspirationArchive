## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Aelita (also known as "Aelita: Queen of Mars") is a 1924 Soviet silent science fiction film directed by Yakov Protazanov. It was one of the earliest feature films about space travel and is considered a landmark of Soviet cinema.

The film is based on Alexei Tolstoy's 1923 novel and tells the story of an engineer who travels to Mars and becomes involved in a popular uprising against the ruling class.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

Moscow, 1921. Engineer Los becomes obsessed with traveling to Mars after receiving a mysterious wireless message. He constructs a rocket ship and, with his friend Gusev, travels to Mars.

On Mars, they discover a civilization ruled by Queen Aelita and the Elders, where underground slaves are kept in cold storage. Gusev inspires a slave revolt, and Aelita takes command—though she ultimately intends to rule Mars herself.

The film famously ends with Los discovering the entire Mars adventure was a daydream, prompted by an advertisement for tires.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Soviet Ideology:** The film's revolutionary themes reflect Soviet ideology, with the uprising of oppressed workers against their rulers.

**Constructivist Aesthetics:** The Martian sets designed by Isaac Rabinovich and Victor Simov are iconic examples of constructivist art in cinema.

**Early Sci-Fi:** One of the first feature-length films about space travel, predating Flash Gordon and influencing future sci-fi cinema.

**Dream vs Reality:** The twist ending questions whether the Martian revolution was real or fantasy, reflecting themes of escapism and disillusionment.
