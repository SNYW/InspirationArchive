# The Man from the First Century (1962)

## The Pitch

The Man from the First Century (original Czech title: Muž z prvního století, also known as Man in Outer Space) is a 1962 Czechoslovak science fiction comedy directed by Oldřich Lipský. The film was entered into the 1962 Cannes Film Festival, marking one of the few Eastern Bloc sci-fi comedies to receive international recognition during the Cold War.

The story follows Josef, an upholsterer working on the first Czech spacecraft, who accidentally launches the vessel during final adjustments and finds himself transported to the planet Blue Star. Upon arriving, he encounters Adam, an inhabitant of this distant world, and the two return together to Earth—only to discover that 600 years have passed. Josef is initially institutionalized as a delusional psychiatric patient, but escapes with Adam's help, who proves invisible to contemporary Earthlings. Together, they work to return Josef to his own time.

## The Truth

Set in the 20th century, the Czechoslovak space program is preparing to launch its first spacecraft. Josef Kocián (played by Miloš Kopecký), an upholsterer at the rocket factory, is tasked with making the final interior adjustments to the vessel. During this work, he accidentally activates the launch mechanism and is rocketed into space.

Josef crash-lands on Blue Star, a distant planet inhabited by humans who have evolved beyond Earth's current technological level. He meets Adam (Radovan Lukavský), a Blue Star resident who becomes his guide and companion. Adam explains that time moves differently on Blue Star, and when Josef expresses his desire to return home, Adam agrees to accompany him back to Earth.

Upon their return, Josef and Adam discover that 600 years have passed. The world has changed dramatically, and Josef's claims of space travel are met with skepticism. He is committed to a psychiatric hospital, where his story is treated as a delusion. However, Adam, who is invisible to Earthlings, helps Josef escape from the institution.

The two then work together to find a way to return Josef to his proper time, with Adam's advanced knowledge proving crucial. The film concludes with Josef managing to return to his own era, though the exact mechanism is played for comedic effect.

## The Core

**Time and Relativity:** The film explores themes of time dilation and the relativity of experience through its premise of time passing differently on another planet. Josef's brief journey results in centuries passing on Earth, a concept that would become central to much science fiction but was relatively rare in 1962 Eastern European cinema.

**Social Satire:** The comedy serves as gentle satire of bureaucracy and institutional thinking. Josef's institutionalization for telling the truth about his journey comments on how society treats those who challenge accepted paradigms.

**Czechoslovak Space Race Context:** The film emerged during a period when Czechoslovakia, as part of the Eastern Bloc, was participating in the broader Space Age enthusiasm. The film blends this optimism with distinctly Czech humor and storytelling traditions.

**Early Eastern Bloc Sci-Fi:** The Man from the First Century represents an interesting moment in Soviet-bloc science fiction, offering a lighter, more comedic take on the genre compared to the often serious or cautionary tales produced in other Eastern European countries. Its selection at Cannes demonstrates international recognition of Czechoslovak cinematic talent during this period.
