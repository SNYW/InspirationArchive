## The Pitch

Krakatit is a 1948 Czechoslovak science fiction mystery film directed by Otakar Vávra, based on Karel Čapek's 1922 novel of the same name. The story follows Prokop, a brilliant chemist who awakens in a hospital with severe burns to his hands, suffering from delirium. As his memories gradually return, he recalls inventing "krakatit"—a devastating explosive named after the volcano Krakatoa that can be detonated remotely using high-frequency radio signals. His former friend Jiří has sold the formula to Balttin Works, a foreign weapons manufacturer, and they coerce Prokop into recreating his invention. The film explores the terrifying power of scientific discovery weaponized for war and the moral responsibility of inventors.

## The Truth

The film opens with a barely conscious, unidentified man—the chemist Prokop—receiving intensive care from a doctor and nurse. His hands are badly burned and cut. As he wanders through delirium on the streets, he encounters his old classmate Jiří Tomeš, who learns that Prokop has invented a powerful explosive called "krakatit."

Jiří takes Prokop home to recover, but during a fever dream, Prokop reveals the formula for krakatit to a university professor—actually a projection of his own mind. Jiří secretly writes down the formula and sells it to Balttin Works, a foreign weapons manufacturer represented by the mysterious Carson.

Prokop later recovers at a countryside doctor's home, where he reads newspaper reports about krakatit being promoted by Carson. His memory returns fully—he remembers the explosion in his laboratory. He rushes to confront Carson, who reveals that while they have the formula, they cannot reproduce the explosive without Prokop's expertise. Prokop refuses to help, but is kidnapped and forcibly brought to the Balttin palace to work in their chemical laboratory.

At Balttin Works, Prokop discovers the terrifying potential of krakatit—it can be detonated remotely from great distances using radio signals. The company plans to use it as a weapon of war. Prokop must decide whether to help them perfect the device or find a way to destroy his invention and prevent its use.

## The Core

**The Ethics of Scientific Discovery:** Čapek's novel, written in 1922, presents a prescient meditation on the responsibility of scientists for their inventions. Prokop's creation of krakatit represents the eternal dilemma of discovery—scientific knowledge itself is neutral, but its application can bring either great benefit or catastrophic harm. The film emphasizes how easily brilliant minds can be coerced or corrupted by those who seek power through technology.

**War and the Military-Industrial Complex:** The Balttin Works represents the military-industrial complex that harnesses scientific discovery for profit and warfare. The film's critique of arms manufacturers who profit from conflict resonated strongly in post-WWII Czechoslovakia and remains relevant today. The remote detonation capability of krakatit foreshadows modern drone warfare and precision weapons.

**Individual Conscience vs. Collective Power:** Prokop stands alone against a powerful corporation and the machinery of war. His struggle represents the individual scientist's confrontation with forces far larger than themselves—corporate interests, governments, and military establishments that would use their discoveries for destruction. The film asks whether one person can resist such overwhelming power.

**Czech Science Fiction Tradition:** Krakatit represents an important moment in Czechoslovak science fiction cinema, connecting to the rich literary tradition of Karel Čapek—who coined the word "robot" in his play R.U.R. The film adaptation of his novel continues Čapek's exploration of technology's impact on humanity, delivered through the lens of 1940s European anxieties about war and totalitarianism.
