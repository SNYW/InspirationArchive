# Star Odyssey (1979)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Star Odyssey (Italian: Sette uomini d'oro nello spazio, also known as "Seven Gold Men in Space") is a 1979 Italian space opera film directed by Alfonso Brescia under the pseudonym Al Brescia. Produced in the wake of Star Wars' massive success, it represents one of four low-budget Italian sci-fi films in Brescia's interconnected series, serving as the final entry following "Cosmos: War of the Planets," "Battle of the Stars," and "War of the Robots." The film unfolds in the year 2312, when humanity discovers that Earth has been auctioned off to an evil alien despot named Kress by a galactic council of alien civilizations. Now facing enslavement, Earth's fate rests with Professor Maury and his ragtag group of human and robot defenders who attempt to reclaim the planet from Kress and his army of cyborgs. The film is notable primarily as a cult curiosity—a quintessential example of Italian "mergatta" filmmaking, where studios rapidly produced knockoffs of successful Hollywood films with minimal budgets, creating works that have since become beloved for their intentional or unintentional comedic value.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens in the year 2312, where we learn that a council of alien civilizations has been auctioning off "insignificant" planets to the highest bidder. The blue marble known as Earth (Sol 3) comes up for auction, and the winning bid is placed by Kress, an evil despot from another galaxy. This auction establishes that Earth is considered unimportant in the grand scheme of the universe—mere property to be bought and sold among alien powers.

Kress arrives on Earth in his massive spaceship, marking the first contact between humanity and alien life. Rather than arriving as a benevolent visitor or destroyer, Kress comes as an owner claiming his new property. He immediately begins using his robot army to capture humans, gathering humanoid slaves to sell to his fellow evil counterparts in other parts of the galaxy. The implication is that Earth has simply become inventory in an intergalactic slave trade.

The primary human resistance comes in the form of Professor Maury, a kindly scientist who assembles a ragtag group of defenders. His team includes several humans and robot allies. Notably, one of the human defenders wears a Spider-Man t-shirt, an anachronistic touch that underscores the film's bizarre tone. The defenders operate from a hidden base, planning their attacks against Kress's occupation.

Throughout the film, Kress and his cyborg army systematically capture humans while Professor Maury's group attempts to develop a strategy. The key discovery comes when Maury and his team determine that the metal of Kress's alien ship—called "Iridium" or "etherium"—has a weakness. This becomes their strategic target: destroy the ship's metal, and they can neutralize Kress's advantage.

The film's climax involves the defenders executing their plan to destroy the Iridium metal, leading to a battle between the human/robot resistance and Kress's forces. Ultimately, Kress is defeated and forced to retreat from Earth. However, the resolution carries an ironic twist: rather than simply losing, Kress resells the planet at the space auction for a profit, suggesting that Earth's value was ultimately transactional—the despot simply found a better deal.

The film concludes with Earth "saved" but not through any grand heroic victory; rather, through the mechanics of alien capitalism. This ambiguous ending leaves humanity's fate uncertain, having been both conquered and sold in the same narrative arc.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Alien Capitalism and Ownership:** The film's central premise presents a surreal vision of cosmic economics, where entire planets are treated as commodities in an intergalactic marketplace. This reflects concerns about consumerism and commodification, imagining a universe where even Earth—the entire human race—is merely inventory. Kress represents predatory capitalism made literal: he acquires property (humanity) not for any ideological purpose but purely for profit, buying low and selling high.

**Humanity's Cosmic Insignificance:** By opening with Earth's auction as just another transaction among many, the film dramatically diminishes human importance. We are not special, not chosen, not even particularly noteworthy—we are simply another asset to be appraised and sold. This theme of cosmic insignificance was not uncommon in 1970s sci-fi, but Star Odyssey presents it through the lens of economic transactions rather than typical "we are not alone" narratives.

**Imitation and Originality:** As a Star Wars knockoff, Star Odyssey embodies questions about originality and imitation in cinema. The film reuses effects and costumes from Brescia's previous "War of the Robots," creating a self-referential ecosystem of Italian genre filmmaking. This mirrors the film's theme of repetition and replication within the narrative itself—the aliens auction off the same planets repeatedly, Kress buys and resells Earth for profit, and Brescia recycles the same footage.

**Camp Aesthetics and Enjoyment:** Critics have noted that Star Odyssey functions as unintentional comedy. "Popular Mechanics" described the light-sabers as "cardboard swords painted with fluorescent paint," while "Worst Movies Ever Made" called the effects "laughable" and the story "confused at best." The film has developed a cult following among fans of so-bad-it's-good cinema. This camp quality—the earnestness of its low-budget production values—actually makes the film compelling in ways its creators likely never intended.

**Italian Genre Film Tradition:** Star Odyssey represents a specific moment in Italian cinema history—the mergatta phenomenon. These rapidly-produced knockoffs capitalized on the success of hits like Star Wars, delivering sci-fi adventures to Italian audiences hungry for the genre. While these films are rarely considered great art, they document an important commercial cinema era and remain fascinating cultural artifacts. Brescia, who directed numerous these films, became synonymous with the Italian sci-fi knockoff tradition.
