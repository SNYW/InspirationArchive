## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Who Wants to Kill Jessie? is a 1966 Czechoslovak science fiction comedy film that pushes the boundaries between dreams and reality. Directed by Václav Vorlíček and written by Miloš Macourek, the film tells the story of a young couple—scientists Beránek and Růženka—who invent a remarkable machine capable of materializing objects and people from their dreams directly into the real world. What begins as a remarkable scientific breakthrough quickly spirals into chaotic comedy when they accidentally summon Jessie, a dynamic comics character from Růženka's dream illustrations. The film is notable for its imaginative premise and playful exploration of the collision between everyday reality and the fantastical world of comic book imagery, complete with word balloons, sound effects, and larger-than-life superhero personas literally walking the streets of Prague.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film centers on document Růženka Beránková, a comics illustrator who frequently dreams vivid comic book scenarios featuring a dynamic heroine named Jessie. Her husband, document Jindřich Beránek, is a scientist working on a revolutionary machine designed to extract and materialize objects from the dream realm into physical reality. Their initial experiments successfully bring simple objects from Růženka's dreams into the lab—a practice target, a chair—proving their machine works.

The trouble begins when Růženka's dreams grow more ambitious. In her latest dream, she imagines Jessie, a powerful comic book heroine, and the machine accidentally materializes the fictional character into the real world. Jessie emerges from the machine with all her comic book attributes: superhuman strength, dramatic poses, and the ability to fly. However, the machine's power source proves inadequate for containing such a powerful dreamed entity, and Jessie begins to destabilize, threatening to cause catastrophic damage to the surrounding area.

Complications multiply when the team realizes they cannot simply "delete" Jessie—she exists now, and simply turning off the machine might cause her to disappear in a potentially lethal explosion. The scientists must find a way to safely discharge Jessie's energy or discover a power source capable of sustaining her existence. Additional chaos ensues as other characters from Růženka's comics—Superman, a gunslinger—also begin to materialize partially, adding to the mayhem.

The film reaches its climax as the scientists scramble to find a solution. They ultimately discover that Jessie can be safely discharged by having her complete a heroic act—saving someone in danger—which provides a narrative "conclusion" to her story arc that allows her energy to be safely released. The machine is thus able to send her back to the dream realm, but only after she completes her story.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Power of Imagination and Fiction:** The film explores the fascinating premise that fictional creations, when given sufficient mental energy and belief, can become real. Růženka's dream-creations are not merely fantasies but possess a kind of proto-existence that the machine can tap into. This theme suggests that stories and characters, when genuinely imagined with passion and detail, take on a life of their own—commenting on the creative process and the relationship between creators and their creations.

**Reality vs. Fantasy:** The collision between mundane reality and comic book imagery serves as the film's central comedic and thematic engine. The film plays with the idea that our fantasies, when stripped of their fictional context, might be impossible to sustain in the real world. Jessie's inability to exist peacefully without a "story" to contain her suggests that narratives need structure and resolution to function.

**Science and Creativity:** The film presents an interesting dialogue between scientific rationalism (Jindřich's machine) and creative imagination (Růženka's comics). Their marriage represents the potential union of science and art, but the film also shows the dangers when these two realms interact without proper preparation. The machine represents scientific capability exceeding ethical or practical preparation.

**Eastern Bloc Creativity:** Made in 1960s Czechoslovakia, the film represents a fascinating example of Eastern Bloc science fiction that focuses on comedic and fantastical elements rather than the technological utopianism or dystopia common in Western science fiction. The film's imaginative approach to genre filmmaking within the constraints of the Czechoslovak film industry demonstrates the creative ways filmmakers worked within and around genre conventions.
