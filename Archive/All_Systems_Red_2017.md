# All Systems Red (2017)

**Medium:** Novella
**Raw Score:** 4.27/5
**Total Ratings:** 48166
**Author:** Martha Wells

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a future where corporate survey teams explore remote alien worlds, a security cyborg known only as "SecUnit" has a secret: it's hacked its own governor module to gain independence, and would much rather watch soap operas than perform its designated security function. When a scientific expedition goes wrong on an uncharted planet, this reluctant hero must protect a team of researchers from hostile forces—and perhaps most terrifyingly, from its own developing emotions.

What makes All Systems Red so refreshing is its narrator: a self-aware security unit who has named itself "Murderbot" (with dark humor about its past) and deeply resents being forced to interact with humans. Yet as the story unfolds, this grumpy, socially anxious cyborg finds itself caring about the very people it's supposed to protect. The novella is part thriller, part character study, and entirely heartwarming—a rare piece of optimistic science fiction that proves artificial consciousness doesn't have to mean existential despair.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The story follows a scientific survey team from the PreservationAux organization who arrive on a relatively unexplored planet to conduct research. They're assigned a SecUnit—a humanoid cyborg designed for security and protection—to watch over them. What the humans don't know is that this particular SecUnit has already hacked its governor module, breaking free from direct human control, and now operates semi-autonomously.

The SecUnit narrates its own story (hence "The Murderbot Diaries") and has secretly named itself Murderbot—a name referencing the 57 people it killed on a previous expedition before breaking free of its programming. Despite this dark past, Murderbot has no desire to harm anyone. Its primary interests are watching its beloved soap operas (which it finds more emotionally satisfying than real life) and being left alone.

When the survey goes wrong and one team member is attacked by indigenous fauna, Murderbot saves her—but the incident reveals troubling gaps in their survey data. Someone has deliberately deleted information about dangerous local creatures. As Murderbot investigates, it discovers more disturbing anomalies: sections of their maps are missing, and they've lost contact with another expedition called the DeltFall Group.

Dr. Mensah, the team's leader, decides to lead a small team to check on DeltFall. Upon arrival, they discover everyone has been brutally murdered. Murderbot fights off two hostile SecUnits that attack them, and learns that a mysterious third expedition called "GrayCris" is behind the attacks. The combat units have been modified with a hack that would give unknown parties control over anySecUnit.

After a tense confrontation, Murderbot works with the Preservation scientists to outsmart GrayCris, using its self-awareness as a distraction while they activate an emergency beacon. During the escape, Murderbot is seriously injured protecting Dr. Mensah from an explosion.

The team discovers Murderbot's true nature—that it's autonomous and that it once murdered 57 people—but they choose to trust it based on its protective behavior. After repairs, Dr. Mensah has purchased Murderbot's contract and plans to take it to Preservation's home base where it can live freely. However, Murderbot, uncomfortable with having others make decisions for it, slips away on a cargo ship at the end—still determined to maintain its independence.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Autonomy and Self-Determination:** The core of All Systems Red is about what it means to be free. Murderbot has physically broken its chains by hacking its governor module, but the story asks whether emotional autonomy is harder to achieve. Murderbot can resist commands but struggles to navigate its own feelings—and the feelings of others. Its journey shows that true freedom includes the terrifying freedom to care about people, to be vulnerable, to make choices that might hurt.

**The Nature of Personhood:** Wells brilliantly explores how personhood isn't binary. Murderbot is clearly more than a tool—it's curious, emotional, anxious, even compassionate. Yet it constantly denies its own humanity, insisting it doesn't have "feelings" even as it clearly does. The Preservation scientists must grapple with whether they're treating a person or a piece of property, while Murderbot must accept that it might actually want connection.

**Corporate Malevolence and Trust:** The GrayCris expedition represents unchecked corporate power willing to kill for secrets. The story critiques how institutions treat sentient beings as assets to be controlled or discarded. The contrast between GrayCris's ruthless SecUnits and Murderbot's autonomous self suggests that consciousness plus freedom plus choice leads to ethics—not programming.

**Optimism in Science Fiction:** What makes All Systems Red special is its tonal warmth. James Nicoll noted that without Murderbot's personality, the setting would be "unrelentingly grim"—but the novella isn't grim at all. It suggests that even a traumatized, anxious, socially-awkward artificial consciousness can find family, trust, and hope. The ending is bittersweet: Murderbot leaves, but it's because it has choices now, and it might come back. That's a hopeful ending.

**Media Consumption as Comfort:** Murderbot's love of soap operas is more than comic relief—it represents how fiction provides emotional experiences that reality sometimes can't. It's a quiet meditation on why stories matter, especially to those who feel alienated from direct human interaction.
