## The Pitch

Weltraumschiff 1 startet... (Spaceship Number 1 Starts) is a remarkable German short film from 1940, directed by Anton Kutter. This pioneering science fiction short depicts the first manned flight to the Moon, chronicling Spaceship-1's historic journey commanded by Commodore Hardt, the inventor of a powerful rocket fuel called "Neotan-X." 

Produced during a remarkable period in German cinema history, the film combines scientific enthusiasm with narrative storytelling, presenting a vision of space exploration that was decades ahead of its time. The production involved filming before television crews and thousands of spectators at the actual launch event, adding an unprecedented documentary quality to this fictional narrative.

## The Truth

The film follows Commodore Hardt as he prepares and executes the first-ever lunar mission using his revolutionary Neotan-X rocket fuel. The story emphasizes the scientific and technical aspects of space travel, presenting the expedition as a triumph of human ingenuity and engineering prowess.

Much of the narrative unfolds through narration delivered by "scientists" and press conferences, giving the film an educational quality that was intended to inspire young viewers toward careers in science. The plot focuses on the technical achievement rather than interpersonal conflict—the mission itself is the story.

The special effects, particularly the sequences showing the spacecraft flying above the Moon's surface, were considered fantastic for their time. The film was assembled from footage of two canceled productions that were halted due to the outbreak of World War II, with additional archival footage incorporated into the final cut. This origins story contributes to the film's fragmented narrative but also explains its remarkable special effects work.

## The Core

**Pioneering Science Fiction:** Weltraumschiff 1 startet... represents an early example of German science fiction cinema's engagement with space travel narratives. At a time when actual spaceflight remained decades away, Kutter's film imagined the technical and emotional dimensions of cosmic exploration with surprising prescience.

**Technical Visualization:** The film's emphasis on the science behind space travel—rocket fuel, launch procedures, lunar surface conditions—demonstrates a commitment to plausibility that distinguishes it from pure fantasy. This approach reflects the German tradition of scientific filmmaking and educational cinema.

**Historical Context:** The film's production history is intertwined with the turbulent era surrounding World War II. Its assembly from abandoned projects gives it an unusual legacy in science fiction history, representing both the ambitions of pre-war German cinema and the disruptions of the war years.

**Visual Innovation:** Despite its narrative simplicity, the film is notable for its special effects work depicting space travel, which remains impressive decades later. The Moon landing sequences showcase creative techniques that influenced later science fiction filmmaking.

Weltraumschiff 1 startet... stands as a fascinating artifact of early science fiction cinema, representing German filmmakers' engagement with space exploration narratives during a pivotal historical moment.
