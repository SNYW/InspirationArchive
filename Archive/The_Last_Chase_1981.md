# The Last Chase (1981)

**Medium:** Film

**Creator/Author:** Martyn Burke (Director/Writer)

## The Pitch

In 2011 America, two decades after a devastating viral pandemic wiped out much of the population and collapsed democracy, a totalitarian regime has outlawed all private vehicles—from cars to boats to aircraft—claiming imminent fossil fuel depletion. Former race car driver Franklyn Hart, forced to publicly endorse public transit while privately despising the oppressive regime, has hidden his vintage Porsche 917 in his basement for years. When his secret is discovered, Hart teams up with a young electronics whiz and makes a desperate cross-country dash to "Free California," which has seceded from the totalitarian United States. Pursued by a government F-86 fighter jet and monitored by an omnipresent surveillance system, Hart's rebellious drive becomes a symbol of resistance against a police state that traded liberty for control. This obscure 1981 Canadian-American production is a prescient dystopian drama that predates many later works exploring themes of autocracy, surveillance, and the fight for personal autonomy.

## The Truth

The film is set in 2011, twenty years after a catastrophic viral pandemic devastated the United States, killing a substantial percentage of the population and causing societal collapse. In the chaos that followed, a totalitarian cabal seized power, moving the government to Boston and banning all private ownership of automobiles, boats, and aircraft on the pretext of imminent oil depletion. The regime maintains control through a pervasive mass surveillance system that monitors citizens' every move.

The protagonist, Franklyn Hart, is a former professional race car driver who lost his family to the pandemic. Publicly, he works as a spokesperson for the state-run mass transit system, forcefully promoting public transportation while secretly harboring deep contempt for the oppressive regime. Years earlier, when vehicles were being confiscated, Hart secretly stored his prized orange Porsche 917 CAN-AM roadster in a secret compartment beneath his basement. Over the ensuing years, he gradually restored the car by scavenging parts from abandoned junkyards.

When young electronics prodigy Ring McCarthy discovers Hart's plan to drive to Free California—an independent territory that seceded from the totalitarian U.S.—Hart reluctantly agrees to bring him along. Their escape triggers the government's response, led by the sinister Hawkins, who represents the Gestapo-like enforcement arm of the regime. Hawkins dispatches retired Air Force pilot J.G. Williams in a Korean War-era F-86 Sabre jet to track down and destroy Hart and his car.

The film follows Hart and McCarthy's perilous journey across the American Southwest, complicated by the need to find fuel from residual underground storage tanks at abandoned gas stations. Their adventure captures the public's imagination, inspiring calls for a return to democracy and personal freedom—thoughts suppressed for two decades.

Williams, initially ordered to destroy Hart, gradually becomes sympathetic to the rebels' cause. After several failed attempts to kill Hart (which he deliberately botches), Williams establishes a radio dialog with McCarthy using Morse code. However, Hawkins monitors the communications and activates a Cold War-era laser cannon ahead of Hart's route.

Williams attempts to warn Hart by releasing his external fuel tanks to create an inferno that might stop the car short of the cannon's range—but Hart, assuming yet another betrayal, drives through. Williams then makes the ultimate sacrifice, crashing his jet into the laser installation to destroy it. Hart and McCarthy reach California, where they are welcomed as heroes, their defiance having reignited the spirit of freedom across the nation.

## The Core

**The Automobile as Symbol of Freedom:** The Last Chase uses the car—not just as transportation but as a potent symbol of individual liberty, personal choice, and self-determination. By outlawing vehicles, the regime isn't merely restricting movement; it's asserting control over the fundamental human desire for autonomy. Hart's Porsche represents not just speed or nostalgia, but the inherent right to move freely and live independently of state control.

**Prescient Dystopian Vision:** Released in 1981, the film anticipated themes that would become central to later dystopian fiction—surveillance states, corporate/governmental overreach, and the erosion of civil liberties. The film's vision of a pandemic triggering authoritarian collapse feels remarkably prescient in hindsight, reflecting anxieties about societal vulnerability that have only intensified.

**The Individual vs. The System:** Hart is initially a compromised figure—publicly promoting the regime's ideology while privately rejecting it. His journey represents the transformation from quiet resistance to open defiance. Meanwhile, Williams represents the internal conflict within the apparatus of oppression itself—a man forced to enforce unjust laws who ultimately chooses conscience over command.

**Fossil Fuel Anxiety:** The film's premise—that oil depletion would lead to authoritarian control—reflects late 1970s anxieties about energy crisis and resource scarcity. While the "official" reason for the vehicle ban is resource preservation, the film reveals it as a pretext for power consolidation, suggesting that crisis is often exploited to expand state control.

**Cult Film Legacy:** Despite critical dismissal upon release (Leonard Maltin called it "a flimsy drama that reeks of Reaganomics"), The Last Chase has developed a modest cult following, particularly after being featured on Mystery Science Theater 3000. Its themes resonate increasingly in an era of climate debate, surveillance concerns, and debates about government overreach—making this obscure relic worth revisiting for fans of dystopian science fiction seeking alternatives to more famous entries in the genre.
