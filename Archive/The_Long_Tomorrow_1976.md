# The Long Tomorrow (1976)

## The Pitch

Set in a dystopian, crime-ridden future New York City in 2031, this graphic novel follows a cynical taxicab driver named Harry Canyon who navigates the mean streets in his disintegrator-equipped cab. When a red-haired woman fleeing gangsters seeks his help, Harry finds himself tangled in a deadly game involving the Loc-Nar—a green crystalline sphere that contains "the sum of all evils." Written by Dan O'Bannon and illustrated by Moebius, this was one of the first true cyberpunk stories, a pioneering work that influenced the visual designs of Blade Runner, Alien, Tron, and The Abyss. The story is a love letter to film noir and hardboiled crime fiction, transplanted into a bleak science fiction future.

## The Truth

Harry Canyon is a world-weary taxi driver in New York City, 2031—a metropolis plagued by crime and corruption. Speaking in the classic hardboiled detective cadence, Harry narrates his daily grind: dodging fare-skippers and would-be robbers, using the disintegrator ray installed in his back seat to dispatch the occasional fool who tries to take him for a ride.

One evening, Harry stumbles into a violent confrontation between gangsters and a young red-haired woman. He rescues her from the gangster Rudnick, who murdered her father. She reveals that her father discovered an artifact called the Loc-Nar—a green, crystalline sphere that contains unimaginable power—and she's been hunted ever since by those who want it.

Harry takes her to his apartment, where they become intimate. She proposes a scheme: sell the Loc-Nar to Rudnick and split the money. At the exchange, when Rudnick attempts to take the artifact, the Loc-Nar disintegrates him. But the woman double-crosses Harry as well, trying to keep all the money for herself and pulling a gun. Harry is faster, using his disintegrator on her.

Harry keeps the money, sums up the adventure as "a two-day ride with one hell of a tip," and drives off into the neon-lit night—the Loc-Nar's corruptive influence claiming another victim, though one far from innocent.

The original comic was serialized in two segments in the French magazine Métal Hurlant in 1976, later reprinted in Moebius Book Four and published in America by Heavy Metal magazine. It was loosely adapted as the "Harry Canyon" segment of the 1981 animated anthology film Heavy Metal.

## The Core

The Long Tomorrow is remarkable for being one of the first works to fully embody what would later be called cyberpunk—a term that wouldn't be coined until the early 1980s. Yet here, in 1975-76, O'Bannon and Moebius created the visual and thematic template: rain-soaked neon cities, technology as both tool and corruption, morally ambiguous protagonists navigating a future where the line between human and machine blurs.

The story is fundamentally about the corrupting nature of power. The Loc-Nar represents absolute evil—not as a metaphysical force, but as an amplifier of human greed and ambition. Everyone who encounters it, from the father who discovered it to the gangsters who pursue it to Harry himself, becomes corrupted. Even "winning" in this world means becoming complicit in violence.

Yet O'Bannon and Moebius tell this dark tale with a wry, satirical edge. The protagonist is a mercenary who doesn't care about justice—he just wants to get paid. The ending offers no redemption, no transformation—Harry is simply richer and slightly more dead inside. This cynical, noir-inflected view of the future was radical for its time and proved endlessly influential.

William Gibson himself acknowledged that the artwork in Heavy Metal (including The Long Tomorrow) shaped how Neuromancer "looked" on the page. The visual language of cyberpunk—mega-corporations, rain-slicked streets, holographic advertisements, noir anti-heroes—can trace its lineage directly to this unassuming graphic novel.
