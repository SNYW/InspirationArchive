# The Midas Plague (1954)

**Tags:** 🌍 USA | 🎭 Satirical | 📚 Dystopia | 🧬 Poverty

**Medium:** Novella / Short Novel
**Creator/Author:** Frederik Pohl

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a future where technology has solved scarcity, society has inverted. The poor are burdened with endless consumption—they must buy, use, and discard constantly to keep the economy alive. The rich, paradoxically, live simply, able to afford the luxury of restraint. When Morey Fry, a young man from the "consumer" class, accidentally discovers he can't stand any more consumption, he faces a choice: be "cured" of his aberration or hide his unacceptable preference for simplicity. This satirical novella inverts the typical sci-fi scarcity narrative into a darkly funny exploration of abundance, class, and the absurdities of economic consumption.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The story takes place on an Earth where unlimited atomic energy has made material goods free and abundant. The economy no longer runs on production but consumption—people must constantly buy, use, and throw away products to keep society functioning. This has created an inverted class system: the lower classes are forced to live in mansions, wear expensive clothes, eat lavish meals, and constantly upgrade their possessions, while the wealthy can afford to live modestly.

Morey Fry works as a "consumer" at the bottom of this inverted society. He's exhausted from the constant pressure to consume more and more. One day, he discovers that he's developed an aversion to all this consumption—he simply can't face another lavish meal, another new suit, another mansion. His body rejects the endless abundance.

Fearing he'll be sent to "reconditioning" (a terrifying mind-altering treatment that forces people to enjoy consumption), Morey hides his condition. He meets a girl named Gila, who seems to share his malaise but is more openly rebellious. Together, they navigate this strange world of forced abundance, trying to find a way to live simply without being "cured."

The story culminates in Morey and Gila making a desperate choice about their future in a world that punishes simplicity as a disease.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Pohl's novella is a sharp satirical inversion of traditional scarcity-focused sci-fi. Rather than imagining a future where resources run out, he imagines a world where they've become infinite—and the consequences are absurdly inverted. The story critiques:
- **Consumer culture**: The relentless drive to consume is shown as a burden rather than a privilege
- **Economic assumptions**: Pohl questions whether endless growth and consumption are actually desirable or even sustainable
- **Class and privilege**: The rich have the freedom to be frugal; the poor are forced into excess—a biting commentary on how "luxuries" become burdens when mandatory

The humor is dark but prescient—the story anticipates concerns about consumerist excess, planned obsolescence, and the environmental implications of unlimited consumption that wouldn't become mainstream until decades later. It's a thinking person's comedy that rewards those who enjoy seeing conventional assumptions turned inside out.
