## The Pitch

Metropolis is a pioneering Japanese manga by Osamu Tezuka, published in 1949 and considered the second installment in what is regarded as Tezuka's early science fiction trilogy (alongside Lost World from 1948 and Nextworld from 1951). The story is set in a futuristic city and follows the aftermath of a scientist's creation of an artificial human being. While the manga shares its title with Fritz Lang's iconic 1927 film, Tezuka has stated he had only seen a single still image from the film in a magazine at the time of creation—making this a completely independent work that merely shares thematic DNA with Lang's masterpiece. The manga was later adapted into a feature-length anime film in 2001, produced by Madhouse Studios with notable creators like Katsuhiro Otomo involved.

## The Truth

In the summer of 19XX, Dr. Yorkshire Note that dinosaurs once flourished and became extinct when they advanced beyond their ability to adapt. Humans, Earth's current dominant life form, had one special asset – intelligence. Dr. Bell wondered if humans might one day advance beyond the point of no return and render themselves extinct.

Meanwhile, a secret society known as "The Red Party" has infiltrated the International Scientists Conference in Metropolis. Their leader, Duke Red, orders his henchman to follow Dr. Charles Lawton, who has been conducting experiments into synthetic cells. When sunspot activity irradiates Lawton's tank of synthetic proteins, it brings them to life. Duke Red orders Lawton to create an entire human body out of synthetic tissue, with a face modeled after a marble statue.

Fearing his creation would be used for evil, Lawton brings the artificial human to life and destroys his lab, hiding the truth from Duke Red. He raises the child in secret, naming him Michi. When Michi accidentally renders his "father" unconscious and goes out to play, he becomes involved in stopping a runaway truck and saving a flower-selling girl named Emmy.

The story unfolds as Michi discovers his extraordinary powers—including flight, super strength, and gills for underwater breathing—while being hunted by the Red Party who want to use him for their scheme to take over the world. Michi's journey leads to confrontations with the Red Party, giant mutated animals caused by sunspot radiation, and ultimately a dramatic showdown that explores what it means to be human.

## The Core

Metropolis represents a foundational moment in the history of Japanese manga and anime. As Osamu Tezuka's second major science fiction work, it established many themes and visual storytelling techniques that would become hallmarks of his career and the manga industry as a whole.

The manga explores profound questions about humanity, creation, and technological advancement. Dr. Bell's opening meditation on extinction sets the thematic tone: what happens when humans—or their creations—advance beyond their ability to adapt? Michi, the artificial human, serves as a mirror for humanity itself, representing both the pinnacle of creation and the potential for destruction.

The story also reflects Tezuka's innovative use of his "Star System"—where characters recur across different works. Characters like Duke Red and Notarlin made their debut in Metropolis and would appear in later Tezuka works. Most significantly, Michi became the prototype for both Astro Boy and Princess Knight, arguably Tezuka's most famous creations.

Despite being created in post-war Japan with limited resources, Metropolis demonstrates Tezuka's ambitious approach to science fiction, combining adventure, mystery, and speculative science in a way that was innovative for its time. The manga's themes of artificial intelligence, government conspiracy, and the ethics of creation prefigure many science fiction concerns that would become central to the genre in decades to come.
