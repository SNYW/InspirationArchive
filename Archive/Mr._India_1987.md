## The Pitch

Mr. India is a 1987 Hindi-language science fiction superhero film directed by Shekhar Kapur and produced by Boney Kapoor. The film stars Anil Kapoor as Arun Verma, a humble violinist and philanthropist who inherits a revolutionary cloaking device created by his late scientist father. When Arun discovers that the device can make its user invisible, he uses it to fight the evil criminal Mogambo, who plans to conquer India. The film also stars Sridevi as Seema Sahni, a journalist who becomes Arun's love interest, and Amrish Puri as the memorable villain Mogambo. Released on May 29, 1987, Mr. India became a major commercial success and is now regarded as a landmark in Indian cinema for introducing the superhero genre to Bollywood.

## The Truth

The story follows Arun Verma, a street violinist who runs a home for orphaned children in an old house in India. Struggling with debt, Arun decides to rent out rooms in his home to make ends meet. He meets Seema Sahni, a local journalist, who becomes his first tenant and eventually his love interest.

One day, Arun learns from Professor Sinha, a family friend, that his late father was a renowned scientist who created a revolutionary cloaking device capable of making anyone invisible. In a flashback, it is revealed that Mogambo, a powerful criminal with aspirations of conquering India, was responsible for killing Arun's father to obtain the device. Professor Sinha is soon targeted by Mogambo's henchmen, but Arun manages to obtain the device before they can capture him.

Using the cloaking device, Arun becomes a masked superhero, invisibly fighting crime and disrupting Mogambo's operations across India. His catchphrase becomes "Mr. India" - the invisible man protecting the nation. Mogambo, frustrated by this unseen adversary, resorts to increasingly desperate measures, eventually threatening to blow up an entire city unless Mr. India reveals himself.

The climax involves a confrontation where Arun must choose between remaining invisible to protect himself or revealing his identity to save the city. He ultimately defeats Mogambo by exposing his operations to the public and bringing him to justice. The film ends with Arun and Seema together, and India safe from Mogambo's tyranny.

## The Core

Mr. India holds significant importance in Indian cinema history for several reasons. It was one of the first Bollywood films to explore the superhero genre, predating the wave of superhero films that would dominate Indian cinema decades later. The concept of an ordinary citizen becoming a superhero using advanced technology was revolutionary for its time in Indian filmmaking.

The film also marked the last collaboration between the legendary screenwriting duo Salim-Javed, who were responsible for many classic Bollywood films. Their screenplay combined science fiction elements with classic Bollywood entertainment - action, romance, comedy, and drama - creating a unique blend that appealed to mainstream audiences while introducing innovative themes.

Mogambo remains one of the most iconic Bollywood villains, with Amrish Puri's performance becoming legendary. His catchphrase "Mogambo khush hua" (Mogambo is happy) became deeply embedded in Indian pop culture. The character's ambitions of world conquest through technological superiority provided a compelling antagonist for the film's themes of technology serving good versus evil.

The film's special effects, created by veteran cameraman Peter Pereira using pre-digital techniques including masking and traditional in-camera tricks, were considered groundbreaking for Indian cinema in 1987. The practical effects used to show an invisible character moving objects and fighting villains were particularly innovative for their time.

Mr. India's success established Shekhar Kapur as a major director and paved the way for future Indian science fiction and superhero films. Its influence can be seen in later Bollywood films that attempted to combine genre filmmaking with Indian storytelling sensibilities. The film also demonstrated that Indian cinema could successfully incorporate science fiction elements into mainstream commercial filmmaking.
