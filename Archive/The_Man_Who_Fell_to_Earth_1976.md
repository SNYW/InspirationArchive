## The Pitch

Thomas Jerome Newton is an extraterrestrial who crash-lands on Earth in New Mexico, appearing as a humanoid Englishman. He comes from a distant planet suffering from catastrophic drought, and his mission is to find a way to ship water back home. Using advanced technology from his world, Newton patents countless inventions and amasses tremendous wealth as the head of a technology conglomerate. But as he becomes entangled in human society—forming a relationship with a lonely hotel worker named Mary-Lou—he finds himself trapped by human vices, corruption, and his own growing addiction to alcohol and television. David Bowie delivers a mesmerizing, alien performance in this surreal meditation on isolation, capitalism, and the impossibility of going home.

## The Truth

Thomas Jerome Newton arrives on Earth from a dying planet, using his advanced technology to quickly acquire wealth and patents as the head of World Enterprises Corporation. His goal is to finance a spaceship capable of carrying water back to his homeworld. While in New Mexico, he meets Mary-Lou, a young woman from Oklahoma working part-time jobs at a hotel. She introduces him to human customs—church, alcohol, sex—and they move in together near where he first landed.

Dr. Nathan Bryce, a former professor who had affairs with his students, becomes Newton's confidant as a fuel technician at World Enterprises. Bryce suspects Newton's alien nature and secretly photographs him with a special X-ray camera, revealing his extraterrestrial physiology. When Newton discovers Bryce knows his secret, he reveals his true form to Mary-Lou, who flees in horror.

Newton completes his spaceship for its maiden voyage, but just before takeoff, he is seized by the government and a rival company. His business partner Farnsworth is murdered. Newton is held captive in a luxury apartment within a hotel, sedated with alcohol and subjected to medical tests. Years pass. Mary-Lou returns, now older and ravaged by alcohol. They spend their time drinking and playing table tennis. She declares she no longer loves him; he agrees.

Eventually Newton escapes his derelict prison, but he cannot return home. A despondent, alcoholic Newton creates a radio recording with alien messages intended for his home planet. Bryce, now married to Mary-Lou, buys a copy of the album and meets Newton at a restaurant. Newton remains young and wealthy, but has fallen deeply into depression and alcoholism. He asks about Mary-Lou before collapsing in a drunken stupor—forever changed, forever trapped on Earth.

## The Core

Nicolas Roeg's film is a haunting exploration of alienation and the corrupting nature of human systems. Newton comes as a savior to his dying world, armed with technology far beyond human understanding, yet he is destroyed not by any external threat but by human vice itself. The film critiques capitalism through Newton's transformation from noble savior to corrupted corporate mogul—his mission to save his planet becomes subverted by the very systems of wealth and power he must exploit to achieve it.

The film also explores the impossibility of true connection across difference. Despite forming genuine relationships with Mary-Lou and Bryce, Newton remains fundamentally alone, his alien nature making authentic human connection impossible. His addiction to alcohol and television—consuming multiple screens at once—represents both his alienation and his slow assimilation into human decay.

Visually, Roeg's surreal, non-linear editing creates a disorienting experience that mirrors Newton's fractured perception. The film raises profound questions about what it means to be human, what we sacrifice in pursuit of progress, and whether salvation is possible when one's mission conflicts with the corrupt systems required to accomplish it.
