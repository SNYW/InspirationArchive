## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

The Science of Fictions is a 2019 Indonesian drama-speculative film written, directed, produced, and edited by Yosep Anggi Noen. Set in 1960s Indonesia, the film tells the story of Siman, a quiet man who discovers a foreign film crew shooting a fake Moon landing in a desolate part of his country. After witnessing this deception, his tongue is cut out—a traumatic incident that leaves him mute. Siman then loses himself in dreams of becoming an astronaut, hismuteness and strange, slow-motion gait making him a target for scorn and exploitation in his village. The film premiered at the Locarno Film Festival in Switzerland in August 2019 and had its Indonesian theatrical release in December 2020. Veteran theater actor Gunawan Maryanto won the Citra Award for Best Actor and Maya Award for Best Actor for his portrayal of Siman. The film was nominated for the Golden Leopard at Locarno and received multiple nominations at the 40th Citra Awards.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film is set in 1960s Indonesia during the height of space race enthusiasm when the Moon landing captured the world's imagination. The story centers on Siman, a simple, quiet man living in a rural village near Parangkusumo Beach in Yogyakarta—a landscape that resembles the Moon's surface.

One day, Siman stumbles upon a foreign film crew secretly filming a fake Moon landing in the desert-like landscape near his village. This traumatic discovery leads to his tongue being cut out, presumably to prevent him from revealing what he witnessed. The brutal act leaves him mute and profoundly traumatized.

After this incident, Siman becomes obsessed with space and astronauts. Despite his muteness and the strange, slow-motion gait he develops (possibly as a result of trauma), he dreams of becoming an astronaut himself—perhaps as a way to finally reach the real Moon that was faked in front of him. His muteness and peculiar manner make him an outcast in his village, where he faces scorn and exploitation from others.

Parallel to Siman's story, the film also features a colorful soldier in the village who parodies Suharto, the future Indonesian dictator—adding a socio-political dimension to the narrative. This element connects the personal story of trauma and obsession with the broader political context of Indonesia during this period.

The film explores themes of memory, trauma, national identity, and the power of dreams in the face of crushing reality. Siman's journey is both a literal and metaphorical exploration of reaching for something beyond one's circumstances, even when those circumstances include profound physical and psychological trauma.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Trauma and Memory:** The film explores how traumatic experiences shape and reshape an individual's identity. Siman's cutting out of his tongue represents not just physical mutilation but the silencing of truth and the lasting psychological impact of witnessing something that challenges one's understanding of reality.

**Fake News and Historical Deception:** The Science of Fictions can be read as a socio-political parable for the age of fake news. The fake Moon landing represents how those in power can manipulate reality and construct false narratives. Siman's subsequent obsession with becoming an astronaut suggests a desire to reach the "truth" that was stolen from him.

**National Identity and Post-Colonialism:** The film is set against the backdrop of Indonesia's political evolution, with the soldier paroding Suharto connecting personal trauma to national history. The foreign crew filming a fake Moon landing on Indonesian soil can be read as a metaphor for neocolonial exploitation and the manipulation of developing nations.

**Dreams as Escape:** Siman's dream of becoming an astronaut represents both a literal desire and a metaphorical escape from his traumatic circumstances. The film suggests how dreams can sustain us even when reality offers only scorn and exploitation.

**The Politics of Observation:** Those who witness uncomfortable truths are often silenced or marginalized. Siman's fate after discovering the fake landing speaks to the danger of seeing too much in a world where powerful interests control narrative.

The film received mixed reviews—some critics praised its ambitious thematic scope and visual poetry, while others found it pretentious and overly abstract. Regardless, it represents a unique voice in Indonesian cinema, blending surrealist imagery with socio-political commentary in ways that challenge conventional narrative filmmaking.
