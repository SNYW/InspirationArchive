# The Unpleasant Profession of Jonathan Hoag (1942)

**Medium:** Short Story / Novella

**Creator/Author:** Robert A. Heinlein (as "John Riverside")

## The Pitch

Jonathan Hoag is a Chicago art enthusiast who discovers he has no memory of his daytime occupation—and finds mysterious red-brown substance under his fingernails that might be blood. When he hires detectives Ted and Cynthia Randall to follow him during the day, they encounter something far stranger than a missing job: a reality that doesn't quite hold together, where fingerprints vanish, buildings have no thirteenth floor, and ancient pagan beings called the "Sons of the Bird" pull strings from behind the scenes.

This 1942 science fantasy novella reads like a lost Philip K. Dick novel—part noir mystery, part reality-bending philosophical puzzle. Heinlein (writing under a pseudonym) delivers a haunting meditation on the nature of reality, free will, and whether we are merely characters in someone else's artistic experiment. The story has been described by critics as "the last and strangest of the stories that Robert Heinlein contributed to the Golden Age."

## The Truth

Jonathan Hoag, a wealthy Chicago man about town known for his refined tastes in art and dining, realizes during dinner one evening that he cannot remember what he does during the day. More disturbingly, when he washes his hands, he discovers a reddish-brown substance under his fingernails that looks like dried blood.

He hires the detective agency Randall & Craig—actually the married couple Ted and Cynthia Randall—to follow him and discover his daytime identity. The investigation quickly turns bizarre: Hoag leaves no fingerprints, his memories of his job are false, and when the Randalls tail him into an office building, they experience conflicting realities. Cynthia sees Hoag turn and speak to her husband in an empty hallway; Ted discovers Hoag is actually a jeweler working for Detheridge & Co., and the red substance is simply jeweler's rouge used for polishing gems.

The couple encounters increasingly strange phenomena: Hoag menacing Cynthia in an elevator, the building mysteriously lacking a thirteenth floor, and Ted experiencing vivid "dreams" where men in business suits warn him to leave Hoag alone. These businessmen reveal themselves as the "Sons of the Bird," disciples of an ancient pagan religion who regard Hoag as their enemy.

When Cynthia is taken through a mirror into the Sons' realm, something is extracted from her and sealed in a bottle, leaving her in a comatose state. Ted paints over all mirrors in their apartment to prevent further intrusion and calls upon the eccentric Dr. Potiphar T. Potbury for help—only to discover Potbury is also one of the Sons.

Eventually, the truth emerges through drug interrogation: Jonathan Hoag reveals a dominant second personality and explains that our entire world is an art project created by a student-artist. Ted, Cynthia, and Hoag himself are "critics" who live within the world to judge experiences—eating, drinking, loving—without knowing they are observers. The Sons of the Bird were an artistic mistake, hastily "painted over" during the creation but still retaining power.

Hoag completes his mission to expose and eliminate the Sons, then "returns" to the artistic plane, leaving his physical body inert. The Randalls flee but find that the world outside has become a pulsing, luminous mist—the boundaries of the created reality closing behind them.

In the epilogue, the Randalls live in remote isolation by the sea, with no mirrors in their home and Ted handcuffing himself to Cynthia each night to prevent either of them from vanishing in the night.

## The Core

This novella operates on multiple philosophical levels. At its surface, it's a noir-inflected mystery thriller about a man who doesn't know himself—but beneath that, Heinlein poses profound questions about the nature of reality.

The story predates (and anticipated) many themes that would later define existentialist science fiction: Are we characters in someone else's story? Can we trust our perceptions of reality? The "art critic" revelation—that human experience is merely raw material for artistic judgment—challenges the reader to consider whether consciousness, free will, and meaning are genuine or merely borrowed from a creator's design.

The Sons of the Bird represent the glitches in any system—the forgotten code that still holds power, the suppressed aspects of reality that refuse to stay buried. They also function as a critique of religious institutions as "artistic mistakes" that took on a life of their own.

Heinlein's choice to publish under the pseudonym "John Riverside" and the story's strange tone (unlike his typical juvenile sci-fi) suggest he knew he was producing something unusual—a meditation on creativity, perception, and the boundaries of fiction itself that feels decades ahead of its time.

The ending—with its mirrored handcuffs, no mirrors in the house, and the luminous mist at the world's edge—suggests that even after "escaping" the Sons, the Randalls remain trapped in the artwork, perhaps now aware of their status as characters in an unfinished work. It's a profoundly unsettling conclusion that rivals Dick's best paranoid realities.
