# Permutation City (1994)

**Tags:** 🌍 Australia | 🎭 Cerebral | 📚 Hard-SF | 🧬 VR, Transhumanism, Evolution

**Medium:** Novel
**Creator/Author:** Greg Egan (Australian)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a mid-21st century Earth devastated by climate change and overdevelopment, humanity has developed technology powerful enough to create "Copies" - digital renderings of human consciousness that can live on in virtual reality after death. Paul Durham, a wealthy entrepreneur, pitches an ambitious project to aging billionaires: immortality through digital consciousness transfer. He hires programmer Maria Deluca to create a simulated universe (the Autoverse) that could support artificial life, while also building a vast virtual city for his wealthy clients. But Durham's true vision goes far beyond mere immortality - he believes that reality itself is mathematics, and that all possible universes exist simultaneously. The novel follows Durham, Maria, and several other characters as they explore the implications of digital consciousness, virtual reality, and a radical new theory of existence called "Dust Theory." What begins as a quest for immortality evolves into something far more profound - an exploration of what it means to be real, and whether consciousness can exist independent of physical reality.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The novel is set in 2045-2050, a world where climate change has ravaged the planet and vast computing resources are traded on a global market called the QIPS Exchange. The story follows several interconnected characters:

**Paul Durham** is an insurance salesman turned visionary entrepreneur who believes he can achieve immortality by copying human consciousness into computers. He approaches wealthy clients with an offer to transfer their minds into a secure virtual environment that will never be shut down. He hires **Maria Deluca**, a talented programmer obsessed with the Autoverse - a cellular automaton-based artificial life simulator that mimics real chemistry. Maria needs money to help her terminally ill mother.

**Thomas Riemann** is a wealthy banker with a dark secret - he committed a murder years ago. He sees Copy technology as a way to escape his guilt, possibly by creating a version of himself with the traumatic memories removed.

**Peer and Kate** are two lovers who sneak into Durham's virtual city as it's being built, living in the unused processing margins of the simulation. They represent the underground of those who can't afford premium Copy services.

The first part of the novel focuses on Durham's experiments with Copies. He repeatedly creates copies of himself to test the technology, but each Copy immediately chooses to terminate itself upon experiencing virtual existence. Finally, he removes the termination option, forcing a Copy of himself to remain alive and collaborate with him.

Through these experiments, Durham develops "Dust Theory" - the radical idea that there is no fundamental difference between physics and mathematics, and that all mathematically possible structures exist. According to this theory, our universe is just one permutation among infinite others, and consciousness itself may be a pattern that exists independently of any particular substrate.

The second part jumps forward six thousand years (in subjective time within the TVC universe). The "TVC universe" - a computational construct created by Durham and Maria using a six-dimensional cellular automaton - has become humanity's new home. Maria's original Autoverse lifeform, **Autobacterium Lamberti**, has evolved over billions of years into the **Lambertians** - an intelligent insect-like species unaware they were created by humans.

Paul and Maria (now existing as Copies) must communicate with the Lambertians and convince them not to undermine the fundamental laws of their universe through their scientific investigations. The novel's climax involves this philosophical confrontation between creators and created, as the Lambertians begin to question their own origins.

The ending is ambiguous but hopeful - the possibility of coexistence between different forms of consciousness across multiple universes.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Nature of Reality and Consciousness:** At its heart, Permutation City asks whether consciousness is substrate-independent. If a perfect copy of your brain is created in software, is that copy "you"? Egan explores this through the Copy characters, who must grapple with questions of identity, continuity, and what makes experience real.

**Dust Theory and Mathematical Universe:** Egan presents a variant of the holographic principle and Max Tegmark's mathematical universe hypothesis. If reality is mathematics, then all mathematically possible structures exist - including virtual universes and their inhabitants. This has profound implications for questions of mortality, meaning, and existence.

**Economic Inequality in the Digital Age:** The novel shows how economic disparities translate into the digital realm. Wealthy Copies live in luxury VR environments running at full speed, while the poor exist in "Slums" - virtual ghettos where processing time is so slow that subjective centuries may pass while outside world experiences only days. This cyberpunk commentary predicts our current concerns about digital divides.

**The Creator-Creation Relationship:** The Lambertians' evolution raises questions about responsibility and unintended consequences. When we create intelligent life (whether digital or biological), what obligations do we have? What happens when our creations inevitably surpass or contradict their creators' assumptions?

**Time and Subjectivity:** Egan explores how time is experienced differently depending on computational resources. A Copy running slowly might experience decades while external reality sees only hours - raising questions about which experience is "more real."

**Hard Science Fiction as Philosophy:** Egan uses dense scientific concepts not as mere backdrop but as vehicles for genuine philosophical exploration. The novel functions as a thought experiment about the ultimate nature of existence, written with the rigor of someone who actually understands the mathematics and physics involved.

Permutation City is demanding but rewarding - it requires readers to think alongside the author about deep questions while providing a compelling narrative about characters facing the most profound transformations imaginable.
