# The Metabarons (1992)

## The Pitch

The Metabarons is a French science fiction graphic novel series chronicling the blood-soaked legacy of a dynasty of perfect warriors across five generations. Set in a universe where technology has evolved into something approaching divinity, each Metabaron must undergo brutal initiation rituals—being mutilated by their father and implanted with powerful mechanical enhancements—before eventually battling their own parent to the death to claim their inheritance. This space opera draws heavily from Greek tragedy and Frank Herbert's Dune, exploring themes of cycles of violence, the corruption of power, and whether any humanity can survive when perfection is achieved through systematic dehumanization.

## The Truth

The saga unfolds through the origin story of the Castaka family, beginning with Dayal—the last fertile male survivor of a civil war on planet Ahour-The-Dwarf. After his planet is destroyed by the Technopriests, Dayal trains his twin daughters Myrtha and Narda as warriors. When tragedy strikes and both sisters face terminal illness, their father merges them into a single being—setting in motion the genetic experiment that will create the perfect warrior bloodline.

The first true Metabaron, Othon von Salza, rises to power through extreme violence, killing 100,000 pirate vessels to earn his title. Yet his greatest act is transforming himself into the first true Metabaron—replacing body parts with cybernetic implants and developing "metabaronic" weapons. The tradition is established: each father must mutilate his son to test their endurance, then gift them a mechanical replacement. The son must eventually kill their father to inherit the title.

Each generation continues this pattern:
- **Aghnar** inherits but becomes a messiah to a primatoid tribe before claiming his birthright
- **Steelhead** receives a cybernetic head to replace his destroyed skull
- **Aghora** is raised as an androgynous warrior, merging their own brain cells with a clone to produce the final heir

The series culminates with the Nameless Metabaron—the final, most powerful iteration—who appears in Jodorowsky's earlier work The Incal. A frame narrative features the android Tonto recounting this entire bloody history to another android, revealing that even the most "perfect" warrior dynasty is ultimately trapped in cycles of self-destruction.

## The Core

The Metabarons serves as a dark meditation on the cost of perfection. Jodorowsky argues that the pursuit of ultimate power necessarily destroys what makes us human—each generation sacrifices more of their humanity, their connections, their emotions, until the final Metabaron is a weapon so pure he's become hollow.

The series explores the corruption inherent in power structures through the lens of family. The Metabarons aren't evil—they're trapped. The Bushitaka code (named after bushido) demands total victory at any cost, including family bonds. Yet this "perfection" leaves each warrior isolated, unable to connect, ultimately instruments of destruction rather than beings capable of love.

Visually, Juan Giménez's artwork creates a cosmic cathedral of rust and flesh—machines that look organic, warriors who look mechanical, planets that seem to breathe. The art captures both the grandeur and the grotesque nature of this universe where biotechnology has advanced beyond recognition.

The Metabarons also functions as a prequel and complement to The Incal, providing context for the mercenary warrior who appears in that earlier work. Together, the two series form Jodorowsky's unified cosmology of the "Jodoverse," exploring how individual choices ripple across generations—and whether breaking cycles of violence is possible.
