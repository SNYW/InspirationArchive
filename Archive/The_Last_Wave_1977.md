# The Last Wave (1977)

**Tags:** 🌍 Australia | 🎭 Philosophical, Surreal | 📚 Science-Fantasy | 🧬 Religion, Colonialism, Identity

**Medium:** Film
**Raw Score:** 3.45/5
**Total Ratings:** 5000

**Creator/Author:** Peter Weir (Director), Tony Morphett, Petru Popescu (Screenplay)

## The Pitch

A white solicitor in Sydney takes on a murder case involving Aboriginal defendants—and discovers he shares a mystical connection with them. As unprecedented weather anomalies ravage Australia, David Burton begins experiencing vivid prophetic dreams of drowning and apocalypse. The line between rational reality and Aboriginal Dreamtime blurs until he can no longer distinguish premonition from truth. This is a haunting meditation on colonialism, indigenous wisdom, and the fragility of Western rationalism.

## The Truth

David Burton (Richard Chamberlain), a corporate tax lawyer, is assigned to defend four Aboriginal men accused of murdering another Aboriginal man in a Sydney pub incident. Though initially reluctant—the case is far outside his expertise—he becomes obsessed with the case and begins experiencing vivid dreams involving running water and drowned corpses.

His dreams intensify after he meets Chris Lee (David Gulpilil), one of the accused, who reveals that Burton is "Mulkurul"—descended from spirits who came from the rising sun. The strange weather phenomena (unusual rainfall, hail, frog infestations) intensify, and only the Aboriginal people recognize their cosmological significance.

Burton discovers the murder was likely a tribal execution, and his dreams foretell an apocalyptic flood. He persuades his family to leave the city before a catastrophic flooding disaster strikes. During the chaos, Lee escapes prison and leads Burton through subterranean tunnels beneath Sydney to a sacred Aboriginal ritual site.

In an ancient chamber, Burton finds a painting depicting European explorers arriving from South America and a prophecy of catastrophic oceanic disaster. He discovers a corpse wearing medieval Western garments and a stone mask that bears his own face. After struggling with the tribe's shaman, Burton kills him with a stone relic and escapes through the sewers, collapsing on a beach. The film ends with Burton staring at the horizon as a massive surreal wave looms—leaving ambiguous whether this is prophecy, hallucination, or reality.

## The Core

The Last Wave explores the collision between Western rationalism and Indigenous cosmology. David Burton represents the modern, logical Western mind—his profession is corporate taxation, not criminal defense, yet he's drawn inexorably into a world where dreams have meaning and time is cyclical rather than linear.

The film interrogates colonialism not through violence but through epistemological erasure—the Aboriginal people possess ancient knowledge about the world that the settler society dismisses as superstition. Burton's journey forces him to confront the possibility that his entire framework for understanding reality is incomplete.

Weir poses the question: "What if someone with a very pragmatic approach to life experienced a premonition?" The answer is existential crisis. The film suggests that dismissing non-Western knowledge systems as primitive leaves us vulnerable to forces we cannot comprehend.

The apocalyptic imagery—the unusual weather, the prophesied flood, the towering final wave—can be read as environmental anxiety (1977 was during growing environmental consciousness) or as spiritual reckoning. The ambiguity is deliberate: Burton never fully "resolves" his transformation. The final image leaves us suspended between reality and prophecy.

Technically, the film is a masterpiece of the Australian New Wave, with Russell Boyd's cinematography creating an atmosphere of mounting dread. The soundtrack blends traditional Aboriginal instruments with orchestral elements, further emphasizing the fusion of worlds.
