## The Pitch

In the平行世界 of the Obscure Cities, the Brussels-like metropolis of Brüsel faces a terrifying threat: urban renewal. Flower shop owner Constant Abeels awakens one day to find himself at the center of a bureaucratic nightmare—the city has marked his block for demolition to make way for a grand development project. As he fights to save his shop and neighborhood, Abeels discovers that Brüsel itself may hold secrets that connect it to other cities in this mysterious alternate world—secrets that certain powerful forces will kill to protect.

This is steampunk at its most elegant: a visually breathtaking exploration of architecture, nostalgia, and the battle between progress and preservation. Schuiten's intricate artwork renders Brussels in stunning Art Nouveau detail, while Peeters weaves a tale that's part mystery, part political satire, and wholly enchanting.

## The Truth

Constant Abeels runs a modest flower shop in the heart of Brüsel, a city caught in the grip of aggressive modernization. The city government, driven by mysterious higher powers, has announced a massive redevelopment plan that will demolish entire neighborhoods to make way for gleaming new structures. Abeels's block is slated for destruction.

As Abeels fights the bureaucratic machinery—filling out endless forms, attending hearings, seeking allies—he becomes increasingly entangled in a web of conspiracy. He discovers that the redevelopment isn't just about profit or progress: certain factions want to destroy old buildings because they may serve as "Obscure Passages" to other dimensions within the Obscure Cities universe.

Abeels encounters a colorful cast of characters: urban planners with hidden agendas, elderly residents who remember the old Brüsel, and mysterious figures who seem to know more about the city's secret connections than they're letting on. His simple fight to save his flower shop becomes a larger battle for the soul of the city.

The story reaches a climax as Abeels must choose between accepting compensation and moving away, or staying to fight for a city that may be more than it appears. Without giving away the ending, Brüsel ultimately explores whether some things are worth saving even when the whole world says they're obsolete.

## The Core

Brüsel is a masterful exploration of several interconnected themes:

**Nostalgia vs. Progress**: The comic directly addresses the tension between preserving historic architecture and embracing modernization. Brüsel's redevelopment mirrors real-world "Bruxellisation"—the controversial demolition of Brussels's Art Nouveau buildings. Schuiten, whose family was personally affected by these decisions, channels his childhood memories into a passionate argument for preservation.

**The Bureaucracy of Destruction**: Peeters presents urban renewal as a faceless, soul-crushing machine. The bureaucratic processes are rendered with Kafkaesque absurdity—endless forms, incomprehensible regulations, and the sense that real decisions are made somewhere else, by someone else.

**Hidden Dimensions**: As part of the Obscure Cities series, Brüsel maintains the mystery that certain buildings serve as portals to other worlds. This adds a metaphysical layer: perhaps the old buildings aren't just historically valuable, but cosmically significant.

**The Little Man vs. The System**: Abeels is an ordinary person thrust into extraordinary circumstances. His struggle represents the universal fight against faceless systems—whether urban renewal, corporate consolidation, or political machinery.

Visually, Brüsel is a feast for the eyes. Schuiten's detailed architectural renderings evoke both historical Brussels and a fantastical alternative city. The artwork deserves slow, careful examination—every panel contains meticulously drawn details that reward close inspection.
