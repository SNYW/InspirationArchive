# The Time Machine (1978)

**Medium:** Film

**Creator/Author:** Directed by Henning Schellerup | Based on the novel by H.G. Wells

## The Pitch

In 1970s America, Dr. Neil Perry is a brilliant scientist working for theMega Corporation, a defense contractor. When he completes his time machine prototype, his corporate overlords demand he shift focus to developing an "anti-matter bomb" - a weapon that could devastate the world. Using his creation to travel through time, Perry becomes a reluctant prophet, witnessing humanity's self-destruction and the evolution of a future divided between the gentle Eloi and the monstrous Morlocks. This 1978 TV movie offers a grimly prescient warning about corporate militarism and technological apocalypse, reimagining Wells' classic for a Cold War audience increasingly anxious about nuclear annihilation and corporate power.

## The Truth

Dr. Neil Perry works for theMega Corporation, a powerful defense contractor. His genius has secured a $20 million grant to build a time machine, but when the project nears completion, his superiors order him to abandon it in favor of developing an antimatter bomb - a weapon of mass destruction. 

When the power module completes earlier than expected, Perry uses his machine to travel through time. His first journey takes him to 1692 Salem, Massachusetts, where he's accused of witchcraft and barely escapes being burned at the stake. He then detours to 1871 during the California Gold Rush, where he's mistaken for a robber and arrested.

Returning to his own time, Perry is disturbed by reports of environmental destruction caused by Mega's weapons. He travels to the future to gather proof, witnessing the fiery apocalypse that consumes civilization in 2025. Humanity retreats underground, eventually evolving into two species: the Eloi, gentle surface-dwellers, and the Morlocks, subterranean monsters who farm the Eloi for food.

In the far future, Perry befriends Weena, an Eloi, and her companion Ariel. Together with other Eloi, they use plastic explosives from a museum to seal the Morlocks' tunnels, freeing the Eloi from predation. Perry returns to his own time with evidence of the catastrophe to come.

Back in the present, Perry presents his findings to the Mega board. While his colleague Branly believes him, executives Haverson and Worthington are only interested in weaponizing time travel for military advantage. Disgusted, Perry, Branly, and secretary Agnes leave Mega Corporation. Perry chooses to return to the future - to Weena and the freed Eloi - rather than remain in a world careening toward destruction.

## The Core

The 1978 Time Machine is a product of its era - a cautionary tale about the military-industrial complex, environmental destruction, and the unintended consequences of technological advancement. The film updates Wells' Victorian warning about class division for the nuclear age, replacing the industrial worker/capitalist dynamic with a corporate/military apparatus that enables humanity's ultimate self-destruction.

The Morlocks represent humanity's potential devolution - those who retreated underground, who embraced darkness and predation. The Eloi represent the survivors, gentle and innocent but vulnerable. This reflects anxieties about nuclear war and environmental collapse that defined late 1970s discourse. Perry's journey is ultimately futile in preventing the catastrophe, but he can help the survivors in the aftermath.

The film also critiques corporate power and militarism, presenting Mega Corporation as an amoral entity willing to destroy the world for profit. The antimatter bomb project mirrors real-world concerns about nuclear proliferation and the military-industrial complex that Eisenhower warned about in his farewell address.

What makes this version distinctive is its TV movie aesthetic - low-budget and earnest, with practical effects that give the Morlocks and Eloi an unsettling, almost surreal quality. It's a forgotten artifact of 1970s futurism, less polished than George Pal's 1960 version but more directly political in its critique of American corporate and military power.
