# The Final Programme (1973)

**Tags:** 🌍 UK | 🎭 Surreal, Philosophical | 📚 Science-Fantasy, Post-Apocalypse | 🧬 Identity, Transhumanism, Religion

**Medium:** Film

**Creator/Author:** Directed by Robert Fuest, based on the novel by Michael Moorcock

## The Pitch

In a surreal post-apocalyptic Britain, playboy physicist Jerry Cornelius navigates a world of collapsed governments, eccentric villains, and existential threats to recover his father's secret microfilm. The film follows Jerry's chaotic journey through a society in decay, leading to an underground laboratory where scientists attempt to create the perfect being through the "Final Programme"—a design for self-replication that promises to transcend humanity itself. Part countercultural odyssey, part philosophical meditation on identity, this 1973 British oddity remains a fascinating artifact of early 1970s speculative fiction.

## The Truth

The story opens in Lapland at the funeral of Jerry Cornelius's father, a Nobel Prize-winning scientist who developed the "Final Programme"—a blueprint for creating a perfect, self-replicating human being. Back in post-apocalyptic Britain, scientists led by the enigmatic Miss Brunner (who has a habit of consuming her lovers) seek to obtain the microfilm containing the programme. Jerry's evil brother Frank has imprisoned their sister Catherine and holds the microfilm.

Jerry launches an assault on Frank's heavily fortified house, battling through lethal traps including a poison gas chamber and a deadly chessboard. During the confrontation, Jerry accidentally kills his sister Catherine. After defeating Frank, Jerry and Miss Brunner recover the microfilm and retreat to Brunner's hidden base in Lapland.

There, the scientists attempt to execute the Final Programme, which requires merging two individuals into a single hermaphroditic being. Miss Brunner chooses Jerry over the intended subject, Dmitri. During the process, solar radiation causes the two subjects to blur together. The resulting creature—a hunched, pre-modern hominid with Jerry's consciousness—emerges from the chamber. The being observes that while it may not be a Messiah, its creation marks the end of one age and the beginning of another. The film ends with the creature stepping outside and declaring, "It is a very tasty world."

## The Core

The Final Programme explores themes of identity, transformation, and the boundaries of humanity. Moorcock's source novel was intended as an alternative retelling of his Elric of Melniboné saga, with Jerry as the chaotic anti-hero and Miss Brunner as the Stormbringer equivalent—a force of transformation that consumes and remakes.

The film reflects early 1970s counterculture anxieties about societal collapse and technological transcendence. The Vatican no longer exists, Amsterdam has been destroyed, and Trafalgar Square lies buried under wrecked cars—yet the characters continue their absurd quests with dark humor. The Final Programme asks whether humanity can evolve beyond its current form, or whether transcendence means the destruction of individual identity.

The merging of Jerry and Brunner represents a radical reimagining of selfhood—the protagonist doesn't die but transforms into something fundamentally other. The creature's ambiguous final statement ("It is a tasty world") suggests both appetite and acceptance of a new reality. This is not salvation but evolution into an unknown and possibly incomprehensible new form of existence.
