# My 20th Century (1989)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

My 20th Century (Az én XX. századom) is a 1989 Hungarian comedy-drama science fiction film written and directed by Ildikó Enyedi. The film premiered at the Toronto Festival of Festivals and won the Golden Camera (Caméra d'Or) award at the 1989 Cannes Film Festival—a remarkable achievement for a debut feature. It was selected as the Hungarian entry for the Best Foreign Language Film at the 62nd Academy Awards and chosen as part of the New Budapest Twelve, a list of the twelve best Hungarian films.

Set in Budapest in 1880, the film follows twin sisters Dóra and Lili, who are separated at birth after their mother dies and two men each take a girl. The narrative then jumps to New Year's Eve 1900, where the twins' divergent paths converge and diverge across turn-of-the-century Europe—a time of electric light, flight, the telephone, movies, radio, and the birth of modern technology. The film is a "whimsical erotic fantasia of central Europe, a millennial meditation on modernity," rendered in striking black-and-white and infused with the spirit of early cinema.

Critics have called it "a true masterpiece of 'domesticated sci-fi'" and "one of the great debuts in film history, as formally daring, visually inventive, and thematically complex as Citizen Kane." On Rotten Tomatoes, it holds a 100% approval rating. The film explores the twin origins of cinema and modernity—both born of electricity—while examining the paradoxes of technological progress.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens in Budapest in 1880, where twin sisters Dóra and Lili are born to a poor mother who dies in childbirth. After their mother's death, the twins support themselves by selling matches on the street. One night, they fall asleep on the job, and two men take their matches. After a coin flip, each man takes a girl—Dóra and Lili are separated and go on to live completely different lives.

On New Year's Eve 1900, the film jumps forward to follow both sisters. Dóra, now a drifter and con artist, finds herself aboard the Orient Express trying to scam two men out of money in Austria. Meanwhile, Lili has become a revolutionary and boards the same train in Austria, where she is briefly seen by Dóra—though Dóra, drunk, instantly forgets her identical twin.

At a library, Lili encounters Z, a man who cannot stop staring at her. Z falls deeply in love with Lili, but she remains focused on her revolutionary politics, carrying a bomb she plans to use to assassinate the minister of the interior. Even when her plot fails, she remains consumed by her mission.

Later, Z encounters Dóra on a ship, but believing her to be Lili, he gives her the number of his cabin. Dóra robs him, and the two later have sex—a case of mistaken identity with profound consequences.

When Z and Lili meet again, he takes her to his apartment. Lili, who had previously rejected Z sexually, apologizes and tells him she regrets her decision. Believing she is apologizing for robbing him (not understanding she is a different twin), Z takes her to his apartment and they have sex.

The following evening, Lili attacks the minister with her bomb, but after looking into his eyes, she cannot go through with the assassination. She blows out the bomb and runs away. Seeking refuge from a crowd of police, she hides in a fun house, where—turning a corner—she sees Dóra. Z finds his way there as well and briefly sees both twins together before they run away from him.

The film also features appearances by historical figures: Péter Andorai appears as Thomas Edison, and Paulus Manker portrays Otto Weininger—a real-life philosopher who appears as a character in the film.

The narrative weaves together themes of synchronicity, identity, and the collision of personal destiny with historical forces. The twins represent two possible paths for the twentieth century—one of technological triumph and one of revolutionary passion. The film's structure mirrors the birth of cinema itself, using techniques that pay homage to silent films while meditation on the promises and perils of modernity.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Birth of Cinema and Modernity:** The film is fundamentally about the twin births of the twentieth century: cinema and modern technological society. Both emerged from electricity, and Enyedi treats this convergence with a sense of wonder and magic. The opening sequence—featuring the first filmed images of a pigeon in flight—establishes cinema as a technology that captured movement, change, and the fleeting moments of modern life. The twins themselves can be read as two versions of modernity: one embracing technological progress, the other revolutionary political change.

**Technology as Promise and Peril:** The film presents technology as ambivalent—a source of wonder, magic, and utopian possibility, but also of danger and dehumanization. Enyedi stated in interviews that she made the film to explore "the dreams and possibilities that existed in the 19th century and came true in the 20th." Yet she also warns that "our age has become overtechnical and soulless. Science has lost its moral basis." The inventions of electric light, flight, the telephone, movies, radio, and microchips are now taken for granted, but the film reminds us of the sense of wonder they once inspired—and the dangers they also pose.

**Identity and the Double:** The twin structure allows Enyedi to explore questions of identity, fate, and choice. The twins are separated at birth and live completely different lives, yet they keep encountering the same people, places, and situations. The film asks whether identity is determined by birth and circumstance or by choice and chance. The men who take the twins make their decision with a coin flip—the most arbitrary of methods—establishing early that fate itself may be random.

**Critique of Male Scientific Triumph:** Critics have noted that the film offers "a pointed critique of man's arrogance through his pursuit for truth and his assumed infallibility of science in its infantile state." The film's male characters—Z, Thomas Edison, Otto Weininger—are all defined by their pursuit of knowledge, power, or status. The twins, by contrast, represent female biological capability and emotional connection. The film suggests that the twentieth century's embrace of science and technology came at the cost of human connection and wonder.

**Synchronicity and Missed Connections:** The film is structured around encounters that almost happen—twins who see each other but don't recognize themselves, lovers who meet by mistake, revolutionaries whose bombs fail to explode. This creates a "mischievous fairy tale of synchronicity and fin-de-siècle wonder," where the patterns of life emerge not from grand design but from random coincidence. The fun house finale—where mirrors multiply the twins into infinite reflections—embodies this theme: reality is a hall of distorted mirrors, and identity is always fragmented.

**The Lost Romance of Pre-Technology:** The film concludes with a pigeon—the same pigeon from the film's opening, which was one of the first things ever filmed. This ending "conveys the lost romance of pre-technological communication" and "recalls the failure of modern technology to realize its utopian potential and achieve genuine global and social connection." The pigeon represents a time before instant communication, when messages traveled slowly and carried weight.

My 20th Century remains significant as an early example of female-directed science fiction cinema, a formally daring debut that invented new ways to tell stories about time, technology, and identity. Its celebration of wonder and its warning about soulless technology resonate in an age of artificial intelligence and digital overload—reminding us that the choices we make about technology define what the twenty-first century will become.
