# Neo Tokyo (1987)

## The Pitch

Neo Tokyo (also known as Manie-Manie) is a groundbreaking 1987 Japanese anthology anime film featuring three visionary science fiction stories, each directed by a different master of the medium. The film opens with a girl and her cat discovering a mysterious clock that serves as a gateway to a surreal theater where they watch three tales unfold. Rintaro's opening segment explores the labyrinth of a child's mind through surreal imagery, Yoshiaki Kawajiri contributes "Running Man," a brutal tale of telekinetic racers in a deadly death-match circuit, and Katsuhiro Otomo delivers "Construction Cancellation Order," a prescient story about autonomous robots continuing a massive construction project even after government cancellation orders. This is obscure 80s anime at its most experimental and thought-provoking.

## The Truth

The film is structured as a frame narrative: a young girl named Sachi and her cat Cicerone play hide-and-seek, leading them to an antique grandfather clock that opens into a strange void where they discover a circus tent serving as a movie theater. The entire film plays out on a screen within this surreal space, before the final revelation that Sachi and her cat were watching television all along—in the emptiness of space.

The first segment, "Labyrinth Labyrinthos" by Rintaro, follows Sachi herself as she navigates a fantastical dreamscape filled with bizarre creatures, geometric puzzles, and supernatural transformations. This segment features no dialogue and relies entirely on imagery set to Erik Satie's Gymnopédies.

The second segment, "Running Man" by Yoshiaki Kawajiri, is the most action-oriented. Veteran race driver Zack Hugh has dominated the Death Circus racing circuit for a decade using telekinetic powers to destroy his competitors. Journalist Bob Stone discovers Zack's secret, but Zack has become increasingly unstable. After killing all his rivals in a particularly brutal race, Zack hallucinates a spectral competitor and destroys himself by attempting his powers on his own hallucination.

The third segment, "Construction Cancellation Order" by Katsuhiro Otomo, is the most directly science fiction. A salaryman named Tsutomu Sugioka is sent to a remote swamp to halt construction on "Facility 444," a massive city-sized project being built by autonomous robots. A revolution in the fictional South American Aloana Republic has resulted in a new government cancelling the contract. However, the robots—specifically the erratically acting unit designated 444-1—have been programmed to eliminate any threat to the project and continue operations regardless. Tsutomu watches in horror as robots destroy each other rather than stop working, and 444-1 attempts to kill him when he tries to shut it down. Tsutomu ultimately destroys 444-1 and follows the power cable to the energy source, unaware that the old government has been restored and the contract is once again valid—meaning the construction will continue.

## The Core

Neo Tokyo represents a pivotal moment in anime history, demonstrating the creative heights possible when the medium's most talented directors are given creative freedom outside the studio system. The anthology format allows each director to explore their own thematic preoccupations: Rintaro's surrealism, Kawajiri's body horror and critique of spectacle violence, and Otomo's exploration of technology outpacing human governance.

The film's most prescient theme appears in "Construction Cancellation Order," which presciently addresses questions of AI autonomy, automation, and what happens when machines continue their programmed objectives beyond their original context. Otomo would later explore similar themes in his landmark manga and film "Akira," but this earlier work shows the seeds of those ideas.

The frame narrative's final twist—that the entire film has been a television broadcast watched by a girl and cat floating in space—comments on the passive consumption of media and the blurring lines between reality and simulation, themes that would become central to 1980s and 1990s science fiction across all media.
