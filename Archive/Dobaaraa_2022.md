# Dobaaraa (2022)

**Medium:** Film

## The Pitch

Dobaaraa (transl. "Second Time") is a 2022 Indian Hindi-language science fiction mystery thriller directed by Anurag Kashyap, serving as an official remake of the 2018 Spanish film Mirage. The film stars Taapsee Pannu as Antara Awasthi, a woman who discovers an opportunity to save the life of a 12-year-old boy who accidentally witnessed a death during a thunderstorm 25 years ago. The key to her mission lies in connecting with the past through a television set during a similar storm in the present day.

The narrative unfolds through a complex web of parallel timelines and temporal anomalies. When Antara encounters the phenomenon, she finds herself able to communicate across time, setting off a chain of events that threatens to unravel reality itself. The film is notable for its intricate storytelling, requiring viewers to pay close attention to the constant shifts between timelines—a hallmark of Kashyap's signature style that has earned praise from critics for its bold narrative construction.

Despite receiving highly positive reviews from critics who lauded its direction, screenplay, and performances, Dobaaraa was a commercial failure at the Indian box office, collecting only around ₹6.77 crore against its ₹30 crore budget. The film opened the London Indian Film Festival 2022 before its theatrical release on August 19, 2022.

## The Truth

The story centers on Antara Awasthi (Taapsee Pannu), who in 2022 finds herself living a complicated life—estranged from her husband Vikas (Rahul Bhat) and working as a doctor. During a violent thunderstorm, she discovers that her television set has somehow connected to a signal from 1992, allowing her to communicate with a young boy named Anay Anand (Aarrian Sawant). Through a series of blurred television transmissions, Antara realizes that Anay had accidentally witnessed a murder 25 years ago—a murder that shaped the lives of everyone in the present.

The central mystery involves the death of a man named Abdul, which Anay witnessed as a child. Through her connection to the past via the television, Antara attempts to warn Anay and prevent the tragedy, believing that saving the boy will somehow improve her own circumstances in the present. However, as she manipulates the timeline, she discovers that every change creates unexpected consequences—alternate realities where her relationships, her career, and even her very identity are fundamentally altered.

The film's narrative operates on multiple temporal layers, with Antara desperately trying to piece together the puzzle of what happened in 1992 while simultaneously grappling with the realization that meddling with the past may have irreversible effects on the present. The mystery deepens as she works alongside a police officer with a mysterious past (Pavail Gulati), uncovering secrets about the original crime and its connection to her own family.

Kashyap's direction creates a disorienting but engaging atmosphere, constantly shifting between timelines in a manner that demands viewer attention. The film's climax reveals the full complexity of its time-bending premise, showing how Antara's attempts to rewrite history create a reality that is both familiar and profoundly alien—much like the original Spanish source material, but with distinctively Indian emotional beats.

## The Core

**Time Travel as Narrative Puzzle:** Dobaaraa explores the paradoxes of time travel through the lens of cause and effect, examining how attempts to alter the past inevitably reshape the present in unpredictable ways. The film engages with the "butterfly effect" concept, demonstrating that even well-intentioned interventions in historical events can lead to devastating unintended consequences. The television set serves as both a literal device and a metaphor—representing how we receive and interpret information from the past, and how media shapes our understanding of historical events.

**Isolation and Individual Agency:** Antara's journey is fundamentally one of isolation—she is the only person who remembers the original timeline, making her both uniquely powerful and profoundly alone. The film explores the psychological toll of carrying knowledge that no one else possesses, and the desperation that comes with believing one person can change fate. Her isolation is compounded by the feeling of being trapped in a reality that feels both familiar and wrong—a commentary on how trauma can make the present feel unreal.

**Memory and Identity:** The film interrogates the nature of memory and how our past experiences define our identities. When the timeline shifts, Antara finds that her relationships, her career, and even her own personality have been altered—raising questions about whether we are defined by our memories or by some deeper essence. The 25-year gap between timelines also reflects on generational memory and how past traumas echo through time, affecting people who weren't even alive when the original events occurred.

**Critical Reception and Artistic Merit:** While Dobaaraa struggled commercially, critics praised its intellectual ambition and complex storytelling. The film holds a 73% rating on Rotten Tomatoes, with reviewers highlighting Kashyap's ability to adapt the intricate original material for Indian audiences. Some critics noted that the film requires multiple viewings to fully appreciate its temporal complexity—a characteristic that places it within the tradition of cerebral science fiction that rewards engaged, attentive viewing.

**The Remake Question:** As an official remake of Mirage, Dobaaraa exists in dialogue with its Spanish predecessor. While the core narrative remains similar, Kashyap infuses the film with distinctively Indian emotional textures and cultural references. The film can be seen as exploring how universal themes of time, memory, and regret translate across cultural contexts—demonstrating that the anxiety of being unable to change one's past is a universally human experience.
