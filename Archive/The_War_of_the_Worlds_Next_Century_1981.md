# The War of the Worlds: Next Century (1981)

**Tags:** 🌍 Poland | 🎭 Satirical | 📚 Dystopia | 🧬 Rebellion

**Medium:** Film
**Raw Score:** 6.9/10
**Total Ratings:** 1400

**Creator/Author:** Piotr Szulkin (Director/Writer)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a dystopian near-future Poland, aliens from Mars arrive with ostensibly friendly intentions, but their presence quickly transforms Earth into a totalitarian police state where television serves as the primary tool of propaganda and control. The film follows Iron Idem, a news presenter for the state-controlled television network, who finds himself complicit in the alien occupation as he is forced to broadcast pro-alien messaging to the masses. When his wife is kidnapped and he witnesses the brutal repression of citizens, Idem must navigate his moral awakening and decide whether to remain a puppet of the regime or risk everything to rebel against both the alien invaders and their human collaborators.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens with the arrival of Martians on Earth, who present themselves as benevolent visitors seeking friendship with humanity. However, their presence immediately transforms the visited location into a surreal police state where television becomes the dominant tool of propaganda and social control.

The protagonist, Iron Idem (played by Roman Wilhelmi), is a news presenter for the TV program "Iron Idem's Independent News" – a program that, despite its name, is carefully controlled by his superiors. Once the aliens arrive, Idem is pressured to shift his programming to overtly support the Martian presence, becoming an unwilling mouthpiece for the occupation.

One morning, Idem wakes to find police officers and aliens ransacking his apartment. His wife is kidnapped in a body bag, and Idem is forced to collaborate further with the state apparatus, which now works alongside the Martians to encourage citizens to donate blood – a seemingly benign request that carries darker implications.

After being thrown out of his apartment, Idem wanders through the city, observing citizens who have become stupefied and apathetic in the face of repression. He eventually decides to rebel during a televised event called "TV Super Show" – a concert organized as a farewell to the Martians. During this broadcast, Idem delivers a scathing critique of the aliens and the state response to their presence.

However, Idem's rebellion is misinterpreted by the public. When he sits at an outdoor market afterward, a water supply worker recognizes him and tells him he "failed to pull it off," noting that society is content with what the government provides: "everything under a single pillar, a healthy climate, beer available." Disillusioned by this response, Idem removes his wig and gives it to the worker's child, telling the boy that tomorrow he will take Idem's place.

Later, an old man who had been trying to destroy televisions approaches Idem and confesses he plans to blow up the local TV station, asking for Idem's help due to his special access. Suspicious, Idem warns this sounds like a setup – which it is. Government workers pull Idem into a car and force him to watch as the old man is blown up. They assure Idem the man will become a martyr, memorialized in statue form.

After the Martians depart, Earth's media completely reverses its narrative. The alien visit is now depicted as an aggressive invasion, and Iron Idem is painted as the main collaborator. Idem is arrested and put on trial. In exchange for seeing his wife one last time, Idem agrees to play up his sensational television character until his execution.

Idem's former employer agrees to this arrangement, and his wife is delivered to him – dead in the same bag her captors used to kidnap her. Idem is sentenced to death by firing squad and tied to a pole while a television screen indicates the execution is being filmed for public consumption. However, when the firing squad shoots, only the version of Idem on the television screen collapses. The real Idem, still tied to the pole, remains unscathed and conscious, appearing amazed at his survival. He frees himself and leaves the television studio, stepping into an outside world obfuscated by light and mist.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

The War of the Worlds: Next Century is a razor-sharp satirical allegory that functions on multiple levels. Most obviously, it's a critique of totalitarian states and the manipulation of media for propaganda purposes – particularly pointed given it was made in Communist Poland in 1981, just before martial law was declared.

The film explores how television specifically functions as a tool of social control, creating false narratives and shaping public perception. The protagonist's journey from compliant broadcaster to reluctant rebel to scapegoated traitor illustrates the moral compromises individuals make under authoritarian regimes, and how those who collaborate – even reluctantly – become expendable when political winds shift.

Szulkin inverts H.G. Wells' original narrative: instead of alien invasion as external threat, the Martians are initially welcomed, and the true horror comes from the human collaboration and the system that enables occupation. The blood donation request echoes historical atrocities while appearing superficially benign.

The film's ambigious ending – where Idem survives his execution both literally (the bullets miss) and figuratively (he becomes a television ghost) – suggests that in a world dominated by mediated reality, the distinction between representation and reality has completely collapsed. The "real" Idem can only exist outside the frame, in the misty world beyond the television screen.

Ultimately, the film asks uncomfortable questions about complicity, resistance, and whether individual rebellion can ever truly matter when the systems of power are so thoroughly entrenched.
