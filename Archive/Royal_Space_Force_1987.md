# Royal Space Force: The Wings of Honnêamise (1987)

**Medium:** Anime Film

**Creator/Author:** Hiroyuki Yamaga (Director), Gainax (Studio)

## The Pitch

In an alternate world reminiscent of Earth during a Cold War-era standoff, a disengaged young man named Shirotsugh Lhadatt has drifted through life without purpose—failing at his dream to become a fighter pilot and finding himself in the Royal Space Force, a demoralized space program that has yet to achieve anything. Everything changes when he encounters Riquinni, a devout young woman who preaches on the streets about humanity's ancient sin of stealing fire. Inspired by her idealism and determination to make something meaningful of his existence, Lhadatt volunteers for humanity's first manned space mission—knowing the program is plagued by corruption, public apathy, and political machinations that may doom them all. This is the story of one young man's journey to space, and what it means to reach for the stars when your world has lost its way.

## The Truth

The film takes place in the Kingdom of Honnêamise, an alternate-Earth nation locked in a tense cold war with the neighboring Republic. Shirotsugh Lhadatt is a young man who has failed at becoming a fighter pilot and now serves in the Royal Space Force, the kingdom's fledgling and unsuccessful space program. The program has been plagued by failures and public indifference, with its personnel viewed as expendable misfits.

During a training accident that kills a fellow astronaut, Lhadatt befriends Riquinni Nonderaiko, a deeply religious young woman who spends her days preaching on the streets about humanity's original sin—the theft of fire from the gods. Riquinni lives with a sullen orphan named Manna in poverty, and despite the hardships she faces, she maintains her faith and optimism. She sees something groundbreaking in Lhadatt's work and inspires him to volunteer for the Space Force's final attempt to send the world's first astronaut into orbit.

As Lhadatt undergoes astronaut training, he grows as a person and develops a relationship with Riquinni. However, he also discovers the dark underbelly of the program: General Khaidenn, the Space Force commander, has been lying to the public, claiming they are building a "space warship" to generate support. The project's chief engineer is killed in an engine test accident, and controversy mounts over the expense of the project during a time of widespread poverty in the kingdom.

In a moment of despair, Lhadatt goes AWOL and stays with Riquinni. During this time, he reads the holy book she gave him, which speaks of humanity being cursed for stealing fire—the knowledge of the gods. One night, Lhadatt nearly assaults Riquinni while she is undressing, but stops himself at the last moment. Riquinni denies the incident ever happened and pushes him away.

Lhadatt returns to the program and survives an assassination attempt by the Republic. Before launching, he finally reconciles with Riquinni and befriends Manna. The launch site is positioned in a demilitarized zone, and the government hopes the rocket will provoke the Republic into attacking—using the launch as a political provocation.

Despite the Republic launching a full invasion during the countdown, Lhadatt refuses to abort the launch. He convinces the vulnerable ground crew to proceed, and the spectacular launch stun both warring nations into inaction. As his capsule reaches orbit, Lhadatt broadcasts a prayer for humanity's forgiveness. The capsule is bathed in sunlight, and a montage shows his life's journey and the history of his world. On the surface, Riquinni witnesses the first snowfall and gazes skyward.

## The Core

Royal Space Force: The Wings of Honnêamise is fundamentally a meditation on hope, purpose, and what it means to strive for something greater than oneself. The film asks whether humanity deserves to reach for the stars—or whether our nature as sinners who stole fire condemns us to destruction.

The character of Riquinni represents religious faith and idealism in a world that has become cynical and corrupt. Her belief that humanity must atone for its original sin provides the thematic backbone for Lhadatt's journey. When he finally reaches space and broadcasts his prayer for forgiveness, it suggests that the act of reaching upward—of trying to become something more than what we are—might be humanity's path to redemption.

The film also explores the intersection of science, religion, and politics. The Space Force is manipulated by a corrupt government that sees the program as a political weapon rather than a genuine achievement. The religious establishment is shown as struggling against poverty and irrelevance. And the scientists and engineers who actually build the rockets are depicted as idealists working against impossible odds.

Notably, the film rejects typical anime conventions—director Hiroyuki Yamaga deliberately avoided standard anime tropes in favor of a more realistic, grounded approach. The worldbuilding is meticulous, creating an alternate Earth with its own history, religions, and political tensions. Science fiction writer Ted Chiang has cited this film as the finest example of worldbuilding in fiction.

The film was produced by Gainax as their debut project and was famously a commercial failure at the box office, costing ¥800 million but earning only ¥347 million. However, it eventually became profitable through home video sales and gained cult status. More importantly, it established Gainax as a studio willing to take risks and push boundaries—paving the way for their later masterpieces like Neon Genesis Evangelion.
