# Royal Space Force: The Wings of Honnêamise (1987)

**Tags:** 🌍 Japan | 🎭 Cerebral | 📚 Science-Fantasy | 🧬 Religion, Exploration, Rebellion, Humanity

**Medium:** Anime
**Raw Score:** 7.5/10

**Creator/Author:** Hiroyuki Yamaga (Director), Gainax

## The Pitch

In an alternate world bearing an uncanny resemblance to Earth, a disengaged young man named Shirotsugh Lhadatt has given up on his dreams of becoming a fighter pilot and instead drifts through his life in the Royal Space Force—a fledgling space program demoralized by repeated failures. Everything changes when he meets Riquinni, a devout young woman who preaches on the streets about humanity's fallen nature and the stolen fire of knowledge. Inspired by her quiet conviction, Shirotsugh volunteers for humanity's first orbital mission, becoming the pilot of a desperate last-ditch attempt to launch a person into space. Set against a backdrop of government corruption, war, and public indifference, this is a deeply human story about finding meaning beyond oneself.

## The Truth

The film unfolds in the Kingdom of Honnêamise, an alternate Earth where technology has developed along different lines—roughly 1950s level but with distinct cultural characteristics. Shirotsugh is a young man who has already failed at his dreams, settling for a position in the Royal Space Force that seems destined for nothing. His life changes when he encounters Riquinni, a religious woman who believes humanity is cursed for having stolen fire—the knowledge of good and evil. She lives with a sullen orphan named Manna and spends her days preaching to uninterested crowds.

Riquinni's genuine faith and concern for the marginalized moves Shirotsugh in ways he cannot articulate. He begins visiting her, and she gifts him a holy book that speaks of humanity's eternal curse. Meanwhile, the Royal Space Force, led by General Khaidenn, attempts to rally public support by falsely claiming they are building a "space warship" rather than a scientific mission. The project faces numerous obstacles: an engineer dies in an accident, the public questions the expense while the nation suffers poverty, and a neighboring Republic threatens war.

Shirotsugh experiences a crisis of faith and goes AWOL, staying with Riquinni. During this time, he almost assaults her while she is undressing, but stops himself—an incident she later refuses to acknowledge. When Shirotsugh returns to the city, he survives an assassination attempt by the Republic. Before departing for the launch, he finally connects with Manna and says goodbye to Riquinni.

The launch occurs in a demilitarized zone, with the government hoping the rocket will provoke the Republic into attacking. Though Khaidenn pushes the launch forward, the Republic invades. Shirotsugh, already in the capsule, refuses to abort and convinces the ground crew to proceed. The spectacular launch shocks both nations into stillness.

As Shirotsugh orbits the planet, he broadcasts a prayer for humanity's forgiveness. His capsule enters sunlight, and a montage shows his life, his world's history, and achievements. On the surface, Riquinni witnesses the first snow fall and gazes upward.

## The Core

Royal Space Force represents a radical departure from conventional anime storytelling. Gainax's debut film deliberately rejects typical anime motifs in favor of meticulous worldbuilding and character study. The film's core themes explore the nature of meaning and purpose—whether individual action matters in a world corrupted by power, poverty, and war. Shirotsugh's journey is fundamentally about transcending his cynicism and finding the will to attempt something greater than himself, even when the odds seem impossible and the world offers no encouragement.

The film also examines faith and reason. Riquinni's religious worldview presents a challenge: if humanity is fundamentally cursed, what meaning can individual action possess? The answer the film suggests lies not in religious certainty but in the act of trying itself—the launch becomes an existential statement that humanity can transcend its limitations, regardless of whether it "deserves" to.

Visually, the film presents a distinctive alternate Earth with 1950s-level technology but unique cultural developments. The design avoids typical anime aesthetics, creating something that feels both foreign and intimately familiar. Ryuichi Sakamoto's score complements this approach, adding gravitas without melodrama.

Critically, the film failed commercially but achieved cult status. Science fiction writer Ted Chiang has cited it as the finest example of worldbuilding in fiction. Its influence on subsequent anime, particularly through the Gainax founders who would later create Neon Genesis Evangelion, remains substantial.
