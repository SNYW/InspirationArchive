# Royal Space Force: The Wings of Honnêamise (1987)

**Tags:** 🌍 Japan | 🎭 Optimistic | 📚 Space-Opera | 🧬 Exploration, Religion

**Medium:** Anime Film
**Creator/Author:** Hiroyuki Yamaga (Writer/Director)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In an alternate world resembling Earth but fractured by war between the Kingdom of Honnêamise and the neighboring Republic, a disengaged young man named Shirotsugh Lhadatt drifts through life without purpose—until he encounters Riquinni, a devout young woman who preaches about humanity's original sin of stealing fire from the gods. Inspired by her earnest faith and vision of a better world, Shirotsugh volunteers to become the first human to journey into space, despite his nation's failing space program and a corrupt government that exploits the project for propaganda. This 1987 Gainax production is less about the technical marvel of space travel and more a meditation on idealism, sacrifice, and what it means to strive for something greater than oneself. Ted Chiang has called it "the most impressive example of worldbuilding in fiction."

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film takes place in the Kingdom of Honnêamise, an alternate-Earth nation locked in cold war with the neighboring Republic. Shirotsugh Lhadatt is a young man who once dreamed of becoming a fighter pilot but now serves in the Royal Space Force—a demoralized space program that has suffered numerous failures. He lives a purposeless existence until he befriends Riquinni Nonderaiko, a religious woman who spends her days street preaching about humanity's fall from grace.

Riquinni believes humanity stole the "fire" of knowledge and is eternally cursed for this transgression. She lives with a sullen orphan named Manna and survives through her faith. When a fellow astronaut dies in training, Riquinni's inspire Shirotsugh to volunteer for the Space Force's desperate attempt to launch the world's first astronaut into orbit—a project that has been plagued by failure and public apathy.

As Shirotsugh undergoes training, he faces countless obstacles: technological difficulties, overwhelming public scrutiny, and the machinations of a corrupt government. General Khaidenn, commander of the Royal Space Force, rallies support by lying that they are building a "space warship." Shirotsugh's journey parallels his coming of age. He visits Riquinni frequently, and she gives him a holy book to read. In a dark moment, Shirotsugh almost assaults Riquinni while she undresses—he stops himself, but she denies the incident ever happened. Later, Riquinni's house is foreclosed, forcing her and Manna to live in a church-provided shack.

The government schedules the launch in a demilitarized zone, hoping the rocket will provoke the Republic into attacking and rally patriotic support. But when the launch time is pushed forward and the Republic launches an invasion, Shirotsugh must make a choice. Already in the space capsule, he convinces the vulnerable ground crew to proceed with the launch despite the chaos.

The spectacular launch stun both warring factions into inaction. As Shirotsugh's capsule orbits the planet, he broadcasts a prayer for humanity's forgiveness—echoing Riquinni's teachings about original sin. The capsule is bathed in sunlight, and a montage shows his life, his world's history, and humanity's achievements. Meanwhile, on the surface, Riquinni witnesses the first snowfall and looks up at the sky.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Royal Space Force is fundamentally about the tension between idealism and cynicism, and whether pursuing seemingly impossible dreams is worthwhile even when the world seems broken. Shirotsugh begins as a disengaged young man without purpose, and his journey to space becomes a metaphor for transcendence—both literal and spiritual.

The film explores how societies对待宏大理想 (treat grand ideals). The corrupt government weaponizes the space program for propaganda, while the public remains apathetic or hostile. Yet the act of reaching for something greater—whether it's space travel or moral perfection—has value regardless of the impure motives of those in power.

Riquinni's religious worldview provides the film's philosophical backbone. Her belief that humanity stole "fire" (knowledge) and is eternally cursed creates a fascinating context for Shirotsugh's journey. By sending a human into space—into the realm of the gods—Shirotsugh's mission becomes an attempt at reconciliation, a prayer for forgiveness rather than a triumph of human ingenuity.

The animation, though showing its age, delivers remarkable worldbuilding. The alternate history setting (implied to be a world where certain historical events diverged) feels lived-in and complex. The film's production history—its massive budget, chaotic production, and commercial failure—ironically mirrors its themes of idealistic pursuits in hostile environments.

What makes Royal Space Force remarkable is its refusal to offer easy answers. The protagonist doesn't "win" in any conventional sense. The launch succeeds, but at what cost? The war continues. The corruption persists. Yet there's something transcendent in the attempt itself—a reminder that human beings can reach for the stars even when they don't deserve to.
