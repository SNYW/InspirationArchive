# AD Police Files (1990)

**Tags:** 🌍 Japan | 🎭 Gritty | 📚 Cyberpunk, Noir | 🧬 Identity, Transhumanism, Corporate Conspiracy

**Medium:** OVA (Original Video Animation)
**Raw Score:** 3.13/5
**Total Ratings:** 8061

**Creator/Author:** Directed by Takamasa Ikegami and Akira Nishimori; Written by Shō Aikawa and Tony Takezaki; Studio: Artmic and AIC

## The Pitch

Set in Neo-Tokyo 2027, AD Police Files is a gritty cyberpunk prequel to the legendary Bubblegum Crisis series. The story follows rookie AD Police officer Leon McNichol as he navigates his early days in the Advanced Police - an elite unit specifically tasked with containing rogue Boomers (cybernetic humanoid robots). In a city where more than 70% cybernetic modification legally classifies you as a "Boomeroid" subject to lethal prejudice, Leon must question what it truly means to be human while battling corporate conspiracies, malfunctioning androids, and the dark underbelly of Mega Tokyo's obsession with mechanical augmentation.

## The Truth

The three-episode OVA opens with Leon, still in the Normal Police, killing a rampant Boomer prostitute in cold blood - a stark introduction to the violent world he's about to enter. We then jump forward to his transfer to the AD Police, where he's partnered with the tough Gina Malso.

In Episode 1, "The Phantom Woman," Leon and Gina investigate illegal Boomer recycling operations tied to an underground sex trafficking ring. Leon is stalked by a vengeful female Boomer who becomes obsessed with him after he killed her in his previous job - she haunts him hoping he'll finish the job. The episode ends with Leon questioning humanity's place in a city increasingly dominated by cybernetic beings.

Episode 2, "The Ripper," takes on a darker tone as prostitutes turn up dead on Tokyo's subway "Paradise Loop." The killer is revealed to be Caroline Evers, a billionaire CEO whose body is 80% cybernetic after she replaced her organs to win a corporate power struggle. After her husband cheats with a human prostitute, she snaps and begins killing working girls. When caught, she's subjected to the "Boomeroid" prejudice - despite being mostly human in her mind, she's killed like a machine. Leon's partner Iris Cara opts for cybernetic eye replacement, symbolically "throwing away a bit of her humanity."

Episode 3, "The Man Who Bites His Tongue," is the most emotionally devastating. Captain Billy Fanword, nearly killed in the line of duty, is transformed into an experimental battle cyborg - his brain and tongue the only original parts remaining. The twisted Dr. Manabe, who built his body, becomes obsessed with him and hooks him on a sensory stimulant drug DA-27 to make him "receptive" to her advances. When Billy discovers she plans to let him die in combat to profit from his design, he goes on a murderous rampage through AD Police headquarters. His partner Gina, his former lover, is forced to kill him at his own request - he wants her to shoot him in his tongue, the only part of his body that reminds him he's still human.

The series was famously halted after just three episodes due to a legal dispute between production company Artmic and Youmex, leaving the Bubblegum Crisis timeline permanently fragmented.

## The Core

AD Police Files is a unflinching examination of transhumanism and identity crisis in the cyberpunk genre. The series poses uncomfortable questions: At what point does technology strip us of our humanity? The "Boomeroid" law - where 70% cybernetic modification makes you legally killable - is a chilling metaphor for how society categorizes and dehumanizes those who are different.

The three episodes form a thematic trilogy. Episode 1 asks whether artificial beings can possess authentic emotions and desires. Episode 2 explores how technology can both empower and dehumanize, as Caroline Evers literally becomes a monster despite her human consciousness. Episode 3 is the most devastating - Billy's tragedy shows that even a hero's "human" spirit can be systematically destroyed by those who claim to be helping him.

The series predates and influenced later cyberpunk works like Ghost in the Shell, yet remains largely forgotten outside of die-hard anime collectors. Its dark, noir atmosphere and willingness to engage with uncomfortable philosophical questions about consciousness, identity, and what makes us human elevates it far above typical 80s OVA fare.
