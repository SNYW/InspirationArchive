# The Little Prince (1974)

## The Pitch

When a pilot stranded in the Sahara Desert encounters a young prince who has fallen to Earth from a tiny asteroid, he embarks on a philosophical journey through the cosmos. This 1974 musical adaptation of Antoine de Saint-Exupéry's beloved novella blends fantasy, musical numbers, and profound wisdom about life, love, and what truly matters. The Little Prince shares his encounters with peculiar adults on various planets—a king who believes he rules the universe, a businessman who counts stars, and a rose he loves but struggles to understand—before finally arriving on Earth where he meets a fox and a serpent who teach him the most important lessons of all.

## The Truth

The film opens with a pilot (Richard Kiley) making an emergency landing in the Sahara Desert. There he meets the Little Prince (Steven Warner), a young boy who asks him to draw a sheep. Over the following days, the pilot learns about the Little Prince's journey to Earth from Asteroid B-612, a tiny planet the size of a house.

The Little Prince first visited other planets in his cosmic journey. On the first planet, he met a King who commands the stars, who makes the Little Prince his ambassador but has no real power. On the second planet, he encountered a Vain man who craves admiration, dressing himself in ridiculous costumes to be admired. On the third planet, he found a Drunkard who drinks to forget his shame about drinking. On the fourth planet, he met a Businessman who endlessly counts stars, believing they belong to him.

The fifth planet was the smallest of all—just big enough for a lamp and its lamplighter, who lights and extinguishes the lamp each minute. This planet fascinated the Little Prince because here, a man could see the sunset forty-four times in one day. The sixth planet held a Geographer who spends his time documenting places but never explores them, advising the Little Prince that his rose is ephemeral.

Finally, the Little Prince arrived on Earth, where he encountered a snake who speaks in riddles and can send people back to where they came from with a single bite. He also met a Fox who teaches him about taming—how creating connections gives meaning to life. The Fox explains that what is essential is invisible to the eyes, visible only to the heart.

Throughout his journey, the Little Prince carries with him his beloved rose—a flower he tends to daily, whose thorns are her only protection. He worries about her because she has four thorns to protect herself against the world.

In the desert, the Little Prince and the pilot spend their final days together. The Little Prince becomes increasingly determined to return to his asteroid and his rose. He allows the snake to bite him—a transaction that will allow his body to return to the stars. The pilot is devastated, but the Little Prince explains that while his body will remain on Earth, his soul will return to his asteroid. The film ends with the pilot searching for the Little Prince in the desert, eventually giving up and flying away—but in the final scene, he hears the Little Prince's laughter in the stars.

## The Core

Beneath its whimsical surface and musical numbers, The Little Prince is a profound meditation on adulthood, innocence, and what gives life meaning. The novella (and by extension this film) critiques how adults become absorbed in "serious matters"—power, wealth, status, accumulation—while losing touch with what truly matters: love, wonder, and human connection.

The Little Prince himself represents childhood innocence and wonder, asking questions that adults have stopped asking. His journey through the planets shows how adults become trapped by their own conceits—the King who rules nothing, the Vain man who no one admires, the Businessman who owns nothing but numbers, the Drunkard who is trapped in cycles of shame. Each planet represents a different adult trap.

The rose is perhaps the film's central symbol—something the Little Prince loves but doesn't fully understand. The rose's vulnerability (her four thorns, her need for protection) mirrors human relationships: we are responsible for what we tame, and that responsibility gives our lives meaning.

The film adaptation adds musical numbers that, while dated, provide additional emotional resonance. The themes of loneliness, love, loss, and the importance of maintaining wonder into adulthood remain powerful. Saint-Exupéry's message—that we must protect our inner child and remember that what matters most is invisible to the eyes—has only grown more relevant in an age of materialism and digital distraction.

The ending is deliberately ambiguous—is the Little Prince's body just a shell? Did he truly return to his asteroid? The film suggests that death is not an ending but a transformation, and that what we love never truly leaves us.
