## The Pitch

Loss of Sensation (original Russian title: "Gibel Sensatsii" meaning "The Downfall of a Sensation") is a 1935 Soviet science fiction film directed by Alexandr Andriyevsky. Based on the 1929 Ukrainian novel "Iron Riot" by Volodimir Vladko, this early sound film explores themes of technological innovation, worker solidarity, and the dangers of automated weapons. Set in an unnamed country, the film follows engineer Jim Ripple who invents universal robots designed to help workers, only to see his creation weaponized against the very people he sought to help.

The film is notable for its place in Soviet cinema history as an early sci-fi exploration of automation and labor, predating many Western robot films. Though the film uses the abbreviation "R.U.R" for its robots, it is not directly based on Karel Čapek's famous play but rather on Vladko's novel. The story presents a cautionary tale about technological progress serving authoritarian interests rather than worker emancipation.

## The Truth

The film centers on Jim Ripple, an engineer from a worker's family who invents universal robots designed to help workers with their tasks. His theory is that cheap robot production will make all goods so inexpensive that Capitalism will collapse. However, his family and fellow workers consider him a traitor for creating technology that could replace human labor.

The key to his invention is a high-capacity capacitor that powers the robots. The government becomes interested in the invention because the robots can be used as a weapon. Ripple is given a secret factory and funding to produce robots. However, the robots are not autonomous or intelligent—they are controlled either by radio or by sound, for which Ripple uses a saxophone. When drunk, he even makes the robots dance.

When workers at a factory go on strike, the administration replaces them with robots. A workers' delegation visits to investigate and discovers the robots have replaced human workers. During an accident while Ripple demonstrates a robot, a worker dies, sparking conflict between workers and the plant administration.

The military decides to use robots against the workers as a weapon, controlled by an officer in a tank using a radio remote control. Trying to prevent hostilities, Ripple attempts to stop the robots with his saxophone, but he is unsuccessful and is killed. Ultimately, the workers gain control over the robots using their own remote control device, which they had covertly assembled by studying Ripple's prototype robot "Micron."

## The Core

**Technology and Labor:** The film presents an early Soviet perspective on automation and worker displacement. Rather than viewing robots purely as beneficial tools, the film explores how technology can be co-opted by authorities to suppress workers—reflecting anxieties about industrialization during the Great Terror era.

**The Saxophone as Symbol:** Ripple's use of a saxophone to control robots is striking—musical expression becomes a metaphor for human control over creation. His failure to save himself through music suggests the inability of art to противостоять (withstand) militarized power.

**Worker Control:** The resolution, where workers successfully take control of the robots using their own remote device, reflects Soviet ideological themes of worker empowerment. However, the deaths and conflict that precede this ending provide a darker commentary on technological advancement without collective ownership.

**Historical Context:** Made in 1935 during Stalin's Great Purge, the film's production company Mezhrabpomfilm was eventually shut down, and the film fell into obscurity for decades. Some scholars have noted subversive impulses in the film—its portrayal of workers opposing state-controlled technology resonates with critical readings of Soviet science fiction.
