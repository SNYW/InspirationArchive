# The Rat Saviour (1976)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

The Rat Saviour (Croatian: Izbavitelj) is a 1976 Yugoslav horror science-fiction film directed by Krsto Papić. Based on a novel by Soviet writer Alexander Grin, this atmospheric cult classic blends Body Snatcher paranoia with surrealist horror to create an allegory about totalitarianism, social conformity, and the nature of humanity itself.

The film was awarded best film at the 1977 International Science Fiction Film Festival in Trieste, the 1980 Paris International Festival of Fantastic and Science-Fiction Film, and 1982 Fantasporto. It was also selected as the Yugoslav entry for the Best Foreign Language Film at the 49th Academy Awards, though it was not nominated. In 1999, a poll of Croatian film critics named it one of the best Croatian films ever made.

Set in an unnamed Croatian town during an economic crisis, the film follows struggling writer Ivan Gajski who, after being evicted from his apartment and unable to sell his books, takes refuge in an abandoned bank. There he discovers a secret society of intelligent rats who can impersonate humans—and who plan to take over the world. What follows is a paranoid thriller that commentary on fascism, communism, and the insidious nature of collective conformity.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

Ivan Gajski is a poor writer struggling to make ends meet in a Croatian town experiencing severe economic hardship. Unable to sell his books on the streets and evicted from his apartment, Ivan finds himself homeless. During a protest in the city square, he meets a mysterious young woman named Sonja who shows him kindness, giving him her scarf before they become separated in the crowd.

With nowhere to go, Ivan takes shelter in an abandoned bank, accessing it through a sewer grate. The bank has been abandoned but still has electricity and food stashed in cupboards. Ivan discovers the phone still works and tries to track down Sonja. One night, he hears music coming from deeper within the building and investigates, finding what appears to be a lavish dinner party—but something is deeply wrong. The guests move strangely, and their faces seem off.

Ivan witnesses the partygoers being called before a shadowy figure: the Rat God, who intends to take over the world. These rat people thrive on poverty, disease, and decay. Ivan realizes he has stumbled upon a conspiracy of intelligent rats who can impersonate humans—and they plan to replace the population one person at a time.

Ivan escapes the bank but finds himself hunted. He teams up with Professor Martin Bošković, whose daughter Sonja has been replaced by a rat person. Together with a small group of humans who have not been replaced, Ivan attempts to develop a poison to kill the rat people while evading detection. The paranoia intensifies as the rat people increasingly infiltrate every level of civil service, and Ivan can no longer trust anyone—including people he has known his whole life.

The film culminates in a tense confrontation with the Rat God himself, leaving the ending deliberately ambiguous: is this real, or is it all a delusion superimposed on Ivan's crumbling mental state? The final images suggest that either interpretation could be true—or perhaps that the distinction no longer matters.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

The Rat Saviour operates on multiple allegorical levels, drawing comparisons to works like Invasion of the Body Snatchers, Eugene Ionesco's Rhinoceros, and Kafka's The Trial. The film can be read as a critique of any totalitarian system—whether fascist, communist, or capitalist—where individual identity is absorbed into a collective whole.

**Totalitarianism and Conformity:** The rat people represent the insidious nature of totalitarianism, creeping into society gradually until it becomes impossible to tell who is human and who has been replaced. The film asks: at what point does resistance become meaningless? When everyone around you has been "converted," are you the sane one, or simply the last to see "the truth"?

**Economic Despair as Breeding Ground for Extremism:** The setting of economic crisis and mass poverty is not incidental. The rat people specifically thrive in conditions of decay and desperation—the worse things get for ordinary people, the stronger the conspiracy grows. This can be read as a warning about how economic collapse creates fertile ground for radical movements.

**The Nature of Humanity:** The film interrogates what makes someone human. The rat people possess intelligence, organization, and even culture—yet they are portrayed as monstrous. Meanwhile, the humans are selfish, apathetic, and willing to turn on each other. The ambiguity about whether the events are "real" or psychological adds another layer: perhaps the real horror is what ordinary people are capable of when pushed to the brink.

**Paranoia and Gaslighting:** Ivan's experience mirrors the gaslighting of political dissidents under authoritarian regimes—his certainty that something is wrong makes him appear insane to everyone around him. The film validates his paranoia while also questioning whether such paranoia is ultimately corrosive to the self.

Visually, the film embraces its low budget with atmospheric black-and-white cinematography and effective use of shadow. The creature effects are modest but creepy, and the real rats add genuine chills. The sparse, unsettling score complements the claustrophobic tone.

The Rat Saviour remains a fascinating artifact of Yugoslav cinema—a film that somehow got past censors to deliver a pointed political allegory that remains relevant today. It's a must-see for fans of Eastern European horror, paranoid sci-fi, and allegorical storytelling.
