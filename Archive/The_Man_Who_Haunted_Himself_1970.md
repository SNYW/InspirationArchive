# The Man Who Haunted Himself (1970)

**Tags:** 🌍 UK | 🎭 Tense, Cerebral | 📚 Science-Fantasy | 🧬 Sentience, Memory, Isolation

**Medium:** Film

**Creator/Author:** Directed by Basil Dearden; Based on the novel "The Strange Case of Mr Pelham" by Anthony Armstrong

## The Pitch

When business executive Harold Pelham survives a near-fatal car crash, he returns home to find that people have been seeing him in places he doesn't remember being—behaving in ways completely out of character. His wife, colleagues, and friends all claim to have interacted with him at times when he was supposedly elsewhere. As Harold investigates, he becomes increasingly paranoid, convinced that a doppelganger is living his life. But the horrifying truth may be far stranger than any conspiracy: Harold may not be the original at all.

## The Truth

Harold Pelham is a successful director at Freeman, Pelham & Dawson, a marine technology company. One day, while driving home from his London office, he suddenly imagines himself in a different car and crashes horrifically. On the operating table, he briefly dies, and the monitor shows two heartbeats before he is revived.

After recovering, Harold notices odd things happening—people act strangely around him, and he finds himself accused of behavior he can't recall. His wife mentions a mysterious silver car parked outside their home. At work, he's been secretly supporting a merger he now opposes. Friends and colleagues claim they've seen him at places he never visited, doing things he has no memory of.

Harold confronts an executive from the rival company, who reminds him of three secret meetings they supposedly had—including at the London Planetarium and in a boat on The Serpentine. Someone is masquerading as him, conducting clandestine affairs and business dealings.

Desperate, Harold consults psychiatrist Dr. Harris and undergoes treatment. During his time in the clinic, the double finalizes the merger and goes on a date with his wife, culminating in them having sex together—the real Harold has been replaced in every way that matters.

When Harold calls home and hears "himself" answer the phone, he races home and confronts his double. Both insist they are the real Pelham. Their high-speed chase ends on a bridge when Harold swerves into the river. Just before impact, his image fades away.

The double survives, experiencing a brief moment of pain (the sound of a double heartbeat), then returns to calm: there is once more only one Pelham.

## The Core

The Man Who Haunted Himself is a clever variation on the classic doppelganger theme, updating the Jekyll and Hyde formula for a 1970s corporate world. The film explores themes of identity, authenticity, and the existential terror of being replaced—of wondering whether the self is truly continuous or can be duplicated, overwritten, or supplanted.

Thecorporate setting is crucial: the film suggests that identity itself has become commodified. The double isn't a supernatural evil; he's simply a more ruthless, opportunistic version of Harold who knows how to play the game of business and relationships. In a sense, the film asks whether modern professional life has already created multiple selves—which one is "real"?

The near-death experience is the mechanism that "releases" the double, suggesting that identity is fragile and contingent—something that can be split and copied. The final twist, where the original dissolves while the double survives, implies that the "self" is ultimately an illusion, and the more adaptable version wins.

The film's quiet horror lies in its intimate scale: this isn't a world-threatening conspiracy, just one man's existential crisis. By grounding the supernatural in the mundane realities of marriage and office politics, the film suggests that the horror of identity loss is always personal.
