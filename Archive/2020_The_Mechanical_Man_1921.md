## The Pitch

The Mechanical Man is a 1921 Italian silent science fiction film and one of the earliest examples of the genre produced in Italy. Directed by and starring André Deed (who created the comedic character Saltarello), it holds the distinction of being the first film to depict a battle between two robots. The story follows a scientist who develops a remote-controlled mechanical man with superhuman speed and strength, only to be killed by a criminal gang led by the dangerous Mado. When Mado uses her own mechanical man to commit crimes across the city, the scientist's brother builds a second robot to stop her—leading to an epic showdown in an opera house where the two mechanical men destroy each other.

For many decades, the film was considered lost until approximately 26 minutes of footage were discovered at the Cinemateca Brasileira in Brazil. Film historian Christopher Workman has suggested that this obscure Italian production may have influenced Fritz Lang's landmark 1927 film Metropolis. The Mechanical Man represents a fascinating early example of science fiction cinema, predating many of the genre's most famous works and showcasing Italian filmmakers' contribution to early sci-fi storytelling.

## The Truth

The film opens with Professor D'Ara (Gabriel Moreau) in his laboratory, where he has successfully created a remarkable device: a mechanical man that can be controlled remotely through a sophisticated machine. The robot possesses superhuman speed and strength, representing the pinnacle of early 20th-century scientific imagination regarding artificial beings.

However, before Professor D'Ara can protect his invention, a criminal gang led by the ruthless Mado (Valentina Frascaroli) storms the laboratory. Mado demands the secret of building the mechanical man, but when the professor refuses, she kills him. The gang captures the mechanical man and its control device, using them to commit a series of crimes including robbery and murder—all controlled remotely by Mado from her hidden lair.

Professor D'Ara's brother (also Gabriel Moreau) inherits the scientist's work and constructs a second mechanical man to combat the criminal's device. The two robots are then set against each other in a spectacular finale at an opera house. During the climactic battle, both mechanical men are destroyed in their violent confrontation, which also results in significant damage to the building itself.

Mado, frantically attempting to control her mechanical man during the battle, is electrocuted at the control panel when a short circuit occurs—bringing poetic justice to the villain. The remaining characters are left to survey the destruction of the mechanical men and the opera house.

The discovered footage represents only about 26 minutes of the original 60-80 minute film, with most of the beginning and the cast credits lost to history.

## The Core

**Early Robot Cinema:** The Mechanical Man stands as a pioneering work in the depiction of artificial beings on screen. While later films like Metropolis (1927) and R.U.R. (1923) are more famous, this Italian production was among the first to show robots battling each other—a trope that would become a staple of science fiction cinema. The film's concept of remote-controlled mechanical beings reflects early 20th-century fascination with electricity, automation, and the boundaries between human and machine.

**Influence on Metropolis:** Film historian Christopher Workman has theorized that André Deed's The Mechanical Man may have influenced Fritz Lang's Metropolis. Both films feature giant mechanical beings—Maria/robot in Metropolis and the Mechanical Man here—as central plot devices. While direct influence is difficult to prove, the Italian film's existence demonstrates that early science fiction cinema was more internationally diverse than often recognized.

**The Lost Film Phenomenon:** The Mechanical Man's journey from lost film to partially recovered artifact mirrors the fate of many silent era films. The rediscovery of footage in Brazil highlights the global nature of film preservation challenges. The surviving fragments offer scholars and enthusiasts a glimpse into early Italian science fiction filmmaking, even as much of the original work remains inaccessible.

**Science Fiction as Precursor:** The film reflects early 20th-century anxieties and aspirations around technology. The remote-controlled robot represents both the promise of technological advancement and fears about misuse of powerful machines. Mado's weaponization of the mechanical man for criminal purposes anticipates later science fiction themes about technology falling into wrong hands.

The Mechanical Man may be largely forgotten today, but it represents an important piece of science fiction cinema history—one that demonstrates the genre's international roots and the enduring appeal of artificial beings in storytelling.
