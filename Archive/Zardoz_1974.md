# Zardoz (1974)

**Tags:** 🌍 UK | 🎭 Surreal | 📚 Post-Apocalypse | 🧬 Immortality, Rebellion, Transhumanism

**Medium:** Film

**Total Ratings:** 27000

**Creator/Author:** John Boorman

## The Pitch

In a distant 2293, humanity has split into two species: the immortal "Eternals" who live in luxury within a hidden paradise called the Vortex, and the mortal "Brutals" who toil in irradiated wastelands, growing food for their masters. When Zed, a Brutal Exterminator, stows away aboard Zardoz—the flying stone god the Brutals worship—he discovers the terrible truth behind his world: he is the product of a eugenics experiment, engineered to penetrate the Vortex and destroy the stagnant eternal society. What follows is a surreal journey of revelation, revolution, and rebirth.

## The Truth

The film opens in the post-apocalyptic wasteland where Brutals live as farmers, supplying food to the invisible Eternals in exchange for protection from the Exterminators—warriors who serve Zardoz, a giant flying stone head that provides weapons in return for tribute. Zed, a curious Exterminator, hides inside Zardoz during one of its missions and temporarily "kills" its operator, Arthur Frayn.

Zed arrives in the Vortex, a hidden paradise where immortal Eternals live in indolent luxury. He is captured but eventually allowed to live among them as a subject of study. The Eternals are overseen by the Tabernacle, an artificial intelligence that grants them immortality. However, their endless lives have led to boredom, madness, and creative stagnation. Many have descended into catatonia, forming a social layer called the "Apathetics."

Through his interactions with Consuella, May, and the subversive Friend, Zed learns the truth: he is the product of long-term eugenics experiments conducted by Arthur Frayn, who controlled the outside world. Frayn bred Zed to be a "superman" who would penetrate the Vortex and force change upon the stagnant eternal society.

As Zed gains understanding of the Vortex's problems, he becomes a pawn in the Eternals' internal conflicts. Eventually, with help from May and Friend, Zed absorbs all the Eternals' knowledge and destroys the Tabernacle. This disables the force fields protecting the Vortex, allowing the Exterminators to invade. Most Eternals welcome death as a release from their meaningless existence.

Zed impregnates May during his transformation, and together with several followers, she escapes to the outside world to bear their children as mortal, enlightened beings. Consuella, who has fallen in love with Zed, gives birth to a son within the remains of the stone head. The parents age and die, leaving only handprints on the wall and Zed's revolver as testament to their existence.

## The Core

Zardoz is a scathing critique of immortality and the stagnation that comes with unlimited time. Boorman argued that humanity was "hurtling into the future so fast our emotions couldn't keep up." The Eternals represent the ultimate expression of this—they have everything but meaning. Their immortality has rendered them impotent, apathetic, and morally bankrupt.

The film explores class division and the engineered consent of oppression. The Brutals willingly serve Zardoz and even kill each other at its command, never questioning why they must sacrifice while the Eternals live in paradise. Zed's journey represents the awakening of class consciousness—he was literally designed to be the instrument of the Eternals' destruction, a biological weapon created by one of their own.

The Tabernacle, as artificial intelligence, serves as both protector and jailer. It grants immortality but at the cost of creativity and purpose. Its destruction is necessary for humanity to progress, suggesting that technological solutions to human problems often create new, worse problems.

Ultimately, the film argues for mortality as a gift. The ending—where Zed and Consuella age and die, leaving their child to continue the cycle—suggests that finite life gives meaning to existence.
