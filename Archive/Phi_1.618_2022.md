## The Pitch

Phi 1.618 is a 2022 Bulgarian-Canadian dystopian science fiction film directed by Theodore Ushev in his directorial debut. Set in a bleak future where humanity has achieved immortality through artificial creation, the film presents a world where men are genetically engineered as asexual, immortal beings—and where women have become biologically obsolete. As a mysterious poison spreads across Earth, these loveless immortals embark on a galactic colonization mission, carrying with them the barely-living body of a woman as a relic of the past. The film is based on Vladislav Todorov's novel "The Spinning Top" and explores themes of gender, reproduction, memory, and what it means to be human when humanity itself has been redesigned.

## The Truth

In a dystopian future, humanity has been fundamentally transformed. Aseхual, immortal men have been created through genetic engineering, making the female sex biologically redundant for reproduction. These immortal men live without emotion, connection, or desire—they are essentially biological machines designed for survival, not meaning.

As a deadly poison spreads across the Earth, rendering the planet uninhabitable, these loveless immortals make the decision to colonize the galaxy. They abandon their dying world but take with them a single prize: the barely alive body of a woman named Gargara, preserved as a mysterious artifact from humanity's past.

On the spaceship, the calligrapher Krypton is tasked with creating a copy of the entire legacy of the Immortals—documenting their cold, emotionless history. While working, he discovers Gargara, whose irascible personality stands in stark contrast to the passive, programmed existence of the immortal crew. Her presence challenges everything the immortals have been taught about the past and about emotion.

The narrative explores what happens when Krypton encounters this living reminder of love, passion, and biological diversity. Gargara represents everything that was lost when humanity "evolved" beyond gender, reproduction, and connection. The film culminates in a confrontation between the sterile immortals and this one remaining symbol of human feeling and mortality.

## The Core

**Transhumanism and Gender Abolition:** The film presents a provocative meditation on transhumanist ideals taken to their. By creating asexual, immortal beings, humanity has "s logical extremeolved" death and reproduction—but at the cost of everything that makes us human. The film asks whether immortality is valuable without love, connection, or the spark of mortality that gives life meaning.

**The Value of Difference:** Gargara represents the irreducible value of diversity—biological, emotional, and social. The immortals have become homogeneous, interchangeable, and utterly devoid of personality. Her "irascible" nature is presented not as a flaw but as the essence of being alive. The film suggests that conflict, emotion, and difference are not problems to be engineered away but constitute the core of human experience.

**Colonialism and Escapism:** The immortals' response to planetary death is not to fix their world but to flee and conquer new ones. This can be read as a critique of technological utopianism that treats Earth as disposable, or as a broader commentary on humanity's tendency to escape problems rather than face them.

**Memory and Legacy:** Krypton's job—copying the entire legacy of the Immortals—raises questions about what is worth preserving. Is a civilization without art, love, or passion truly worth documenting? The film suggests that legacy without emotion is meaningless.

Phi 1.618 stirred controversy when it won an award at the Moscow International Film Festival during the Russo-Ukrainian War; director Theodore Ushev made antiwar statements and refused the award. The film was also Bulgaria's shortlisted entry for consideration as their Oscar submission for Best International Feature Film at the 95th Academy Awards.
