# Invasión (1969)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Invasión (Invasion) is a 1969 Argentine science fiction mystery film directed by Hugo Santiago and written by the legendary literary duo of Jorge Luis Borges and Adolfo Bioy Casares. Widely regarded as an important cult film, its style is considered intermediate between classic cinema and the French Nouvelle Vague. The film is set in the fictional city of Aquilea (representing Buenos Aires), where a group of defenders attempts to ward off an enigmatic alien invasion that remains abstract, undefined, and ultimately unstoppable.

The film achieved significant critical acclaim, ranking 38th in a survey of the 100 greatest Argentine films conducted by the Museo del Cine in 2000, and notably climbing to the 2nd position in a 2022 survey organized by specialized Argentine film magazines. Invasión premiered at the Cannes International Film Festival during the first Directors Fortnight and won an award at the Locarno International Film Festival in 1969. The film represents a unique collaboration between Santiago and the two most celebrated Argentine writers of the 20th century, bringing Borges' characteristic themes of labyrinths, impossible quests, and inevitable defeat to the screen.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The narrative of Invasión unfolds in Aquilea, a city that stands in for Buenos Aires, as it faces an alien invasion from an unspecified enemy. The film focuses not on the nature of the invaders themselves, but on the small group of defenders who attempt to resist them.

The resistance is commanded by Don Porfirio, played by Juan Carlos Paz, who coordinates the defenders from behind the scenes, calling each member individually to give them instructions. His second-in-command is Julián Herrera, played by Lautaro Murúa, who leads the fighters on their missions. Herrera is depicted as a stoic, emotionless hero—the prototype of what critics have termed the "Borgean compadrito," a character type drawn from Borges' fiction.

Irene, played by Olga Zubarry, is Herrera's wife, but she leads a double life—a revelation that provides the film's final twist. The defenders operate largely in isolation, their efforts ignored by the general population of Aquilea, who seem indifferent or unaware of the existential threat facing their city.

As the film progresses, it becomes clear that the defenders' struggle is doomed from the outset. The invaders are numerous, their nature incomprehensible, and their victory assured. The narrative echoes the structure of the Trojan War, as described in the Iliad—the defenders know they cannot win, yet they fight anyway, finding meaning in resistance rather than victory.

The film's atmosphere is one of melancholy and fatalism. The defenders patrol the streets of Aquilea, including notable locations like the Boca Juniors stadium (La Bombonera), attempting to hold back an enemy they cannot understand or defeat. Some negative rolls of the film were lost during the Argentine dictatorship of 1976-1983, but the film was restored in France in the 1990s using surviving negatives and lower-quality positives.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Labyrinth of Inevitable Defeat:** Invasión translates Borges' literary preoccupations into cinematic form. The defenders face not merely a powerful enemy, but an incomprehensible one—its nature never explained, its origin never revealed. This mirrors Borges' famous labyrinths, where protagonists wander through spaces designed to be unfathomable. The invasion is absolute and impossible to define, representing the ultimate unknowable threat.

**The Trojan War as Metaphor:** The film explicitly references the Trojan War, positioning the defenders as modern versions of the Greeks who fought knowing their cause was doomed. Like the defenders of Troy, the fighters of Aquilea wage a battle where defeat is written from the beginning. This fatalistic framework transforms the military conflict into a meditation on human dignity in the face of inevitable destruction.

**Borges and Film Collaboration:** Invasión represents one of the rare occasions when Borges' literary vision was directly translated to the screen. Working with his longtime collaborator Adolfo Bioy Casares, Borges brought his characteristic themes—impossible quests, labyrinthine structures, defeated heroes—to the science fiction genre. The film shares thematic DNA with Borges' story "The Theme of the Traitor and the Hero," where resistance and defeat are intertwined.

**The Indifferent Masses:** One of the film's most striking themes is the population's indifference to the invasion. While a small group of defenders fights in the streets, the people of Aquilea go about their lives, unaware or unconcerned with the existential threat. This reflects a broader commentary on society's tendency to ignore those who sacrifice themselves for collective survival.

**Connection to El Eternauta:** The film's themes of alien invasion and doomed resistance connect it to the Argentine comic book El Eternauta (1957), written by Héctor Germán Oesterheld. That work, also set in Buenos Aires, features defenders who know victory is impossible against the overwhelming alien force. Both works emerged from a particular moment in Argentine culture when the threat of authoritarianism made allegorical resistance narratives particularly resonant.

**Film as Lost Memory:** The partial destruction of Invasión's negatives during the Argentine dictatorship and subsequent restoration in France adds another layer of meaning to the film. The fact that parts of the film were lost and reconstructed mirrors the film's themes of loss, memory, and the impossibility of complete understanding—much like Borges' explorations of memory and time.

Invasión remains a landmark of Argentine cinema and a unique document of literary collaboration, bringing Borges' pessimistic vision of resistance and defeat to the science fiction genre in a film that is as haunting as it is enigmatic.
