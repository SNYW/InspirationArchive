# Wizards (1977)

**Medium:** Animated Film

**Creator/Author:** Ralph Bakshi

## The Pitch

In a world devastated by nuclear war, two twin wizards are born—one representing the forces of magic, the other technology. This post-apocalyptic animated epic follows Avatar, the kind-hearted fairy wizard, as he must stop his evil brother Blackwolf from unleashing ancient propaganda technology to conquer the last peaceful kingdom. It's a psychedelic blend of fantasy, science fiction, and political allegory that predates many modern animated films.

## The Truth

Earth has been devastated by nuclear war. It takes two million years for the radioactive clouds to clear and allow sunlight to return. The surviving humans have transformed into mutants, while humanity's true ancestors—trolls, fairies, elves, and dwarves—emerge to live in the idyllic land of Montagar.

Queen Delia of the fairies gives birth to twin wizards: the kindhearted Avatar and his evil, mutated brother Blackwolf. After Delia's death, Blackwolf attempts to usurp leadership but is defeated in a magical duel by Avatar and exiled from Montagar.

Three thousand years later, Blackwolf leads the dark land of Scortch, where he and his army of mutants salvage and restore ancient technology. He discovers old Nazi propaganda films from World War II and uses them as psychological warfare to inspire his troops and terrorize the enemy.

Meanwhile, in Montagar, Avatar trains President King's daughter Elinore to become a fairy. The President is assassinated by Necron 99, a robot sent by Blackwolf. Avatar defeats the robot and renames it "Peace," reprogramming it for nonviolence.

When Blackwolf's "dream machine"—a projector playing propaganda footage—threatens Montagar, Avatar, Elinore, Peace, and the elf warrior Weehawk set out to destroy it. Their journey leads them through fairy sanctuaries, snowy mountains, and eventually to the heart of Blackwolf's domain.

In the final confrontation, Avatar discovers that Blackwolf has been using propaganda to control his armies. In a twist, Avatar uses the ultimate technological weapon—a pistol—to kill Blackwolf. The projector is destroyed, the mutants are defeated, and peace is restored. Avatar and Elinore depart to start their own kingdom, while Weehawk becomes the new ruler of Montagar.

## The Core

Wizards is Ralph Bakshi's bold departure from his gritty urban films into fantasy, and it shows his signature willingness to tackle controversial themes. The film serves as an allegory for the creation of Israel and the Holocaust, exploring how propaganda can be weaponized to control populations and justify violence.

The central theme revolves around the moral ambiguity of technology: Blackwolf's technology ultimately fails because it relies on manipulation and fear, while Avatar's "magic" (the pistol) succeeds not through supernatural power but through simple, direct action. The film suggests that both magic and technology are neutral forces—their value depends entirely on how they're wielded.

Visually, Wizards is striking. Bakshi employed rotoscoping and live-action footage integration years before his Lord of the Rings adaptation. The backgrounds, designed by Ian Miller and Mike Ploog, create distinct visual worlds: the idyllic Montagar versus the grim, industrial Scortch. The animation predates Miyazaki's similar style in The Castle of Cagliostro by two years, making this a pioneering work in Western animation.

The film also marks Mark Hamill's cinematic debut, recorded during the same period he was cast in Star Wars. This connection to the broader science fiction canon, combined with the film's unique visual style and political subtext, makes Wizards a fascinating artifact of 1970s speculative cinema.
