# The 10th Victim (1965)

## The Pitch

In a post-World War III world where violence has been channeled into a government-sponsored reality TV program called "The Big Hunt," the 10th Victim follows Caroline Meredith, a veteran hunter who is one kill away from completing her tenth and final round—and the massive wealth that comes with it. Assigned Marcello Poletti as her target, she travels to Rome to execute the perfect assassination on live television, complete with corporate sponsorships from the Ming Tea Company. But as the cat-and-mouse game unfolds, both hunter and victim fall in love, leading to a subversive twist on the survival game formula. This 1965 Italian cult classic predates The Hunger Games by nearly five decades and influenced everything from Battle Royale to Austin Powers.

## The Truth

The film opens in the year 2079, decades after World War III devastated civilization. To prevent the outbreak of a fourth global conflict, the world government has legalized violence through "The Big Hunt"—a televised competition where randomly paired contestants engage in ten rounds of hunt-and-kill, alternating between hunter and victim roles. The last person standing after ten rounds wins enormous wealth and freedom.

Caroline Meredith (Ursula Andress) is a veteran contestant who has successfully completed nine rounds—five as hunter, four as victim—and is now hunting for her tenth and final victim. If she succeeds, she will become one of the legendary "Tens" and retire wealthy. Eager to maximize her earnings and fame, she has secured a lucrative sponsorship from the Ming Tea Company and plans to make her kill a spectacular public spectacle.

Marcello Poletti (Marcello Mastroianni) is selected as her victim—a six-time killer whose winnings have been squandered by his greedy ex-wife and current mistress. With no money left and no will to survive, Marcello has become apathetic about the game.

Caroline arrives in Rome under the pretense of being a journalist studying Italian men's sexual habits. She arranges an interview with Marcello at the Temple of Venus, but he suspects her true identity. He arranges for a crocodile to attack her during a televised event, but she escapes unharmed.

Undeterred, Caroline lures Marcello to a secluded beach and drugs him. She brings him back to the Temple of Venus for the killing—but in a dramatic twist, she fires blanks, unable to go through with the murder. Marcello, now understanding her feelings, reciprocates her love.

In the final confrontation on live television, Caroline shoots Marcello, only for him to reveal he loaded her gun with blanks. He then shoots her—but she's wearing bulletproof armor. The two lovers decide to abandon the game entirely and escape together. They board a plane and decide to get married, with the pilot showering them with flowers as the film ends.

The film concludes with both contestants breaking free from the system that defined their lives, choosing love over violence—a subversive ending that producer Carlo Ponti insisted upon despite director Elio Petri's original darker vision.

## The Core

The 10th Victim is a masterwork of socio-political satire that uses science fiction conventions to critique media spectacle, violence as entertainment, and the commodification of human life. Elio Petri, working at the height of Italy's pop art movement, created a visually striking vision of 2079 that juxtaposes ancient Roman architecture with futuristic pop aesthetics.

The film is notable for being one of the first works to explore the "death game" concept that would later become a genre staple—from Battle Royale to The Hunger Games. However, Petri's approach is distinctly Italian, blending the conventions of commedia all'italiana with existential science fiction.

The film explores themes of violence as catharsis—asking whether channeling aggression into controlled contexts truly eliminates war or merely masks humanity's darker impulses beneath a veneer of civilization. The corporate sponsorship of murder (complete with product placement and advertising deals) anticipates our modern reality of monetized content and reality television exploitation.

Most significantly, the film's subversive happy ending challenges the viewer's expectations about violence, love, and freedom. Rather than the pessimistic conclusion of Sheckley's original story, Petri (forced by Ponti) created a romantic escape that suggests love can triumph over systemic violence—but only if one is willing to abandon the game entirely.
