# The World of Edena (1983)

**Medium:** Graphic Novel / Bande Dessinée

**Creator/Author:** Jean "Mœbius" Giraud

## The Pitch

When two interstellar travelers crash on a barren planet, they discover a mysterious pyramid and are transported to a world called Edena—a "Garden of Eden somewhere in another galaxy." What begins as a survival story evolves into a profound meditation on human nature, sexuality, civilization, and the boundary between dreams and reality. Mœbius, the legendary French artist behind Arzach and The Incal, created this series originally as a promotional piece for Citroën, then expanded it into a philosophical science fiction saga that explores what it truly means to be human. The artwork transitions from Mœbius's signature intricate style to the clean "ligne claire" approach, creating a visually stunning meditation on the human condition.

## The Truth

The story follows Stel and Atan, two protagonists from a highly industrialized civilization who crash their spacecraft on a barren planet. Equipped with an old Citroën, they traverse the desolate landscape until they discover a camp of interstellar refugees surrounding a mysterious pyramid. When they enter the pyramid, they are transported to Edena—a strange, dreamlike world that exists somewhere in another galaxy.

On Edena, Stel and Atan must relearn everything about being human. Cut off from their technological civilization, they are forced to consume natural food, and in the process, they rediscover their sexuality and humanity. Atan transforms into "Atana" during this journey of rediscovery. They encounter the enigmatic Master Burg, Stel's dream guide who aids them on their quest. Burg is actually revealed to be a palindrome of "Major Grubert," the protagonist of Mœbius's other work The Airtight Garage—both characters are intended to be the same person across Mœbius's universe.

The series explores profound questions through deceptively light storytelling: the nature of dreams, nutrition and health, biology and sexuality, the human desire to live in a structured society, and the concepts of archetypal good and evil. The later volumes, particularly "The Gardens of Edena," "The Goddess," "Stel," and "Sra," delve deeper into these philosophical themes while expanding the mythology of Mœbius's interconnected universe.

## The Core

The World of Edena represents Mœbius at his most philosophical and personal. Unlike his more action-oriented works like The Incal or The Airtight Garage, this series functions as a meditation on what civilization strips away from human experience. By removing his protagonists from their technological society and placing them in a primordial paradise, Mœbius explores how humans fundamentally relate to their bodies, their desires, and their need for meaning.

The series challenges the assumption that technological progress equals human advancement. Stel and Atan's transformation on Edena suggests that civilization, while providing comfort and structure, also alienates us from basic human experiences. Their rediscovered sexuality, their relationship with natural food, and their spiritual journey all point toward a critique of modern industrial society.

Mœbius was heavily influenced by his time in a commune in Tahiti and the teachings of Jean-Paul Appel-Guéry, which explains the series' focus on nutrition, health, and natural living. The artwork's transition between styles—the intricate Mœbius aesthetic and the cleaner ligne claire—mirrors the protagonists' journey between artificial civilization and natural Eden.

For worldbuilding, Edena represents the ultimate "lost world" concept—not a lost continent or hidden society, but an entire paradigm of existence that humanity has forgotten. The recurring connections to The Airtight Garage establish one of science fiction's most elaborate shared universes, suggesting that all of Mœbius's works exist in a multiverse where similar souls repeatedly encounter each other across different realities.
