# Polygon (1977)

**Medium:** Animated Short

**Creator/Author:** Anatoly Petrov (director), Sever Gansovsky (story)

## The Pitch

Polygon (also known as Proving Ground) is a 1977 Soviet animated science fiction short film directed by Anatoly Petrov and produced by the legendary Soyuzmultfilm studio. Based on a story by Sever Gansovsky, this 10-minute animated short uses a distinctive animation technique called "photographica" - featuring two celluloid layers for each character with special color schemes and deliberately out-of-focus layering to create three-dimensional depth without CGI. The film presents a dark anti-war parable set in a not-too-distant future where a grieving professor invents a mind-reading automated tank capable of detecting human hostility - with devastating consequences. The film won First Prize at the Yerevan Film Festival (1978) and was officially selected for the Oberhausen Film Festival (1979).

## The Truth

In the not-too-distant future, a professor from a major military power has lost his son - a paratrooper killed during warfare. Consumed by grief and revenge, he devotes his scientific expertise to creating an ultimate weapon: an automated tank equipped with mind-reading capabilities that can detect feelings of hostility and fear in humans. The tank's AI allows it to identify and eliminate anyone who harbors aggressive thoughts.

The professor's revenge succeeds beyond his expectations - the tank destroys several generals responsible for sending his son to die. However, the professor himself soon becomes a victim of his own creation. As he contemplates the consequences of his actions and feels guilt, fear, and anger, the tank turns on him. The film ends with an open conclusion, leaving the professor's fate ambiguous but suggesting he too has fallen victim to the weapon he created.

The animation features faces modeled on famous celebrities of the era including Ringo Starr, Jean Gabin, Paul Newman, Yul Brynner, and Mel Ferrer. The film's musical theme is an instrumental arrangement of "El Bimbo" by French euro disco group Bimbo Jet.

## The Core

**The Horror of Automated Violence:** The film presents a chilling vision of weapons technology that operates without human judgment. The mind-reading tank represents the ultimate dehumanization of warfare - a system that judges people based on their thoughts rather than their actions. The professor's invention turns soldiers into potential targets and creates a world where any expression of anger or fear becomes dangerous.

**Revenge as Self-Destruction:** The professor's quest for vengeance leads inexorably to his own destruction. The film suggests that the military mindset that created the system - the willingness to use any means necessary to achieve victory - ultimately consumes even those who wield it. His grief transforms him into exactly the kind of threat his own creation was designed to eliminate.

**The Cycle of Violence:** By creating a weapon that responds to hostility, the professor ensures that any resistance or even emotional response will be met with force. The system perpetuates endless conflict - anyone who mourns, resents, or fights back becomes a target. This creates an eternal cycle where the instrument of revenge becomes an instrument of perpetual war.

**Animation as Art Form:** The innovative photographica technique demonstrates how Soviet animators achieved remarkable visual depth and dynamism without any computer assistance. The artistic choices - including celebrity caricatures and the distinctive visual style - add layers of meaning and cultural reference that reward close analysis.
