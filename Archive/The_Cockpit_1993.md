# The Cockpit (1993)

**Tags:** 🌍 Japan | 🎭 Gritty | 📚 War | 🧬 War, Sacrifice, Memory

**Medium:** Anime
**Raw Score:** 3.65/5
**Total Ratings:** 1000

**Creator/Author:** Leiji Matsumoto (Original Manga), Yoshiaki Kawajiri, Takashi Imanishi, Ryōsuke Takahashi (Directors)

## The Pitch

A haunting three-part anthology OVA set during World War II, exploring the human cost of conflict through three interconnected stories of German and Japanese pilots facing impossible choices. Directed by a team of anime legends including Yoshiaki Kawajiri (Ninja Scroll) and Ryōsuke Takahashi (Space Battleship Yamato), The Cockpit presents a dark meditation on duty, sacrifice, and moral compromise during history's most devastating conflict. Each episode features a different story, different characters, and different setting, but all share a common thematic thread: what happens when soldiers must choose between their orders and their conscience.

## The Truth

**Episode 1: Slipstream**
Captain Erhardt Von Rheindars, a disgraced German Luftwaffe pilot, is assigned to escort a captured American B-17 Flying Fortress bomber carrying a terrifying secret cargo: a Nazi atomic bomb being transported to Peenemünde. The bomber also carries his childhood sweetheart and her scientist father. Given a prototype Ta 152 high-altitude fighter for the mission, Rheindars faces an agonizing dilemma when his sweetheart pleads with him to let enemy planes destroy the bomber before "humanity sells its soul to the Devil forever"—even if it means her own death. During the mission, after shooting down two British Spitfires, Rheindars makes his choice: he allows the final Spitfire to destroy the bomber, then flies off alone, declaring himself "the man who did not sell his soul to the Devil."

**Episode 2: Sonic Boom Squadron**
Set on August 5-6, 1945—just before the atomic bombing of Hiroshima—this episode follows Ensign Nogami, a young Japanese Ohka (kamikaze) pilot. After his Betty bomber is attacked and he ejects over the Pacific, Nogami witnesses the destruction of his squadron. He returns to base and joins a new crew for a desperate mission the following day. During the attack on an American aircraft carrier, a Reisen pilot sacrifices himself to save Nogami's bomber, allowing Nogami to launch his Ohka. He crashes into the carrier's bridge, and as the carrier begins to sink, the commander finds Nogami's photograph of his girlfriend. Then comes the news: an atomic bomb has just been dropped on Hiroshima. The film ends with the carrier exploding as the commander asks, "Are we all crazy?"

**Episode 3: Knight of the Iron Dragon**
In Leyte, Private Kodai and Private Utsunomiya attempt to reach an air base against impossible odds, driven by a promise they made to a dying comrade. Traveling by motorcycle through hostile territory, they face American soldiers and the brutal reality of a lost war. Utsunomiya is wounded but hides it from Kodai. After an encounter with an American pilot on a motorcycle (treated as a race), Kodai reaches the air base but is gunned down by American soldiers. Utsunomiya dies of his wounds nearby. The American soldier remarks that Kodai "would have won the race, but in a real race, no one shoots at you at the finish line."

## The Core

**The Human Cost of War:** The Cockpit presents war not as glorious combat or heroic sacrifice, but as a series of impossible choices that destroy the souls of those forced to make them. Each protagonist faces a moral dilemma with no good answer—follow orders and become complicit in atrocity, or refuse and face death or dishonor.

**Alternative History and Historical Memory:** By setting two episodes around the atomic bombings, the series forces viewers to confront the reality of 1945 from the perspective of those who lived through it. The timing of "Sonic Boom Squadron"—directly juxtaposing the Ohka attack with the Hiroshima bombing—creates a devastating commentary on the nature of terminal sacrifice.

**Technical Mastery:** The animation, though showing its age, demonstrates remarkable attention to period-accurate aircraft detail. The flight sequences are visceral and grounded, lacking the supernatural elements common in mecha anime. This grounded approach makes the violence feel more real and the choices more devastating.

**Moral Ambiguity:** Unlike propaganda or jingoistic war stories, The Cockpit presents German and Japanese soldiers as human beings—some sympathetic, some less so—all trapped in systems that demand their compliance with atrocity. The series asks: how do ordinary people become complicit in extraordinary evil?

**Neglected Legacy:** Despite being directed by industry legends and based on work by Leiji Matsumoto, The Cockpit has remained obscure in the West, partly due to its controversial "Axis-positive" portrayal and partly due to licensing difficulties. It remains a hidden gem for those interested in mature, thoughtful war anime that refuses easy moral answers.
