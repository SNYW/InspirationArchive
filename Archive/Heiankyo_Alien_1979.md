# Heiankyo Alien (1979)

## The Pitch

Heiankyo Alien is a pioneering maze video game created by the Theoretical Science Group at the University of Tokyo in 1979. Originally developed as a personal computer game for the PC-8000 series, it was later published as an arcade game by Denki Onkyo in November 1979, with a North American release by Sega-Gremlin in 1980 under the title "Digger." The game puts players in control of a Kebiishi—a knight from feudal Japan—who must defend the ancient capital city of Kyoto (Heiankyo) from an alien invasion. Players navigate maze-like corridors, burrowing through dirt to evade and trap aliens, collecting treasure to earn points. The game was notable for its innovative mechanics that prefigured later classics like Pac-Man and Digger's own descendants, and represents an early example of Japanese arcade game design combining historical fantasy elements with science fiction premises.

## The Truth

The player controls a Kebiishi, a supernatural knight from Japanese folklore tasked with protecting the ancient capital. The game begins in a maze where the Kebiishi and several aliens appear. The objective is to collect gold treasures that appear throughout the maze while avoiding the aliens. The player can burrow through the dirt passages to create tunnels, allowing for strategic movement and trap-setting. By dropping dirt on aliens, players can eliminate them and score points. The game ends when all lives are lost.

In the Japanese version, the player character is a kebiishi—a historically accurate warrior monk from Japanese folklore who possessed supernatural abilities to fight evil spirits and demons. This historical-fantasy framing gives the game its distinctive aesthetic compared to Western maze games of the era. The aliens themselves are depicted as mysterious entities invading the ancient capital, combining science fiction (extraterrestrial invasion) with historical Japanese settings.

The game was originally developed by Mitsutoshi Tabata, a member of the Theoretical Science Group at the University of Tokyo, and designed by Itaru Kawakami. It was one of the earliest games created at a Japanese university research group, representing an important moment in the development of Japan's video game industry. The arcade version proved popular enough to receive multiple home ports, including versions for the Game Boy and Super Famicom in later years, as well as a modern re-release on the exA-Arcadia arcade platform.

## The Core

Heiankyo Alien represents a fascinating early example of Japanese game developers blending Western arcade gameplay with distinctly Japanese cultural and aesthetic elements. While the core mechanics—navigating a maze, collecting items, avoiding enemies—would become staples of the arcade medium, the game's use of the kebiishi concept and Heian-kyo setting created a uniquely Japanese take on the emerging science fiction arcade genre.

The game's development at the University of Tokyo's Theoretical Science Group is also historically significant, representing an early academic contribution to what would become a massive Japanese industry. The collaboration between university researchers and commercial publishers like Denki Onkyo helped establish the pattern of Japanese video game development that would dominate the industry in subsequent decades.

The North American release as "Digger" stripped away much of the Japanese cultural specificity, representing the common practice of the era in which foreign games were localized without their original context. However, the underlying gameplay mechanics influenced subsequent maze games, and the game remains notable as one of the earliest examples of the genre with Japanese origins.
