# Návštěvníci (1983)

**Tags:** 🌍 Czechoslovakia | 🎭 Optimistic | 📚 Utopia | 🧬 Time-Travel, Exploration

**Medium:** TV Series
**Raw Score:** 4.1/5
**Total Ratings:** 813
**Creator/Author:** Jindřich Polák (director), Ota Hofman (creator)

## The Pitch

Návštěvníci (The Visitors) is a Czechoslovak sci-fi TV series filmed between 1981 and 1983, consisting of 15 episodes that aired from November 1983 to February 1984. The series was co-produced with West Germany, Switzerland, and France, making it an unusually international production for Eastern Bloc television of the era.

Set in the year 2484, humanity has evolved into a peaceful, post-national civilization governed by the World Council and advised by an artificial intelligence called the Central Brain of Mankind. When the Central Brain predicts that a comet will shift Earth's axis and threaten civilization, a team of time travelers is dispatched back to 1984 on "Expedition Adam 84"—disguised as surveyors in a Lada Niva—to retrieve three notebooks belonging to young Adam Bernau, who will win a Nobel Prize in 2034 for a formula capable of moving continents.

The series combines elements of time-travel adventure with a warm, humanistic vision of the future. The time travelers—historian Filip, doctor Noll, technician Karas, and documentarian Katja—struggle to adapt to 1984 life, fumbling with money and technology while forming bonds with the locals, including the young Adam and his future "great teacher" Alois Drchlík.

## The Truth

The expedition arrives in the town of Kamenice in 1984, where they establish headquarters in the house that will become the future Adam Bernau Museum. Posing as road surveyors, they attempt to befriend young Adam and recover his three notebooks, which contain the world-shifting formula. However, the notebooks are never found—the time travelers arrive too late, and the boy is more interested in his girlfriend Ali than in writing formulas.

Complications arise throughout: the Central Brain sends money from the wrong year (1884 instead of 1984), tools are lost, and team members fall in love with 1984 inhabitants. Filip becomes obsessed with his mission while his colleagues adapt to the past. The team ultimately fails to secure the notebooks but rescues Alois Drchlík from a fatal accident, taking him to 2484.

In the future, Drchlík discovers the Central Brain is standing asymmetrically—a minor mechanical flaw—and fixes it with a wooden wedge. The computer immediately recalculates and announces it was mistaken: the comet poses no threat after all. The series ends with Adam, having been grounded for property damage, telling Ali he spent his time working on a formula to move continents and worlds—setting up the future the expedition sought to document.

## The Core

**Utopian Vision Without Naivety:** Unlike the grim dystopias that dominated 1980s sci-fi, Návštěvníci presents a genuinely optimistic future—yet one earned through the narrative's failures. The 2484 Earth has overcome war, nation-states, and scarcity, allowing people to pursue art and science. Yet this utopia is not static; it requires historical knowledge, human connection, and even intervention from the past to survive. The series suggests that utopias must be actively constructed, not merely awaited.

**Time Travel as Historical Revision:** The series plays with the paradox of historical knowledge. The travelers know Adam's future but cannot control it. They seek to retrieve his formula but fail—yet the future still happens, suggesting that history operates beyond human manipulation. This resonates with the series' Eastern Bloc context: a meditation on whether one can truly know or reshape the future, even with perfect information.

**East-West Collaboration:** The series' multinational production was unusual for Czechoslovak television and reflects its theme of global cooperation. The future World Council mirrors the series' own international production, suggesting that unity—even between Cold War blocs—might be necessary to face existential threats.

**The Gadget Master:** Theodor Pištěk, who designed the series' costumes and gadgets, later won an Oscar—this early work shows his talent for practical sci-fi design. The Lada Niva time machine, surveyors' equipment, and future gadgets ground the series in tangible, Eastern Bloc reality rather than Hollywood spectacle.

Návštěvníci remains a charming artifact of Czechoslovak sci-fi, notable for its optimistic vision, its humanistic themes, and its unusual status as an international co-production from behind the Iron Curtain.
