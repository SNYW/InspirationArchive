## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Což takhle dát si špenát (translated as "How About a Plate of Spinach") is a 1977 Czechoslovak comedy science fiction film directed by Václav Vorlíček and written by Miloš Macourek. The film follows two small-time swindlers, Zemánek and Liška, who are released from prison after serving time for alcohol theft. Looking for their next get-rich-quick scheme, they find employment at a scientific laboratory working on a revolutionary device designed to regenerate old cows. When a wealthy salon owner discovers their work, she sees an opportunity for profit—she wants to use the regeneration technology for human rejuvenation treatments. However, things go spectacularly wrong when the two con artists accidentally test the device on themselves, discovering that instead of restoring their youth, they have been transformed into young children. The film blends slapstick comedy with satirical commentary on youth obsession, beauty standards, and the foolish pursuit of eternal youth.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film begins with the release of two petty criminals, Zemánek (played by Vladimír Menšík) and Liška (played by Jiří Sovák), from prison. The duo had been convicted of stealing alcohol and are now looking for their next scheme. Their search leads them to a laboratory where scientists are developing a revolutionary regeneration device capable of restoring vitality to aging cattle.

When the owner of a luxury beauty salon learns about the technology, she sees tremendous commercial potential. She recruits Zemánek and Liška to help her adapt the device for human use, promising them substantial financial rewards. The plan involves using the regeneration technology to create anti-aging treatments that will make wealthy clients feel young again.

The two swindlers agree to assist, but their greed and incompetence lead to disaster. During a secret nighttime test, Zemánek and Liška decide to try the regeneration device on themselves, hoping to experience the benefits before anyone else. However, the device malfunctions dramatically—instead of giving them renewed adult vitality, it transforms them back into young children.

The rest of the film follows the comedic consequences of their transformation. As children, Zemánek and Liška must navigate a world where they are now physically young but retain adult minds. They encounter their former associates, including Liška's own daughter, who now appears as an adult while he has become a child. The absurdity reaches peak levels as the two grown men, now in children's bodies, struggle to maintain their schemes while dealing with the practical difficulties of being small.

Throughout the narrative, the film explores themes of identity, aging, and the foolish pursuit of eternal youth through a comedic lens. The transformation forces the protagonists to experience life from a completely different perspective, ultimately leading to a reconsideration of what youth and age really mean.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Folly of Youth Obsession:** The film serves as a satirical commentary on society's obsession with youth and appearance. The wealthy salon owner's desire to commercialize rejuvenation technology represents society's superficial values and willingness to pay anything for the appearance of youth. The disastrous results suggest that attempting to reverse aging without understanding its natural place in life leads only to chaos and absurdity.

**Comedy of Errors:** Director Václav Vorlíček and writer Miloš Macourek employ the classic comedy of errors format, using mistaken identities and physical transformations to create humor. The film explores what happens when two adult con artists are literally forced to start over as children—their schemes, their relationships, their entire sense of self are upended by their physical transformation.

**Social Satire:** Beyond its comedic surface, the film satirizes the beauty industry, scientific hubris, and the commodification of youth. The laboratory's work on regenerating cows being adapted for human vanity reflects Macourek's critical eye on consumer society and the lengths people will go to in pursuit of superficial ideals.

**Transformation and Identity:** The body-swap premise allows the film to explore questions of identity—are Zemánek and Liška still themselves when trapped in children's bodies? The film suggests that while physical appearance changes, core personality remains, and perhaps the most valuable qualities have nothing to do with age or appearance.

Což takhle dát si špenát remains a beloved entry in Czechoslovak comedy cinema, representing the creative partnership between Vorlíček and Macourek that produced several cult classics during the 1970s. The film's blend of science fiction premises with traditional Czech comedic sensibilities creates a unique entry in Eastern European science fiction cinema.
