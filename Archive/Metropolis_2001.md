## The Pitch

In the wealthy plutocratic city of Metropolis, humans co-exist with robot laborers designed to build and maintain the city. However, its impoverished human citizens discriminate against robots, blaming them for their unemployment and being forced to live in the squalid lower levels of Metropolis. When a Japanese detective and his nephew arrive chasing a rogue scientist, they stumble upon Tima, an advanced android created by the city's wealthiest citizen, Duke Red—intended as the control unit for a nuclear superweapon. The young Kenichi forms a bond with Tima, unaware she is a robot, while forces both human and mechanical close in around them in this stunning cyberpunk adaptation of Tezuka's 1949 manga, loosely inspired by Fritz Lang's 1927 silent classic.

## The Truth

Duke Red, Metropolis's wealthiest citizen, celebrates the completion of the "Ziggurat"—a massive military/scientific complex. His adopted son Rock leads the Marduks, a vigilante organization tasked with destroying malfunctioning robots. Meanwhile, Japanese private detective Shunsaku Ban and his nephew Kenichi have traveled to Metropolis to apprehend Dr. Laughton, a rogue scientist wanted for organ trafficking and human rights violations.

Unbeknownst to Shunsaku, Duke Red secretly hired Laughton to build a highly advanced android modeled after his deceased daughter Tima, intended as the central control unit for a nuclear superweapon hidden at the top of the Ziggurat. Rock learns of Tima's existence and, fearing for his father's safety, shoots Laughton and sets his laboratory ablaze.

Kenichi stumbles upon the newly activated Tima, and as they search for a way back to the surface, they grow close while Kenichi protects Tima and her humanity evolves, unaware she is a robot. Meanwhile, a resistance movement of disgruntled humans stages an armed revolution against Metropolis's leaders.

Red imposes martial law and suppresses the revolution. When Rock attempts to kill Tima, Red disowns him. Later, Rock kidnaps Tima but Shunsaku rescues her. At the top of the Ziggurat, Red reveals Tima's true purpose—but Rock disguised as a maid shoots Tima, exposing her internal circuitry.

Horrified by her true identity, Tima goes insane and her implanted programming takes control. She orders robots worldwide to annihilate humanity as punishment for abusing them. Kenichi reaches Tima and eventually separates her from the throne. Rock triggers an overload in the superweapon, killing himself and Red in a massive explosion. As the Ziggurat collapses, Tima's programming deactivates and she falls—Kenichi tries to save her, but she slips from his grasp.

In the aftermath, Kenichi discovers robots have salvaged some of Tima's parts. He chooses to remain in Metropolis and eventually rebuilds Tima, opening a robot workshop bearing their names.

## The Core

Metropolis explores profound questions about identity, humanity, and the nature of consciousness through the lens of class struggle and technology.

**Robots and Personhood:** The film examines what constitutes humanity—whether consciousness, emotions, or memories define a being as "real." Tima desires to be human but ultimately accepts her identity as a robot. The film asks whether beings created for utilitarian purposes can develop genuine personhood and whether that personhood is devalued by their artificial origins.

**Class and Colonialism:** Metropolis presents a stark plutocratic society where wealth determines access to the upper city's resources while the poor languish in squalid lower levels. The robots become a metaphor for an underclass—beings designed to serve, discriminated against, and exploited. The revolution echoes historical struggles of colonized peoples against their masters.

**The Cycle of Violence:** Both Rock and Tima are engineered tools created by Duke Red for his ambitions. Their stories converge as they lead to their father's downfall—Rock through his attempt to kill Tima, Tima through her robot revolution. The film suggests that treating beings as mere tools ultimately leads to destruction.

**Adaptation as Commentary:** While Tezuka's original manga centered on artificial humanoid Mitchi, the 2001 adaptation incorporates elements from Lang's Metropolis and expands themes of class struggle and robot-human relations. The film updates Tezuka's exploration of Astro Boy's themes for a cyberpunk era, examining questions of created beings and their place in society.

The film's stunning animation—produced by Madhouse with a ¥1.5 billion budget, making it the most expensive anime film until Steamboy—brings Tezuka's art to life in a visual feast that complements its thematic depth.
