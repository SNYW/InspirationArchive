# Metropolis (1927)

## The Pitch

Metropolis is a pioneering German silent science-fiction film set in a futuristic urban dystopia where a great gulf separates the wealthy industrialists who rule from the underground-dwelling workers who toil to operate the massive machines that power the city. Directed by Fritz Lang and written by Thea von Harbou, the film is renowned for its groundbreaking visual effects, elaborate Art Deco sets, and powerful social commentary on the divide between capital and labor. The story follows Freder, the privileged son of the city's master, who becomes fascinated with Maria, a spiritual leader among the workers, and seeks to bridge the gap between the ruling class and the oppressed laborers.

## The Truth

In the futuristic city of Metropolis, wealthy industrialists and business magnates reign from towering skyscrapers while workers labor deep underground to power the massive machines that keep the city running. Joh Fredersen is the city's autocratic master, his son Freder idles away his time in pleasure gardens until the arrival of Maria, a young woman who brings workers' children to see the lifestyle of their rich "brothers."

Freder becomes obsessed with Maria and ventures into the lower levels, witnessing a deadly machine explosion that he hallucinates as the workers being fed to a Moloch-like deity. Meanwhile, Fredersen discovers secret maps of catacombs beneath the city and consults with the inventor Rotwang, who has built a robot doubles as the "Maschinenmensch" (Machine Human). Rotwang, still obsessed with his lost love Hel (who married Fredersen and died giving birth to Freder), kidnaps Maria and transfers her likeness to the robot.

The false Maria incites the workers to destroy the machines, causing a catastrophic flood that threatens to drown the workers' children. The real Maria escapes and rescues the children while Freder confronts Rotwang on the cathedral roof. During the struggle, Rotwang falls to his death. Freder ultimately mediates between his father and the workers' foreman Grot, uniting the "head" and the "hands" through the "heart."

## The Core

Metropolis is widely regarded as one of the greatest and most influential films ever made, pioneering the science-fiction genre as a feature-length film. Its themes of class division, the dehumanization of labor, and the tension between technology and humanity resonate throughout science-fiction history. The film's famous tagline—"The Mediator Between the Head and the Hands Must Be the Heart"—advocates for empathy and connection across class boundaries.

Visually, Metropolis is a masterpiece of German Expressionist cinema, featuring revolutionary special effects, massive Art Deco sets, and innovative cinematography. The robot Maria design influenced countless works in science-fiction and beyond. Though critics initially found its story naive and its politics simplistic (H.G. Wells famously called it "silly"), the film has been reevaluated over decades and is now celebrated for its ambitious vision and technical achievements.

The film's complex restoration history—portions were lost, then rediscovered in Argentina and New Zealand—adds to its legendary status. In 2001, Metropolis was inscribed on UNESCO's Memory of the World International Register, the first film to receive this distinction.
