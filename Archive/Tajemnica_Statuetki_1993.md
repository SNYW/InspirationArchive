# Tajemnica Statuetki (1993)

## The Pitch

Tajemnica Statuetki (The Mystery of the Statuette) is a pioneering Polish point-and-click adventure game developed by Metropolis Software House for DOS computers in 1993. Set in the early 1990s, the game follows John Pollack, a young American Interpol agent of Polish descent, as he investigates a series of thefts involving ancient religious artifacts around the world. The trail leads him to Joachim Wadner, a former CIA operative and commando with apparent supernatural connections. Originally released only in Polish, the game became a cult classic in Poland despite massive software piracy, selling 4,000-6,000 copies—a remarkable achievement for the nascent Polish gaming industry. It was the first Polish adventure game and helped establish Metropolis Software as a pioneer in Eastern European game development.

## The Truth

The game opens withInterpol discovering that ancient artifacts with religious significance are being stolen from museums and churches worldwide. The thefts share a pattern: items with little market value but profound historical importance are targeted. Investigators realize this is not the work of a common thief but someone with ideological or supernatural motives.

John Pollack, described as Interpol's best trainee, is assigned to the case. He's portrayed as an elite operative with extraordinary abilities—fluent in multiple languages, able to solve a Rubik's Cube in under three minutes, and skilled in hand-to-hand combat. Armed with a gadget-filled briefcase and unlimited funds, Pollack follows the trail to San Ambrosio, a fictional island off the coast of Chile.

Working undercover at a local cafe, Pollack gathers intelligence on Wadner, who has recently been spotted in the area. The investigation involves classic adventure game puzzles: finding hidden objects, combining inventory items, and interacting with NPCs. Players must solve various challenges, including mixing correct cocktails, navigating social situations, and locating pixel-sized items—a notorious aspect of the game's difficulty.

The investigation takes a dark turn when Pollack is captured and must escape from a detention cell using improvised tools and environmental interactions. From Chile, the trail extends through various international locations, with the plot progressively incorporating occult and supernatural elements. The story culminates in a finale involving satanic rituals and occult themes, shifting from a conventional crime thriller into supernatural horror territory.

The game uses digitized photographs taken at the Côte d'Azur, Saint Tropez, Monte Carlo, Nice, and an abandoned Calvinist church in Poland as its visual backdrop—a groundbreaking technique for Polish games at the time.

## The Core

Tajemnica Statuetki holds historical significance as the first Polish adventure game and a foundational title in the country's gaming industry. Developed during Poland's transition from communism to capitalism in the early 1990s, the game represented a bold assertion that Polish developers could create products comparable to Western titles.

The game's design philosophy focused on creating a Polish-language adventure that didn't require English fluency, opening video gaming to a broader domestic audience. This cultural mission—using the vernacular to make interactive entertainment accessible—proved influential for the subsequent generation of Polish game developers.

The use of digitized photographs as backgrounds was technically ambitious for the Polish market, creating an atmosphere that impressed players despite the technical limitations. While pixel hunting and difficult puzzles drew criticism, the narrative quality and originality of the scenario were widely praised.

The transition from detective procedural to occult horror in the final act reflects the game's ambition to transcend genre conventions. By blending crime thriller elements with supernatural themes, it created a distinctive tonal mixture that resonated with players seeking mature, complex storytelling.

The game launched the career of Adrian Chmielarz, who would later found People Can Fly and develop acclaimed titles including The Vanishing of Ethan Carter. The success of Tajemnica Statuetki demonstrated that the Polish market could support original domestic games, paving the way for the country's later success in the global gaming industry.
