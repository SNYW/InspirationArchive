# Planetfall (1983)

**Tags:** 🌍 USA | 🎭 Cerebral | 📚 Hard-SF | 🧬 Sentience, Isolation, Exploration

**Medium:** Video Game
**Raw Score:** 3.8/5
**Total Ratings:** 101
**Creator/Author:** Steve Meretzky (Infocom)

## The Pitch

Planetfall is a classic interactive fiction game from Infocom, released in 1983 as the company's eighth title. Players take on the role of an Ensign Seventh Class aboard the starship S.P.S. Feinstein, serving in the Stellar Patrol. After a series of explosions devastate the ship, the player escapes in a pod and crash-lands on the distant planet Resida—a world that appears completely deserted except for one mysterious structure. There, the player encounters Floyd, a childlike and lovable robot who becomes an invaluable companion. Together, they must unravel the secrets of this dead civilization and discover what happened to its inhabitants before time runs out. Planetfall was Steve Meretzky's first published game and became one of Infocom's most beloved titles, praised for its emotional storytelling and memorable characters.

## The Truth

The game begins on the S.P.S. Feinstein, where the player character is assigned the lowly task of mopping decks under the supervision of the overbearing Ensign First Class Blather. Despite the promise of glorious adventures pitched by recruiters on Gallium, the player's mundane life consists of cleaning duty and avoiding demerits. One day, catastrophic explosions rock the ship, and the player scrambles into an escape pod, crash-landing on a nearby planet.

Upon reaching the surface, the player discovers a seemingly abandoned island on the planet Resida, featuring a single mysterious structure. Inside, they find ancient technology and, crucially, encounter Floyd—a robot with childlike innocence and genuine emotion. Floyd becomes the player's companion and helper throughout the investigation.

As the player explores the abandoned base and pieces together clues, they gradually uncover the planet's tragic history. Long ago, a deadly plague threatened to wipe out all life on Resida. The remaining inhabitants launched a planetwide project to place everyone in suspended animation while automated systems worked to find a cure. By the time the player arrives, the cure has been found, but the planet itself is dying—orbital decay has caused massive global warming, rising oceans, and meteorite bombardments threaten to destroy what remains.

The player must solve the mysteries of the base, help stabilize the situation, and find a way to return home—all while racing against time before the planet is destroyed. The game features the signature Infocom parser, requiring text commands to explore, interact with objects, and communicate with Floyd.

## The Core

**Isolation and Companionhip:** At its heart, Planetfall is a game about loneliness and the profound impact of connection. The player is literally stranded alone on a dying world, the last survivor of a catastrophic event. Floyd represents hope, humor, and emotional support in an otherwise bleak situation. The robot's childlike questions, loyalty, and occasional comic relief transformed what could have been a standard survival game into something deeply human. Floyd became one of Infocom's most memorable characters and set a new standard for emotional storytelling in games.

**The Weight of History:** The game's narrative explores themes of legacy, extinction, and what remains when a civilization disappears. The inhabitants of Resida placed their faith in technology and science to save them, retreating into suspended animation with the promise of revival. But by the time help arrives, it's almost too late—their world is already dying. This raises questions about hope, perseverance, and whether it's worth sacrificing the present for an uncertain future.

**Interactive Fiction as Exploration:** Planetfall represents the height of Infocom's craft—using text as the sole medium to create atmosphere, emotion, and wonder. Without graphics, the game relies on evocative prose to paint pictures of alien landscapes, mysterious ruins, and the haunting emptiness of a world that once teemed with life. The text parser invites active participation, making players feel like genuine explorers rather than passive observers.

**Aesthetics of Melancholy:** The game's tone balances curiosity and discovery with an undercurrent of sadness and urgency. The player knows from the beginning that this civilization is gone—that there's no happy ending for the people who trusted their survival to machines. This melancholy was relatively unusual in games of the era and helped establish that video games could explore complex, bittersweet emotions.

Planetfall's success proved that games could tell emotionally resonant stories and created a template for narrative-driven adventure games. Its influence extends to modern interactive fiction and narrative games that prioritize character development and emotional impact.
