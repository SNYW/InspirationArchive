# Dallos (1983)

**Tags:** 🌍 Japan | 🎭 Pessimistic | 📚 Dystopia, Science-Fantasy | 🧬 Rebellion, Megacorps, Isolation

**Medium:** OVA (Original Video Animation)
**Creator/Author:** Hisayuki Toriumi (Concept), Mamoru Oshii (Co-director)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Dallos is a groundbreaking 4-episode OVA released in 1983, widely considered not only the first OVA ever produced but also the first animated direct-to-video release in history. Set in a near-future Earth where resources have been depleted, the story follows the colonists living on Moon mining stations who have been exploited by Earth's government for generations. When the colonists begin to resist through acts of terrorism, a young man named Shun Nonomura finds himself caught between loyalty to his people and the violent path they're taking. At the center of the colonists' hope is a mysterious artifact called Dallos—a structure on the Moon that has become an object of worship. This is a story about colonial oppression, generational divides, and the question of what it means to be human when your home is no longer your own.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

In the near future, humanity has exhausted Earth's natural resources. To survive, the Earth Federal Government established mining colonies on the Moon, where generations of colonists have been forced to labor under harsh conditions, sending vital resources back to Earth while living in indentured servitude.

The story opens by introducing the harsh reality of life on the Moon colony. The colonists, born and raised on the Moon, have never known Earth as anything but a distant oppressor. The Earth Federal Government treats them as second-class citizens, extracting wealth while providing minimal support. Tensions have been building for generations.

Shun Nonomura is a young colonist who becomes increasingly aware of the injustice faced by his people. He watches as younger colonists—natural-born Moon dwellers—begin to question the older generation's submission to Earth. The younger generation sees Earth not as a motherland but as an oppressive force that has stolen their birthright.

The colonists begin organizing acts of resistance against their Earth overseers. These terrorist acts escalate the conflict between the colony and Earth's government. The Earth Federal Government responds with increased security and crackdowns, creating a vicious cycle of violence.

At the heart of the colonists' hope is Dallos—a mysterious ancient structure on the Moon surface. The colonists have begun worshipping Dallos as a symbol of their resistance and as proof that their world has meaning beyond serving Earth. The structure represents both their cultural identity and their defiance.

Shun becomes deeply involved in the rebellion, his actions dramatically affecting those close to him—including his family and friends. He must navigate the generational divide within the colony itself: the older colonists remember Earth with some nostalgia and hope for peaceful resolution, while the younger generation has known only oppression and demands violent revolution.

The OVA explores the political and personal dimensions of this conflict. Characters must choose sides, and the consequences of those choices ripple through relationships and communities. The mystery of Dallos itself—what it truly is and whether it holds any supernatural significance—remains ambiguous, serving more as a focal point for the colonists' hopes and beliefs.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Dallos is a remarkably political work for its time, exploring themes of colonialism, generational conflict, and identity. The Moon colony functions as a metaphor for any marginalized community that has been exploited by a distant power—the colonists are essentially a conquered people, their labor extracted for the benefit of those who have never set foot on their soil.

The generational divide within the colony reflects real-world tensions in post-colonial societies. The older generation, with memories of Earth or at least stories of it, maintains some connection to the "motherland." The younger generation, born entirely on the Moon, sees themselves as a separate people with their own identity and their own right to self-determination.

Mamoru Oshii's involvement is significant—he would go on to become one of anime's most acclaimed directors, known for works like Ghost in the Shell. Dallos shows the early emergence of his mature concerns: political commentary, ambiguity in narrative, and characters caught in insoluble situations.

The mysterious artifact at the film's center raises questions about religion and resistance—whether hope needs to be grounded in reality, or whether the mere act of believing in something larger than oneself can be transformative for a oppressed people.

As the first OVA, Dallos also represents a pivotal moment in anime history. The direct-to-video format would become enormous influential, allowing for more experimental and mature content than theatrical releases typically permitted.
