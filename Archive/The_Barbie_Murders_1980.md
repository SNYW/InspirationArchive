# The Barbie Murders (1980)

**Medium:** Short Story Collection
**Creator/Author:** John Varley

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

The Barbie Murders is a groundbreaking collection of nine science fiction short stories by award-winning author John Varley. Published in 1980, these stories blend noir mystery elements with Varley's distinctive vision of a future humanity spread across the solar system. The title story presents a fascinating premise: in a future where Barbie dolls have achieved near-sentience and live as a persecuted underclass, a detective must investigate murders of these "Barbies" - exploring themes of identity, humanity, and discrimination through an allegory that echoes real-world civil rights struggles.

Other stories in the collection explore equally inventive concepts - from reality-bending funhouses to alternate dimensions, from artificial intelligence to time manipulation. Several stories are set on the colonized Moon, showcasing Varley's vision of lunar society. The collection won the Locus Award for Best Short Story Collection and established Varley as one of science fiction's most innovative voices. These stories are noir-tinged, often melancholic, and frequently challenge readers' assumptions about identity, consciousness, and what it means to be human.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The collection contains nine stories:

**"Bagatelle"** - A young girl with incredible musical abilities is taken from her family to be trained as an "instrument" for powerful interests, exploring themes of exploitation and the cost of genius.

**"The Funhouse Effect"** - A reality-bending tale where a man discovers that an amusement park funhouse exists in multiple dimensions simultaneously, leading to encounters with alternate versions of himself.

**"The Barbie Murders"** - The centerpiece story takes place in a future where sentient Barbie dolls have become a reality - created as companions, they now exist as a marginalized underclass subject to abuse and murder. When a series of Barbie murders rocks this fragile society, detective Saul Reynolds must investigate, ultimately discovering uncomfortable truths about what it means to create intelligent life and then treat it as less than human. The story works as both a mystery and an allegory for civil rights.

**"Equinoctial"** - A time-travel story set during the construction of the Panama Canal, where workers from different eras collide in a time loop.

**"Manikins"** - The story of a man who creates perfect robotic copies of people, exploring identity and the nature of consciousness.

**"Beatnik Bayou"** - A surreal tale set in an alternate New Orleans where reality functions differently.

**"Good-bye, Robinson Crusoe"** - A story about the last human on a terraformed Mars and the alien intelligence that befriends him.

**"Lollipop and the Tar Baby"** - A story featuring the character Lollipop, exploring themes of memory and identity.

**"Picnic on Nearside"** - Set on the Moon, this story involves a group of colonists dealing with an otherworldly threat.

The collection showcases Varley's range - from hard SF to surrealism, from noir mystery to poignant human drama. His lunar society stories paint a convincing picture of frontier life among the stars, while the title story remains one of SF's most thought-provoking explorations of created consciousness.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

John Varley's The Barbie Murders represents a pivotal moment in science fiction's evolution during the 1970s-80s. The collection demonstrates several key themes that would become central to the genre's development.

The title story serves as a powerful allegory for civil rights and the treatment of "the other." By creating a sentient minority (the Barbies) who look identical to "normal" humans but are treated as less than human, Varley creates a mirror for examining real-world prejudice. The story asks uncomfortable questions about creation, consciousness, and moral responsibility - if we create intelligent life, what obligations do we have toward it?

Several stories explore identity and consciousness - what makes someone (or something) human? "Manikins" directly addresses this through the creation of perfect robotic duplicates, while "The Funhouse Effect" uses dimensional manipulation to explore how identity might persist (or fracture) across realities.

Varley's lunar settings anticipate much of later SF's interest in space colonization, but he focuses not on the technical aspects but on the social dynamics - how would actual people build societies on frontier worlds? His Moon is a gritty, working-class place, far from the gleaming utopias of earlier SF.

The noir elements - detectives, murders, moral ambiguity - show SF growing up, moving beyond simple problem-solving into more complex emotional and ethical territory. Varley doesn't offer easy answers; his futures are messy, his characters compromised, his conclusions uncertain.

The collection won the Locus Award and was nominated for the Hugo, establishing Varley as one of SF's most important voices. His influence can be seen in later works dealing with AI rights, virtual reality, and identity - themes that remain relevant today.
