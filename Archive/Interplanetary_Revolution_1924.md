# Interplanetary Revolution (1924)

## The Pitch

Interplanetary Revolution is one of the earliest Soviet animated films, created in 1924 as a satirical animated short. Originally intended as animated inserts for the sci-fi film Aelita, it was instead released separately as a parody. The 8-minute black-and-white silent film uses primitive cutout animation to deliver a starkly political message about class struggle in space.

The film depicts the bourgeoisie of all countries fleeing Earth in a shoe-shaped spaceship to escape the advancing proletariat, hoping to save their plundered capital. A red army commissar named Kominternov pursues them, ultimately landing on Mars where he fosters a victorious proletarian revolution. The animation is deliberately crude, almost devoid of intertitles, relying on visual symbolism to convey its revolutionary message.

## The Truth

The film opens with the bourgeois class—depicted with bulldog heads wearing top hats with hidden swastikas, vampires in tailcoats—panicking as the proletarian revolution spreads across Earth. These caricatures of capitalists are shown sucking the blood of workers, their hair standing on end from reading the newspaper "Pravda."

Fleeing in a bizarre shoe-shaped spacecraft, the bourgeoisie escape to space hoping to find refuge on another planet. However, the red commissar Kominternov follows them in his own vessel. When he lands on Mars, he discovers the Martians are ready for revolution. He successfully implants communist ideology among the Martian workers, leading to a planetary proletarian uprising.

Meanwhile, Kominternov spots a man and woman on the moon kissing—presumably a jab at the earlier film Aelita, which featured a romantic subplot. The bourgeoisie eventually meet their doom, swallowed by the sun as they attempt to escape their revolutionary fate.

The film was created by students of the State Television and Radio Broadcasting Company, working in an experimental animation workshop. It was never officially shown in theaters upon its initial release.

## The Core

**Soviet Animated Pioneers:** Interplanetary Revolution represents a milestone in animation history as one of the first Soviet animated films. Its creation by Nikolai Khodataev, Zenon Komissarenko, and Yuri Merkulov established foundational techniques that would influence decades of Soviet animation, despite—or because of—its primitive aesthetic.

**Propaganda as Art:** The film exemplifies how Soviet cinema used science fiction as a vehicle for political messaging. The space setting provides allegorical distance to critique capitalism while promoting revolutionary ideology. The caricatures are intentionally exaggerated, even clownish, suggesting the filmmakers were aware of the propaganda's inherent ridiculousness even as they delivered it.

**Bipartite Parody:** Notably, the film parodies not just capitalists but also communists. The red commissar himself is a somewhat absurd figure, and the portrayal of revolutionary triumph borders on the fantastical. This self-aware quality gives the film a nuanced position within Soviet-era political cinema—simultaneously endorsing revolution while acknowledging its own theatricality.

**Historical Context:** Created during the early Soviet period, the film reflects the revolutionary optimism and class warfare mentality of the 1920s. The imagery of capitalists as bloodsucking vampires with swastikas places it firmly in anti-capitalist tradition, while the Mars revolution anticipates decades of Soviet science fiction that would use space exploration as a metaphor for socialist progress.
