# Outwars (1998)

**Total Ratings:** 24

## The Pitch

Outwars is a military science fiction tactical shooter developed by SingleTrac and published by Microsoft in 1998. The game places players in the role of a heavily armed marine in the elite CDF Marine Jump Corps, tasked with battling insectoid aliens known as "The Skulls" who are intent on taking over the galaxy. Featuring a selectable perspective tactical shooter format, players don various rocket-propelled powered armors—Light Scout, Medium Combat, Heavy Assault, or Heavy Dreadnaut—and lead small teams of AI-controlled squadmates through mission-based gameplay across multiple planets.

The game stood out for its innovative approach to loadout customization, allowing players to equip different weapons and equipment at the beginning of each mission. The mechanics supported simultaneous firing of multiple weapon types—including shoulder-mounted guided missiles, pulse rifles, and remote-detonation mines—rather than the consecutive firing typical of shooters at the time. Multiplayer supported up to eight players via Direct3D and Internet Gaming Zone, offering modes including Free for All, Team War, and Capture the Flag.

## The Truth

The single-player campaign unfolds across multiple planets as humanity faces extinction at the hands of The Skulls.

The story begins on Mikhal's World, which has been overrun by The Skulls. Players must reach an escape ship within a two-minute window or be left behind. After surviving this initial evacuation, players are dropped onto Planet Oasis for training, but The Skulls attack almost immediately, forcing another evacuation.

Oasis falls to The Skulls, and the humans order a total evacuation, though the player must first retrieve "special technology" from an abandoned military base before escaping. Immediately after the player leaves, Oasis is destroyed by alien technology, transformed into what Commander Hackett describes as "some kind of planet from hell."

The conflict spreads to multiple worlds as humans lose badly across the galaxy. The player's team is sent to secure Planet Anubis, where miners discovered something mysterious in the mine shafts. The player discovers The Skulls were living underground and, tired of constant threat from the miners, launched their offensive against humanity. During the mission, the player falls into The Skulls' lair and encounters the first Queen boss.

The final assault targets The Skulls' stronghold: the massive Juggernaut. Players must capture the Mastermind to obtain security codes, defeat a second Queen, and escape before the ship explodes. The climax reveals that Oasis—now called DeadWorld—has become The Skulls' main base, from which they plan to annihilate Earth.

The final mission involves infiltrating The Skulls' primary weapon on DeadWorld to disable it and turn it against them. Players face the game's final boss, The King, and after defeating him, reactivate the weapon to destroy the planet before escaping. The game ends with a message: "Stay there, Captain. We're coming to get you."

## The Core

Outwars represents an interesting artifact of late-1990s military science fiction gaming, combining the tactical shooter mechanics popularized by titles like Quake with a narrative framework of humanity under siege by an insectoid alien threat. The powered armor system and simultaneous multi-weapon firing offered mechanical innovations that distinguished it from contemporaries, though the game received mixed reviews for technical issues and AI problems.

The game's narrative follows a familiar pattern of human military forces fighting against overwhelming alien odds across multiple planetary battlefields—a space opera structure that emphasizes exploration and combat across the galaxy. The insectoid Skull aliens function as a classic "other" enemy, with the Queen and King bosses providing hierarchical antagonists reminiscent of sci-fi horror conventions.

From a design perspective, Outwars was SingleTrac's first project for PC after developing primarily for PlayStation, and this cross-platform transition showed in both the game's strengths and limitations. The critical reception was mixed: Pelit scored it 90/100 praising the gameplay beneath dated graphics, while Next Generation cited severe technical issues that marred the experience.

The game now exists as a relatively obscure title from the late 1990s tactical shooter era, representing a particular moment when military science fiction games were exploring the intersection of tactical gameplay and space opera storytelling.
