## The Pitch

In a small coastal town, young Giovanni—a lonely cat boy whose mother is ill and father absent—embarks on a magical journey aboard a steam train traveling through the Milky Way galaxy. Joined by his only friend Campanella, Giovanni witnesses impossible sights: scholars excavating crystal sands, a bird-catcher who transforms herons into candy, and passengers from a Titanic-like ship. But as the train approaches the Coalsack (a black hole), Giovanni must face an unbearable loss. This 1985 anime adaptation of Kenji Miyazawa's philosophical novel is a haunting meditation on friendship, sacrifice, and what it truly means to find happiness.

## The Truth

The film opens with Giovanni at school, where he is too timid to answer a question about the Milky Way, though he knows the answer from reading at his friend Campanella's house. After school, Giovanni works at a print shop to support his ill mother. When he goes to collect milk, he is mocked by his classmates who ridicule him for claiming his father will return with an otter-skin coat.

That night, during the Festival of Stars, Giovanni runs to a hilltop and gazes at the Milky Way. A steam train appears, and he boards it, finding Campanella already on board. The train travels through the galaxy, making stops at various celestial locations. At one stop, they meet their teacher excavating fossils from crystallized sands. At another, they encounter a strange bird-catcher who turns herons into sweets.

A ticket inspector asks to see their tickets. Campanella has one, but Giovanni finds only a piece of paper in his pocket—which turns out to be a rare ticket allowing travel anywhere. They meet passengers from a ship that sank after hitting an iceberg, who share apples with them. As they pass the constellation Scorpius, a child recounts the parable of the scorpion who fell into a well rather than sacrifice itself to a weasel, then prayed to bring happiness to others in its next life—its body bursting into the flame that now burns as the stars of Scorpius.

The train stops at the Southern Cross, where all other passengers disembark for Christian Heaven. Only Giovanni and Campanella remain. Giovanni declares they should travel together forever. But as the train approaches the Coalsack (a black hole), Campanella sees what he calls "the true Heaven"—a meadow where his mother waits for him. Campanella vanishes, leaving Giovanni alone.

Giovanni awakens on the hilltop. He collects the milk and learns in town that Campanella drowned while saving their classmate Zanelli from the river. Campanella's father tells Giovanni his father will soon return home. Giovanni believes Campanella is "at the edge of the universe" because they explored it together. He vows to be like the scorpion—to bring happiness to others—and walks home.

## The Core

Night on the Galactic Railroad is a profound exploration of Buddhist and Christian concepts of heaven, sacrifice, and transcendence. Kenji Miyazawa wrote the novel after his beloved sister died in 1922, and the story reflects his philosophy of self-sacrifice as the highest good.

The film transforms the human characters into anthropomorphic cats—a stylistic choice that simultaneously distances and universalizes the story. The Esperanto text throughout (an homage to Miyazawa, who was a proponent of the language) creates a dreamlike, universal atmosphere.

The central theme revolves on Giovanni's coming-of-age understanding that true happiness comes not from personal gain or reunion with his father, but from the willingness to sacrifice oneself for others—mirroring the scorpion's eternal flame in Scorpius. The ambiguous ending suggests Campanella has achieved transcendence through his act of sacrifice, while Giovanni must carry forward the lessons of their journey by living a life dedicated to others' happiness.

The film also explores the nature of reality versus dream, as Giovanni's galactic journey may be interpreted as a mystical experience, a death fantasy, or a literal otherworldly adventure—never fully resolving which interpretation is "correct."
