## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

I Killed Einstein, Gentlemen is a 1970 Czechoslovak science fiction comedy film that blends time travel paradoxes with sharp political satire. When a futuristic bureaucrat discovers that humanity's future depends on preventing Einstein's theory of relativity from being discovered, she is sent back in time to assassinate the young Einstein before he can revolutionize physics. However, comedy ensues as her assassination attempts repeatedly fail in absurd ways, and she discovers that causality has a sense of humor. The film is notable for featuring what has been called a "selfie stick" several decades before the modern device was invented—making this obscure Czech comedy surprisingly prescient. The movie blends Eastern European absurdist humor with sci-fi conventions, creating a uniquely charming piece of speculative comedy that remains virtually unknown outside of Czechoslovakia.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens in a dystopian future where humanity has been reduced to mere numbers in a vast bureaucratic system. The protagonist, a female time agent known only as Employee 7204, is assigned a mission that seems simple on paper: travel back to 1903 and prevent Albert Einstein from developing his theory of relativity, which the future bureaucracy has determined is the root cause of all subsequent technological warfare and destruction.

Upon arriving in the past, the agent attempts various methods to kill or discredit Einstein. Each attempt hilariously backfires. She tries to poison him, but Einstein's dietary habits make the poison ineffective. She attempts to discredit him scientifically, only to discover she doesn't understand the mathematics well enough. She tries more direct methods, but circumstances conspire against her.

Central to the comedy is a device the agent brings with her—a long pole with a mirror on the end that allows her to take self-portraits, an invention that the period-appropriate characters find completely baffling. This "selfie stick" decades before its time becomes one of the film's most memorable elements.

As the mission fails repeatedly, the agent begins to develop a personal relationship with Einstein, coming to understand the human cost of her bureaucratic mission. The final twist reveals that her very presence in the past may have been instrumental in inspiring Einstein's greatest insights—creating a causal paradox that questions whether any of her actions truly mattered.

The film's comedy operates on multiple levels: surface-level physical humor and timing, deeper political satire about bureaucratic thinking, and philosophical questions about determinism and free will. The ending suggests that some things are simply meant to happen, and no amount of temporal interference can change the fundamental nature of discovery and genius.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Bureaucracy and Irrational Systems:** The film is a sharp critique of bureaucratic thinking taken to its logical extreme. The future society has reduced all of human history to a spreadsheet, identifying Einstein's theory as a "mistake" that led to nuclear weapons and warfare. This satire of technocratic planning suggests that systems designed to optimize outcomes often fail to account for the messy, unpredictable nature of human progress.

**The Nature of Genius and Discovery:** By focusing on Einstein specifically, the film asks whether great discoveries are inevitable—if not Einstein, then someone else would have developed the theory of relativity. The movie suggests that certain knowledge, once reachable, cannot be unlearned, and that attempting to suppress ideas is ultimately futile.

**Time, Causality, and Paradox:** The narrative plays with temporal paradoxes in comedic ways. The agent's presence in the past may have actually inspired Einstein rather than hindered him, suggesting that time travel stories need not be bound by strict logic—comedy allows for happy contradictions.

**Prescient Technology:** The "selfie stick" device, appearing in 1970, is remarkably prescient. Whether intentional or accidental, this anachronism makes the film a fascinating artifact of Cold War-era speculation about future technology.

**Eastern European Humor:** The film represents a specific tradition of Czechoslovak comedy that blends absurdist humor with social commentary. Its relative obscurity outside Eastern Europe represents a broader gap in Western appreciation of Central European science fiction and comedy traditions.

I Killed Einstein, Gentlemen remains significant as a unique piece of Cold War-era sci-fi comedy that predicted technological trends decades in advance while serving as a witty reminder that some ideas—even dangerous ones—are perhaps inevitable in the march of human progress.
