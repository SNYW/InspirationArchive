# Ten Billion Days and One Hundred Billion Nights (1967)

## The Pitch

Ten Billion Days and One Hundred Billion Nights is a landmark Japanese philosophical science fantasy novel that weaves together mythology, religion, and science fiction across cosmic timescales. The story follows three figures—Plato, Buddha, and Jesus Christ—as they are transported through time to witness the end of the world and the ultimate truths of existence. Originally published in Japan in 1967, the novel is considered one of the most influential works of Japanese science fiction, blending Eastern and Western philosophical traditions into a unique cosmic narrative. The book was later adapted into a manga by renowned shōnen-ai author Moto Hagio in the late 1970s, expanding its cultural impact.

## The Truth

The novel opens with the philosopher Plato, who has been tasked with determining the "true systems of the world"—a calculation that will take ten billion days (approximately 27 million years) to complete. During his journey through time, Plato encounters various civilizations and witnesses the evolution and decline of humanity multiple times.

Meanwhile, Jesus of Nazareth, Siddhartha Gautama (the Buddha), and the demigod Asura travel together through one hundred billion nights into the far future. Their journey brings them to Earth 5,670,000 years in the future, where they find a desolate planet where humanity has been replaced by evolved cyborg descendants. The trio becomes embroiled in a brutal war between these cybernetic beings, eventually fighting each other on the ruined Earth.

The narrative explores themes of alien bioengineering of Earth, the lost civilization of Atlantis, and the ultimate fate of human consciousness and evolution. The story tackles questions of divinity, the nature of time, and the cyclical nature of civilization through its interwoven journeys across cosmic epochs.

## The Core

**Religious and Philosophical Synthesis:** The novel represents a unique Japanese approach to combining Western and Eastern religious traditions. By placing Plato, Buddha, and Jesus together as travelers through time, Mitsuse creates a dialogue between Greco-Western philosophy, Buddhism, and Christianity—three major wisdom traditions exploring ultimate truths. This syncretic approach was groundbreaking in 1960s Japanese science fiction and influenced later works in the genre.

**Cosmic Scale and Time:** Mitsuse's treatment of time is perhaps the novel's most distinctive feature. The title itself—Ten Billion Days and One Hundred Billion Nights—emphasizes the vast timescales involved. Rather than focusing on near-future technological speculation, the novel embraces a cosmic perspective that places human civilization within the larger context of geological and astronomical time.

**Evolution and Transhumanism:** The novel presciently explores themes of technological evolution and transhumanism. The cyborg inhabitants of the far-future Earth represent humanity's potential evolutionary descendants—beings that have transcended biological limitations but retained traces of their human origins. This theme anticipates later cyberpunk and post-human science fiction.

**Cultural Translation Legacy:** The novel's later adaptation by Moto Hagio as a manga helped introduce its philosophical themes to wider audiences. This cross-media legacy demonstrates how Japanese science fiction often moves fluidly between literary and visual forms, with foundational works being reinterpreted for new generations.
