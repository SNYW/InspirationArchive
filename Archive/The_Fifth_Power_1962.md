# The Fifth Power (1962)

Tags: 🌍 Brazil | 🎭 Tense | 📚 Dystopia | 🧬 Rebellion, Megacorps

**Medium:** Film
**Raw Score:** 3.85/5
**Total Ratings:** 84
**Creator/Author:** Alberto Pieralisi (Director), Carlos Pedregal (Writer/Producer)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

The Fifth Power (O 5º Poder) is a 1962 Brazilian science fiction thriller that stands as one of the earliest serious entries in Brazilian sci-fi cinema. Directed by Italian-born Alberto Pieralisi and written by Spanish-born journalist Carlos Pedregal, the film tells the story of foreign agents who install subliminal messaging devices on Brazil's radio and television transmitters to manipulate the population into supporting a revolution that would give them control of the country's vast natural resources.

Produced by Pedregal Filmes and shot in Rio de Janeiro with iconic locations including the Sugarloaf Cable Car and Christ the Redeemer, The Fifth Power was at the time the most expensive film ever made in Brazil. The film was selected to represent Brazil at the 13th Berlin International Film Festival. However, tragedy struck when most copies were destroyed in a fire shortly after release, and the film was considered lost for decades until a copy was rediscovered in Berlin. Contemporary critics have compared it favorably to works by William Cameron Manges and Edgar G. Ulmer, with some even noting its sequence at Christ the Redeemer predated and surpassed similar scenes in Moonraker.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

A team of foreign agents arrives in Brazil with a sinister international plot: to take control of the country's enormous natural resources through a sophisticated scheme of mass manipulation. Their method is chillingly prescient for its time—the installation of devices on the country's main radio and television broadcasting towers that emit subliminal messages designed to alter the behavior of the listening public.

The agents' plan unfolds methodically. Through the invisible weapon of subliminal programming, they begin transforming the Brazilian populace into what the film describes as "a pliable and aggressive mob" supportive of a violent revolution. The messages work on the subconscious mind, gradually turning citizens against their own government without their awareness.

The story centers on journalist Carlos, who is out of the country on assignment when the subliminal broadcasts begin, and his friend Laura, who does not listen to the radio or watch television. These two are uniquely unaffected by the messages and soon realize that something has gone terribly wrong in their homeland. Brazil has become a nation of unexplained violence, suicides, crimes of passion, and mass hysteria—with the news headlines showing a country descending into madness.

As Carlos and Laura investigate, they uncover the agents' plot to overthrow the government. Their investigation leads them through Rio de Janeiro's most iconic landmarks, including a dramatic confrontation at the Christ the Redeemer statue and an action sequence on the Sugarloaf Cable Car. The film builds to a thrilling climax as the heroes race to stop the subliminal messages and expose the foreign conspiracy before Brazil falls into total chaos.

The film's context adds another layer of significance: it was released two years before the 1964 Brazilian coup d'état, which overthrew the democratically elected government. Contemporary analysis notes that film's portrayal of mass manipulation and manufactured political upheaval proved eerily prophetic.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**The Power of Media Manipulation:** The Fifth Power is remarkably prescient in its depiction of media as a weapon of mass control. Written by Carlos Pedregal—who actually introduced the concept of subliminal advertising to Brazil in 1951—the film anticipates by decades contemporary concerns about fake news, algorithmic manipulation, and the weaponization of mass media. The idea that an entire population could be turned against itself through invisible messaging seems almost prophetic in the age of social media and targeted disinformation.

**Foreign Intervention and Resource Colonialism:** The foreign agents represent a form of neocolonial extraction—plotting not military invasion but psychological conquest to seize Brazil's natural resources. This theme resonates with Latin American history and ongoing debates about foreign economic influence. The film's message that external forces can manipulate a nation from within without open warfare is remarkably sophisticated for its era.

**The Individual vs. The Masses:** Carlos and Laura's isolation—ironically caused by Carlos's international assignments and Laura's media-free lifestyle—becomes their salvation. The film suggests that those who remain outside the dominant media ecosystem can see truth that the manipulated masses cannot. This tension between individual consciousness and mass hypnosis would become a recurring theme in science fiction.

**Prescient Political Vision:** Perhaps most significant is how The Fifth Power anticipated Brazil's real-world political trajectory. Released in 1962, it depicted manufactured social chaos leading to governmental overthrow—a direct parallel to the 1964 coup that installed a military dictatorship. The film's warnings about subliminal manipulation proved tragically accurate, as the subsequent regime would employ extensive psychological operations against the Brazilian population.

**Technical Achievement:** The Fifth Power was groundbreaking technically. At the time, it was the most expensive Brazilian film ever made, featuring location shooting at iconic Rio landmarks. The action sequences—especially the battle at Christ the Redeemer—were described by contemporary critics as breathtaking and technically innovative, predating similar set pieces in major Hollywood productions by over a decade.
