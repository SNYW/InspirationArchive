# Perestroika (1990)

**Tags:** 🌍 Soviet-Union | 🎭 Satirical | 📚 Dystopia | 🧬 Politics, Satire

**Medium:** Video Game
**Raw Score:** 3.5/5 (7/10) 
**Total Ratings:** 4 
**Creator/Author:** Locis (Nikita Skripkin, Aleksander Okrug, Dmitry Chikin)

## The Pitch

Perestroika (also known as Toppler) is an extraordinarily rare Soviet MS-DOS puzzle game released in 1990 by a small development team called Locis. Named after Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev's policy of "restructuring," this game is a fascinating time capsule of late Soviet-era political satire packaged as a simple arcade experience. The player controls a small frog-like creature jumping between lily pads, collecting colored dots and avoiding "bureaucrats" while trying to reach a coin that symbolizes a higher standard of living. The splash screen features a stylized Gorbachev whose presence causes the Kremlin to break apart—a bold political statement for 1990 Soviet Union. The game was also ported to ZX Spectrum by a Czech programmer group under the name "Perestrojka."

## The Truth

The gameplay centers on controlling a frog that jumps from one lily pad to another in a metaphor for navigating the chaotic landscape of Soviet reform. Each lily pad represents the ever-changing laws and acts of the Perestroika era—constantly shrinking and disappearing only to reappear elsewhere. The player collects dots in four colors: blue symbolizes grocery goods, red represents currency transactions, yellow represents taxes, and brown represents business ventures. A currency transaction can sometimes add extra life, a tax reduces the score, and a company can sometimes sink the player's figure.

Starting from the seventh level, the game introduces one or more evil creatures called "bureaucrats" that follow the frog and attempt to catch it. The frog dies if the lily pad on which it is standing disappears, if the player moves it to a place where there is no lily pad, or if it is caught by a bureaucrat. The frog symbolizes a democrat navigating the impossible landscape of Soviet change, while the bureaucrat enemies represent the entrenched Soviet administrative apparatus resisting reform.

In 1995, Nikita Online released a remake called "Toppler" for Windows 3.1x, keeping all gameplay identical but replacing Gorbachev's face on the title screen with a frog, and substituting the coin for advancing to the next level with a lily flower. The game can still be played today using DOSBox emulation.

## The Core

Perestroika stands as a remarkable artifact of late Soviet-era political expression through interactive media. Created during the brief window of Glasnost and Perestroika when political satire became possible, the game directly engages with the contradictions and chaos of Soviet "restructuring." The frog as a symbol of the democrat trying to navigate impossible circumstances, the ever-changing lily pads representing unstable policies, and most importantly, the bureaucrats as enemies—these all reflect the popular sentiment toward Soviet bureaucracy during the dissolution.

The game is significant not for its gameplay innovation (it draws clear inspiration from earlier games like Nebulus/Tower Toppler) but for its context and timing. Released in 1990, it captures a unique historical moment when Soviet citizens could openly satirize their government through entertainment media. The ZX Spectrum Czech variant ("Perestrojka") further demonstrates how the themes of political transformation resonated across the Eastern Bloc.

Today, Perestroika represents a fascinating intersection of gaming history, political history, and digital preservation. It demonstrates how video games can serve as historical documents—preserving the mood, anxieties, and even dark humor of a society in transformation. For scholars of Soviet culture and digital humanities, it offers a rare glimpse into interactive media's role in one of the 20th century's most significant political transformations.
