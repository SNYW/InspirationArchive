## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

The Next One (also released as "The Time Traveler") is a 1984 Greek science fiction film directed by Nico Mastorakis, known for his work in genre cinema throughout the 1970s and 1980s. The film stars Keir Dullea (best known as Dave Bowman from 2001: A Space Odyssey) and Adrienne Barbeau. Set on a tranquil Greek island, the story follows Andrea Johnson, an American widow living with her young son Timmy, who discovers a naked amnesiac man washed up on the beach following a storm. The stranger, who bears the number "01313-1" marked on his skin, exhibits mysterious powers including healing abilities and resurrection of the dead—ultimately revealed to be an entity from another dimension or the future.

The film emerged during the tail end of renewed interest in cinematic UFOs following Spielberg's Close Encounters of the Third Kind (1977) and E.T. (1982). Critics have noted it as an intriguing example of science fiction blending with religious and messianic themes, drawing explicit parallels between the alien visitor and Jesus Christ. Colin Greenland reviewed the film for Imagine magazine, stating it was "an awkward movie, but a thoughtful attempt to connect ancient and modern mythology."

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film opens on a peaceful Greek island where Andrea Johnson and her young son Timmy live quietly following her astronaut husband's death. After a violent storm, they discover a naked man washed up on the beach, unconscious and bearing the number "01313-1" tattooed on his skin. The stranger has no memory of his identity, so Andrea names him "Glenn."

As Glenn integrates into their household, Andrea and he become romantic partners, with Glenn also serving as a father figure for Timmy. However, strange phenomena begin to surround the mysterious stranger. Glenn possesses two hearts—a fact revealed during a medical examination—and demonstrates the ability to make himself invisible at will. Most remarkably, he can resurrect the dead, bringing back a local villager who dies in an accident.

As Glenn's memory gradually returns, he reveals to Andrea that he originates from another dimension, not merely from the future. He explains that his brother had arrived on Earth two thousand years ago during the time of Jesus Christ, was crucified for being "too perfect," and that Glenn has been searching for him. This establishes the film's central messianic allegory, with Glenn as a Christ-like figure come to Earth.

A subplot involves Yanni, a resentful local who desires Andrea romantically and becomes jealous of Glenn. Yanni stirrs trouble around the island, eventually framing Glenn for a murder he did not commit. Glenn is arrested on Good Friday, deliberately echoing the crucifixion narrative. During his incarceration, the symbolism becomes explicit—Glenn likens himself to Christ, and the film shows him recognizing the face of Christ in church icons.

The resolution involves Glenn's realization that humanity must be allowed to work through its own imperfections rather than having salvation imposed upon them. This represents a repudiation of the Christian message—rather than delivering humanity from above, Glenn comes to understand that humans must find their own way. The film ends with Glenn departing, having influenced Andrea and Timmy but ultimately choosing not to intervene directly in human destiny.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Alien as Christ Figure:** The Next One explicitly explores the parallels between UFO visitation narratives and religious prophecy. Glenn serves as a messianic figure whose arrival echoes Christ's—born of another realm, possessing miraculous powers (healing, resurrection), and ultimately rejected by humanity. The film's deliberate scheduling of Glenn's arrest on Good Friday cements this parallel. However, unlike traditional religious narratives, the film suggests that such intervention may be counterproductive—a being "too perfect" cannot help humanity grow.

**The UFO-Religion Connection:** The film emerges from a tradition of combining alien visitation with religious themes, reflecting 1970s UFO cult interests and writers like Erich Von Däniken who proposed extraterrestrial explanations for religious miracles. Mastorakis explicitly draws connections between faith in UFOs and faith in religion—both require belief in the unseen, both involve "visitations" from otherworldly beings.

**Greek Mythology and Modern Context:** The Greek island setting is not incidental. Greece serves as a bridge between ancient mythology and modern science fiction—the same land that gave rise to gods and heroes now hosts an encounter with an entity from beyond. The film uses this setting to connect ancient religious narratives (Greek, Christian) with contemporary science fiction tropes.

**Free Will vs. Divine Intervention:** Glenn's ultimate revelation—that humanity must work through its own imperfections—represents a philosophical stance on the relationship between higher powers and human development. The film suggests that salvation imposed from above is ultimately hollow; growth must come from within. This humanist message tempers the messianic imagery.

**Isolation and Reintegration:** The protagonist Andrea represents a widow (of an astronaut—a man who reached for the stars) isolated from mainstream society. Glenn's arrival represents both an interruption of her peace and an opportunity for reintegration into meaning-making—first through a personal relationship, then through cosmic significance.

The Next One remains a considerable oddity in the science fiction canon—a Greek film that explicitly engages with both UFO mythology and Christian symbolism, offering a thoughtful (if heavy-handed) meditation on intervention, perfection, and human autonomy.
