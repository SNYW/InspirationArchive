## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Pretty Women Walking Through the City (original title: Lijepe žene prolaze kroz grad) is a 1986 Yugoslav post-apocalyptic science fiction film directed by Želimir Žilnik, one of the most prominent figures of the Yugoslav Black Wave cinema movement. Set in Belgrade in the year 2041, the film presents a haunting vision of a future Yugoslavia that has collapsed into environmental decay and social fragmentation. The story follows a group of elderly men attempting to raise young girls in the traditional values of their ancestors, while the world around them has deteriorated into a desolate wasteland.

What makes this film remarkable is its prophetic nature—Žilnik made this film in 1986, just years before the actual breakup of Yugoslavia, and the film uncannily predicted the nationalist tensions and social disintegration that would tear the nation apart in the early 1990s. The film was produced as part of a wave of Yugoslav cinema exploring socio-political criticisms, and it stands as a chilling artifact that anticipated one of the most violent dissolutions of a modern state.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film is set in Belgrade in the year 2041, presented as a deserted urban landscape that has been transformed into something resembling a dump yard. The once-vibrant city has fallen into severe decay, with the environment polluted and society having collapsed from its former modernist glory.

The narrative centers on a small community of elderly men who have taken it upon themselves to raise a group of young girls. These men cling desperately to the traditional values and cultural heritage of their Yugoslav ancestors, attempting to preserve a sense of identity and continuity in a world that has otherwise collapsed. They live in the ruins of the old city, trying to maintain a semblance of the old ways while the world around them has moved on or been destroyed.

The film explores the tension between preservation and decay, as the old men try to teach the girls about a Yugoslavia that no longer exists—all that remains are fragments of memory, cultural practices, and perhaps an idealized past. The girls represent the future, but what kind of future can exist in a world of environmental destruction and social fragmentation?

The setting of Belgrade in 2041 serves as a character in itself—a city that has experienced some catastrophic event or gradual collapse that has left it in ruins. The urban landscape is shown as a place of emptiness, where the grand modernist ambitions of Yugoslav socialism have given way to decay and abandonment.

The film does not provide easy answers or clear explanations for how the world reached this state. Instead, it presents a meditative portrait of survival and cultural memory, focusing on the human impulse to preserve identity and tradition even in the face of total collapse.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

**Prophetic Vision of Yugoslav Dissolution:** Perhaps the most significant aspect of Pretty Women Walking Through the City is its remarkable prophetic quality. Made in 1986, Žilnik's film predicted with unsettling accuracy the nationalist tensions and ethnic conflicts that would lead to the breakup of Yugoslavia in the early 1990s. The film suggests that the seeds of destruction were already present in the social fabric of Yugoslavia—regional rivalries, ethnic tensions, and the failure of socialist modernism to create a unified national identity. The post-apocalyptic setting can be read as an allegory for the violence and fragmentation that was to come, making the film a chilling historical document.

**Environmental Collapse and Modernist Failure:** The film presents a world where environmental degradation has played a significant role in societal collapse. Belgrade in 2041 is shown as a polluted, ruined city—a far cry from the modernist dreams of socialist Yugoslavia. This environmental decay serves as a critique of the modernist project itself, suggesting that the industrial development and modernization pursued by Yugoslav socialism led not to progress but to ecological catastrophe. The wasteland setting comments on the unsustainable path of industrial modernity.

**Cultural Preservation and Memory:** The elderly men's attempt to raise the girls in traditional Yugoslav values represents a desperate effort to preserve cultural memory in a world that has lost its bearings. The film explores what it means to cling to tradition and identity when the world around you has collapsed. This theme resonates with broader questions about how nations and cultures survive catastrophic change—whether through adaptation or rigid adherence to the past.

**Gender and Generational Dynamics:** The film centers on the relationship between elderly men and young girls, presenting a reversed caretaking dynamic. The old men are attempting to transmit cultural knowledge to a younger generation, but this transmission is complicated by the collapse of the world they remember. The girls represent both the hope for continuity and the uncertainty of what kind of world they will build.

**The Ruins of Socialist Modernism:** Žilnik, as a filmmaker who was famously critical of the Yugoslav communist regime, uses the post-apocalyptic setting to comment on the failures of socialist modernism. The ruins of Belgrade symbolize not just physical decay but the bankruptcy of a political and economic system. The film's dystopian vision suggests that the contradictions inherent in the Yugoslav system—between nationalism and brotherhood, between central planning and regional autonomy—would ultimately lead to collapse.

Pretty Women Walking Through the City remains a haunting and prescient work—a science fiction film that turned out to be a documentary of things to come. Žilnik's ability to foresee the dissolution of Yugoslavia through this apocalyptic lens makes the film essential viewing for understanding both Yugoslav history and the genre of dystopian cinema.
