## The Pitch

Set in Buenos Aires, The Eternaut begins as mysterious deadly snowfall blankets the city, killing everything it touches. Juan Salvo, his wife Elena, and his daughter Martita survive by remaining sealed in their home while friends who venture outside are killed by the snowflakes. As society collapses into chaos, Salvo discovers the snowfall is actually an alien invasion—and the snow is just the first wave. Joining an improvised resistance army, Salvo battles giant insectoid aliens controlled by mysterious "Hands" at iconic Buenos Aires locations like the River Plate Stadium, until a devastating nuclear strike destroys the city. What follows is a mind-bending journey through time and parallel universes that ultimately reveals a profound truth about fate, memory, and the nature of time itself.

## The Truth

The story opens with Juan Salvo appearing in the home of comic book writer Héctor Germán Oesterheld in Buenos Aires, having traveled through time and space. The entire narrative is Salvo's account of what happened.

In 1950s Buenos Aires, Salvo is playing cards with friends when deadly snow begins falling. His neighbors venture outside and die horrible deaths upon contact with the snowflakes. Salvo, his wife Elena, and daughter Martita survive because their home was completely sealed. As the catastrophe unfolds, society breaks down into violence and anarchy.

The group learns the snowfall is part of an alien invasion. The "snow" is actually alien devices. The survivors join an improvised army that battles giant beetle-like insects armed with "lightthrowers" at the General Paz highway. The insects are controlled by devices on their necks, revealing they are remote-controlled by an alien called the "Hand."

The army makes its base inside the River Plate Stadium, using its walls as protection. Salvo and a metal worker named Franco conduct reconnaissance and discover the Hand controls other survivors who have been turned into automatons. The aliens are themselves controlled by "Them"—an unseen alien force.

The army is lured into a trap at Plaza Italia and decimated by giant beasts called "Gurbos." Only Salvo, Favalli, and Franco survive. They discover the main invasion force at Plaza del Congress, blow up the dome containing "Them," and escape just before foreign countries nuke Buenos Aires.

The invasion continues as aliens lure survivors to fake "snow-free zones." Favalli, Pablo, and the historian Mosca allow themselves to be captured and turned into automatons so Salvo and his family can escape. They seize a spaceship, and Salvo accidentally activates a time machine, ending up in a pocket universe with his family separated.

Salvo learns to travel between universes and timelines, eventually appearing at Oesterheld's house. After finishing his narration, he realizes he's in Buenos Aires a few years before the invasion. He rushes home, reunites with his family in a stable time loop, and forgets everything. Oesterheld, knowing the future invasion is inevitable, decides to write a comic about it.

The comic is notable for its author Héctor Germán Oesterheld, who was later disappeared during Argentina's Dirty War (1977) for his work with the Montoneros guerrilla group. His widow Elsa Sánchez de Oesterheld and his grandchildren finally won back the rights to his works in a 2018 Supreme Court ruling.

## The Core

The Eternaut operates on multiple allegorical levels. Most obviously, it's a post-apocalyptic survival story about resisting alien occupation—but the political undertones are unmistakable. The original 1957-1959 publication came during Argentina's political instability, and the 1969 remake explicitly added anti-imperialist and anti-dictatorial themes, suggesting global powers abandoned Latin America to the invaders to save themselves.

Oesterheld was writing an anti-war comic disguised as science fiction. The aliens aren't truly evil—they're "noble beings forced to carry out the orders of others," forced to invade and destroy by their masters. This mirrors his later work with Montoneros and his views on Argentina's military dictatorship.

The time loop structure is brilliant: Salvo experiences the invasion, escapes through time, returns to warn Oesterheld, goes back in time to prevent it (forgetting everything), and Oesterheld—now knowing the future—writes the comic anyway. This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy where fiction becomes the only way to process impending disaster.

What makes this essential for worldbuilding: it's one of the first (if not the first) post-apocalyptic alien invasion stories to center a non-American city, grounding cosmic horror in specific Argentine geography and culture. The Buenos Aires landmarks—River Plate Stadium, Plaza del Congreso, General Paz highway—transformed from familiar urban spaces into war zones, making the alien invasion viscerally local in a way American sci-fi never achieved.
