# The First Men (1960)

**Medium:** Short Story
**Creator/Author:** Howard Fast

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)
In 1960, Howard Fast wrote a thought-provoking short story about a secret government experiment to raise super-intelligent children with telepathic abilities. A group of scientists and educators, led by psychologists Jean and Mark Arbalaid, are given a secluded compound in California to raise 40 gifted children over 15 years. The children rapidly develop superhuman mental and physical abilities, including telepathy, and begin thinking as a unified collective. When the government prepares to investigate the experiment, the children create an impenetrable time-shield around their compound and begin secretly recruiting other gifted children. The story explores themes of human potential, intelligence, and whether humanity is ready for its own evolution.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)
The story unfolds through a series of communiqués between Jean Arbalaid and her brother Harry Felton, a retired military officer. Jean, a renowned psychologist, believes children are products of their environment and proposes an experiment to prove her hypothesis.

Jean and her husband Mark convince the US government to sponsor a secret program at an isolated 8,000-acre compound in California. They recruit 40 naturally gifted children (orphans and those they could acquire) and surround them with educators who act as teacher-parents. The children are immersed in a knowledge-rich environment from birth.

By age five, the children begin discovering their telepathic abilities. As they grow, they develop unparalleled understanding of mathematics, physics, and other technical subjects. They also excel physically, breaking numerous sports records. Crucially, they develop a shared consciousness through telepathy—they think and communicate as one mind.

The children walk nude, make love openly, and possess unmatched knowledge. They love their teacher-parents but pity their inferior intelligence. When the researchers' own children are born at the facility, even these "normal" children flourish under the older children's tutelage.

Near the end of the fifteen-year period, as government investigators approach, Jean worries about how authorities will react to this new race of "super-intelligent demigods." She obtains extensions but finally receives a letter from her sister explaining what happened: the children created a gray, impenetrable barrier—a slice of time shifted a fraction of a millisecond into the future. They can pass through easily, but outsiders cannot penetrate it.

Inside the shield, the children are summoning other gifted children telepathically, planning to create more hidden compounds to raise more super-intelligent humans. Jean's letter ends on a hopeful note: perhaps this new race can "turn the tide of humanity." However, a White House operative concludes that once they crack the shield's basis, they'll "eradicate the disease."

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)
"The First Men" explores several profound themes: the ethics of genetic and environmental engineering, the tension between individual human potential and collective evolution, and society's fear of superior intelligence. Fast questions whether humanity is ready to evolve beyond its current form—whether the emergence of a superior race would be celebrated or destroyed. The story suggests that true human progress may require leaving behind conventional humanity, but also acknowledges the danger such evolution poses to the existing social order. It's a nuanced take on utopian possibilities and dystopian fears, wrapped in a genuinely optimistic premise about raising human potential.
