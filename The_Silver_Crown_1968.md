# The Silver Crown (1968)

**Medium:** Novel/Children's Science Fiction
**Creator/Author:** Robert C. O'Brien

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

On her tenth birthday, Ellen Carroll receives a mysterious silver crown that appears in her bedroom overnight. This seemingly innocent gift sets off a chain of extraordinary events—she witnesses a murder, her house burns down, and she finds herself hunted by sinister men. Alongside a young boy named Otto, Ellen journeys through the mountains, evading kidnappers and assassins, until she arrives at a black castle where a medieval device called the Hieronymus Machine lies dormant. This machine, capable of mind control, has been turning ordinary people into dangerous agents for an unknown purpose. With the power of the silver crown, Ellen must decide whether to embrace her destiny as queen or destroy a device that could enslave humanity.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The story begins with Ellen Carroll turning ten years old. On the morning of her birthday, she discovers a mysterious silver crown in her bedroom. That same day, her house burns down while she's at the park, and she witnesses a murder—a man is killed in front of her. Now orphaned and alone, Ellen decides to hitchhike to her Aunt Sarah in Kentucky.

During her journey, Ellen encounters numerous dangers. She escapes from a kidnapper, is chased through the woods by mysterious men, and eventually teams up with Otto, an eight-year-old boy who becomes her companion. Together, they trek through the mountains while continuing to be hunted by the sinister forces that seem to be connected to her mysterious crown.

Their journey leads them to a black castle deep in the forest, where they discover the Hieronymus Machine—a medieval device that can control people's minds. The machine has been operation for centuries, creating a network of assassins and arsonists who serve its unknown purpose. The machine requires two crowns to operate: one black and one silver. Ellen possesses the silver crown, which is apparently the more powerful of the two.

At the castle, Ellen learns the dark truth about the machine and its creator. The machine was built by followers of St. Jerome who had drifted into occult sciences many centuries ago in Spain. The device can control human minds, turning ordinary people into loyal servants who carry out various missions without question.

Despite being tempted by the offer to become queen of this strange kingdom, Ellen ultimately decides that the machine is too dangerous to exist. She destroys the Hieronymus Machine, choosing to reject the power it offers in favor of preventing its potential for harm.

In the original version of the novel, some adults speculate that the Machine was responsible for sending the crown to Ellen and arranging the events that drew her to the castle. This explanation provides closure for readers. However, in the revised British edition, the ending was changed—Ellen simply says she found the crown at "a little curio shop in Barcelona," removing any connection to the Hieronymus Machine.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

The Silver Crown explores several important themes that resonate beyond its surface-level adventure story.

**The Temptation of Power:** Central to the novel is the question of how individuals respond to power. Ellen is offered dominion over a vast network of controlled minds, yet she chooses to reject this power. This reflects the book's suggestion that true courage often means refusing to exploit others, even when one has the means to do so.

**The Nature of Control:** The Hieronymus Machine represents the ultimate form of manipulation—the ability to strip away free will and turn humans into mere instruments of another's will. By destroying the machine, Ellen affirms the primacy of individual freedom and autonomy over organized control.

**Coming of Age:** As a ten-year-old protagonist, Ellen must navigate adult world dangers and make impossible choices. Her journey represents the transition from childhood innocence to a more complex understanding of moral responsibility.

**The Thin Line Between Fantasy and Reality:** O'Brien masterfully blurs the boundaries between fantasy and science fiction, suggesting that what seems magical may have scientific explanations, and vice versa. This reflects broader questions about the nature of reality and perception.

**Critique of Unquestioning Obedience:** The novel critiques systems that demand blind obedience, showing how the Machine exploits and corrupts human potential. The followers of the Machine are stripped of their individual identities and turned into unthinking agents—essentially losing their humanity.

The Silver Crown stands as an early example of children's literature tackling sophisticated themes of autonomy, power, and moral choice, making it a noteworthy entry in the science fiction canon for younger readers.
