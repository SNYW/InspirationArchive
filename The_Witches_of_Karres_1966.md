# The Witches of Karres (1966)

**Medium:** Novel
**Creator/Author:** James H. Schmitz

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

Captain Pausert is an inexperienced merchant traveler on a solo voyage through space, piloting an old pirate chaser called the Venture. While stopping at the planet Porlumma, he witnesses three young sisters—Maleen, Goth, and the Leewit—being sold as slaves. Moved by sympathy, he purchases their freedom and helps them escape. But these aren't ordinary children: they're witches from the forbidden planet Karres, possessing powerful psionic abilities and a revolutionary faster-than-light drive called the Sheewash Drive. What begins as an act of compassion thrusts Pausert into a galaxy-spanning adventure involving interdimensional alien invaders, space pirates, and governments desperate to capture the secret of the witches' incredible technology. This is a rollicking space opera with humor, heart, and plenty of action—lighter in tone than typical science fiction of its era.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The story opens with Captain Pausert, a young merchant traveler from the planet Nikkeldepain, voyaging solo aboard the Venture. While on Porlumma, he encounters three young girls—Maleen (about 14), Goth (9 or 10), and the Leewit (5 or 6)—who had been enslaved after their own spacecraft crashed during a pleasure trip. Moved by their plight, Pausert uses his limited funds to purchase their freedom.

During the escape from Porlumma, the girls desperately engage what they call the Sheewash Drive, enabling the Venture to outrun pursuit at speeds impossible for conventional space drives. Pausert learns the girls are witches from Karres—a prohibited planet—possessing powerful psychic abilities known as klatha. Their psionic powers and especially the impossibly fast Sheewash Drive attract dangerous attention from various planetary governments and the Imperium itself.

Pausert takes the sisters to their homeworld of Karres, only to find himself facing a barrage of criminal charges upon returning to Nikkeldepain—many relating to his association with the witches and his visit to the forbidden planet. The planetary government is desperate to obtain the suspected new space drive technology.

With the help of Goth, who stowed away on the Venture, Pausert escapes Nikkeldepain and heads to Uldune—a former pirate stronghold now serving as a galactic marketplace where anything can be bought or sold. They rebuild the ship under a new name, "Evening Bird," and assume new identities. During this time, Pausert begins developing minor klatha powers of his own.

Their new trading venture draws the attention of both planetary authorities and the Imperium. They run afoul of industrial espionage, kidnapping attempts, and political intrigue. The ruler of Uldune, believing both Pausert and Goth are witches, entrusts them with a mysterious payload: a frightening rocky mass and a catatonic witch found with it.

The voyage becomes increasingly strange as fellow crew members Vezzarn and Hulik do Eldel are revealed to be spies attempting to locate the Sheewash Drive. Vezzarn uncovers the mystery mass, attracting dangerous alien attention. The situation escalates as they navigate the treacherous Chaladoor region of space, with the mysterious payload playing a key role in the eventual resolution.

Through cleverness, courage, and the witches' psionic powers, Pausert and his companions ultimately triumph over their pursuers and the interdimensional threats. The story concludes with the protagonists having proven themselves against overwhelming odds while forging unexpected bonds of loyalty and friendship.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

The Witches of Karres stands out as a remarkable piece of science fiction for several reasons. First, Schmitz presents a more optimistic, tolerant universe than was common in 1960s space opera. His galaxies are places where humans and aliens can coexist, where ordinary people can live viable lives beyond the constant threat of existential monsters.

The novel features remarkably progressive characterization for its era. Schmitz was decades ahead of the curve in portraying strong female characters. The witches—particularly Goth—are competent, resourceful, and completely equal to their male counterparts without relying on sexual role-playing clichés. This was groundbreaking at a time when female characters in adventure fiction were typically relegated to damsel-in-distress roles.

The story explores themes of freedom versus oppression, as the witches escape slavery and must defend their autonomy against governments and corporations seeking to exploit their abilities and technology. It also examines the ethics of power—both technological (the Sheewash Drive) and psionic (klatha)—and the responsibility that comes with possessing such advantages.

Schmitz's tolerance and sympathy extend even to potential antagonists. His aliens and monsters are often revealed to have their own agendas and perspectives from which their actions are justified or even admirable. This nuanced view of "the other" was almost unique in space opera of the period, which was typically xenophobic.

The novel's lighter tone and humor distinguish it from the often grim and pessimistic science fiction of its era. Rather than exploring dystopian futures or existential dread, The Witches of Karres offers pure adventure—escapism in the best sense, a reminder that science fiction could be fun, optimistic, and entertainment-oriented without sacrificing substance.

The Witches of Karres was nominated for a Hugo Award and remains thought of as Schmitz's best work. It helped establish him as a master of space opera who could combine action, humor, and thoughtful characterization in ways that influenced later writers.
