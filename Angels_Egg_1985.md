# Angel's Egg (1985)

**Medium:** Animated Film (OVA)
**Creator/Author:** Mamoru Oshii (Director/Writer), Yoshitaka Amano (Artist)

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)

In a primordial, post-apocalyptic landscape of ruins, strange technology, and ancient fossils, a young girl wanders alone through desolate cities, clutching a massive egg that she believes will hatch into an angel. When she encounters a mysterious young man who has arrived in a tank, carrying a cross-shaped weapon, the two form an uneasy bond. He wishes to crack open the egg to discover what lies within, while she guards it as her sole hope in a world stripped of meaning. This 71-minute experimental animated film is a meditation on faith, memory, and the loss of civilization, told through haunting, dreamlike imagery and cryptic biblical allusion. Director Mamoru Oshii (later creator of Ghost in the Shell) intended the film as a "visual poem" with minimal narrative, drawing influence from Andrei Tarkovsky and creating what many consider one of the most beautiful and enigmatic works in anime history.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)

The film takes place in a primordial,post-apocalyptic world filled with trees, strange mechanical devices, a giant mechanical eye, and the ruins of cities. The two nameless protagonists exist in this landscape without clear explanation of who they are or how they arrived there.

The story follows a young girl who scavenges through the desolate city, carrying a large egg that she protects with her life. She believes that when the egg hatches, an angel will emerge. The girl is silent throughout most of the film, communicating only through her actions and facial expressions.

A young man arrives in this world by disembarking from a tank. He carries a large cross-shaped weapon on his back. When he first encounters the girl, she runs away from him in fear. He briefly disappears, and the girl continues her journey alone, eventually setting down her egg to rest.

The young man catches up to the girl and briefly steals the egg, returning it only after telling her to keep it closer. Eventually, she allows him to follow her, and the two form a brief bond as they travel together through the ruins.

The boy becomes obsessed with breaking the egg to see what lies inside. He tells the girl the story of Noah's Ark, but with a twist—in his version, the dove that was released from the Ark never returned. The people on the ark eventually forgot about the dove, and forgot about the world they came from. The boy ponders existence and theorizes that the bird never actually existed.

The girl insists the bird did exist and takes the boy to a fossil of a giant bird, attempting to prove her point. However, director Mamoru Oshii has clarified that the angel in the egg is not meant to be the bird from the story.

The film's ending is deliberately ambiguous. Oshii has stated that it represents salvation for the girl, but he deliberately made it difficult to understand. The boy breaks the egg, revealing what is inside—though the film never shows the viewer directly what was within. The ending leaves open questions about whether the girl's faith was rewarded or proven meaningless.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)

Angel's Egg explores several interlocking themes that define Oshii's later work:

**Faith and Doubt**: The girl represents blind faith—she believes in the angel that will hatch from her egg, and this belief gives her purpose in a world of ruins. The boy represents skepticism and rationality—he wants to prove or disprove the existence of what's inside the egg. Their interaction becomes a meditation on whether faith has value even if its object cannot be proven to exist.

**Memory and Loss**: The boy's retelling of Noah's Ark—with the dove that never returned and the forgotten world—serves as an allegory for collective memory and how civilizations forget their origins. The fossilized bird the girl shows the boy represents the physical evidence of something that once lived but no longer exists.

**Dreams and Reality**: Oshii intended the egg to represent dreams and hopes—things that may or may not exist. The intersection of dreams and reality was a theme he explored in earlier works like Urusei Yatsura 2: Beautiful Dreamer, and it would become central to his later films like Ghost in the Shell.

**Biblical Allusion as Aesthetic**: While Oshii is not a Christian, he uses biblical imagery—Noah's Ark, the dove, angels—as a symbolic vocabulary. The references serve more as philosophical and literary inspiration than religious statements. The cross-shaped weapon the boy carries adds to this imagery without making the film explicitly religious.

**The Value of Ambiguity**: Oshii intentionally removed as much narrative as possible, wanting the film to be "a film without clear drama, made out of visual expressions." The cryptic nature isn't a flaw but the point—the film asks viewers to find meaning in imagery rather than plot.

The film is often compared to other experimental animated works like Belladonna of Sadness and Fantastic Planet, but stands as a unique piece—one that divides viewers but is universally acknowledged as visually stunning.
