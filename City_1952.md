# City (1952)

**Medium:** Novel (Fix-up)
**Creator/Author:** Clifford D. Simak

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)
City is a unique fix-up novel composed of eight linked short stories, originally published in Astounding Science Fiction between 1944-1951, plus a ninth tale added in 1973. Told from the perspective of Dogs who recite an oral legend about humanity, the novel traces the rise and fall of human civilization across millennia. Unlike typical dystopian sci-fi, Simak offers a pastoral, contemplative vision—humans abandon cities for decentralized countryside living, ultimately transforming to exist on Jupiter as beings of pure consciousness. The Dogs then inherit Earth, building a pacifist civilization alongside other animals. It's a meditation on loneliness, intelligence, and what it means to be human, wrapped in some of the most unusual worldbuilding in science fiction.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)
The novel is structured as a legend told by Dogs to their pups, with each tale preceded by canine discussion and interpretation.

**The First Tales:** The stories begin with humans abandoning urban centers due to fear of nuclear destruction, but they eventually discover they prefer a pastoral, self-sufficient lifestyle using hydroponic farming and decentralized power. The tales focus on the Webster family and their robot servant, Jenkins. Bruce Webster surgically enables dogs to speak and gives them contact lenses for better vision. As urban society collapses, isolated families become the norm.

**The Rise of the Ants:** A mutant named Joe invents a method for ants to remain active year-round in Wisconsin's climate. The ants build an industrial civilization in their hill, eventually forming a massive "Ant City" that concerns even the Dogs.

**The Jupiter Transformation:** One story, originally published as "Desertion" in 1944, follows a research station on Jupiter—Simak's version is a cold, corrosive hell where only advanced technology permits survival. A scientist and his dog Towser transform into seal-like beings capable of living unaided on Jupiter's surface. They experience Jupiter as paradise, free from the dog's fleas and irritations, communicating telepathically. The transformed humans and dogs choose never to return to the station.

**The Exodus:** Using Joe's mind-trick invention, humanity learns to broadcast meaning directly to others' minds. Most humans choose to leave Earth, abandoning physical form to live transformed on Jupiter. Humanity gradually dies off from loneliness on Earth.

**The Dog Civilization:** Ten thousand years later, Jenkins receives a new body to serve the few remaining humans. The Dog civilization has spread across Earth, introducing other animals to civilization. They become pacifist and vegetarian, managing nature by distributing food to prevent predation. They search for dimensional doors to other worlds.

**The Final Tales:** Realizing humans cannot peacefully coexist with the Dogs and other animals, Jenkins takes the remaining humans to another world via dimensional travel. The human race eventually dies out on this new world.

In the original eight stories' conclusion, Jenkins returns to find the Dogs dealing with the ever-growing Ant City. He visits suspended animation chambers in Geneva where a few humans sleep for advice. He asks on dealing with the ants—human solution would be poisoned bait distributed widely. But the Dogs cannot accept this violent solution and leave Earth for another world.

**The Coda (Epilog, 1973):** Jenkins remains on the original Earth, surrounded by the Ant City. He realizes the Ant City has died. A spaceship returns to take him to the robot worlds. Breaking through the dead ant city, he finds only infinitely repeated sculptures of a human boot kicking over an anthill—a final irony.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)
Simak's vision of human apocalypse is unique: not destruction, but simply isolation and eventual dispersal. The novel reflects wartime attitudes about humanity's inability to live at peace, yet offers something unexpected—a hopeful vision of transformation and transcendence. The Dogs inherit Earth not through conquest but through patience, creating a pacifist civilization that cares for all creatures. The ending is bittersweet: the Dogs' compassion cannot save them from the same loneliness that claimed humanity, yet their choice to leave rather than adopt violent solutions affirms their higher nature. The recurring image of the boot kicking the anthill—humans destroying what they've created—underscores Simak's theme that humanity's fundamental flaw was aggression. Yet the novel doesn't condemn humanity entirely; it suggests transformation and transcendence are possible, even if it means becoming something other than human.
