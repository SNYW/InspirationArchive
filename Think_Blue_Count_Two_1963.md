# Think Blue, Count Two (1963)

**Medium:** Short Story
**Creator/Author:** Cordwainer Smith

## The Pitch (Non-Spoiler Breakdown)
In a future where humanity has spread across the stars, two technicians prepare thirty thousand passengers for a centuries-long interstellar journey to the planet Wereld Schemering. The passengers—selected for their beauty—sleep in suspended animation, while a psychological trip wire watches over them. When a crisis awakens a young woman named Veesey mid-voyage, she must face the darkest aspects of human nature alone in the void of space. This is a haunting meditation on the fragility of civilization and the psychological guards we build to prevent our own destruction.

## The Truth (Full Story Breakdown)
The story opens at a spaceport on Earth, where technicians Greve and Maz are preparing passengers for a long interstellar journey to Wereld Schemering—a planet that has "run dreadfully ugly." Because of this, all thirty thousand passengers have been carefully selected for their physical beauty. Among them is Veesey-koosey, a fifteen-year-old girl designated as a "reserve passenger"—meant to be awakened only in case of emergency. She has no particular skills and is incredibly naive, but her high "daughter potential" means other reserves will instinctively want to protect her.

The technicians carry the grim memory of Old Twenty-two, a previous ship whose reserve passengers awoke mid-journey and, isolated in space, "invented new crimes and committed them upon each other." Fearing for Veesey's safety, they call in specialist Tiga-belas, who installs a psychological guard—a black cube-shaped computer powered by the laminated brain of a mouse—into Veesey's mind.

Three hundred and twenty-six years later, Veesey is awakened along with two other passengers: Trece, a handsome older man, and Talatashar, a man whose face was partially deformed during the freezing process. The ship has stalled because their sailor died of a blood clot, and the solar sail was damaged. The three must work together for a year to repair the ship.

Over time, Talatashar gradually goes mad from isolation and grows resentful toward Trece and Veesey. One day, he restrains Trece and threatens to rape and murder Veesey while forcing him to watch. In desperation, Veesey activates her cube by recalling the codephrase. The device projects vivid shared hallucinations—a warden, Talatashar's mother, a friend for Veesey, and a captain—who deescalate the situation and help them resume their journey.

Safely landed on Wereld Schemering, Talatashar apologizes to Veesey and thanks her for rescuing them. The two remain friends.

## The Core (Themes & Meaning)
Think Blue, Count Two is a profound exploration of humanity's darker impulses and the psychological mechanisms we create to control them. Cordwainer Smith uses the isolated setting of the sleeper ship to examine how civilization's veneer crumbles under pressure and isolation. The story asks whether humanity can truly escape its own nature, or if we are forever bound by our capacity for cruelty.

The psychological trip wire represents humanity's attempt to create external controls for internal moral failures—literally engineering goodness rather than cultivating it. Yet the story also suggests that such mechanisms are imperfect; Veesey must ultimately choose to activate her guard, taking responsibility for her own survival rather than passively relying on external protection.

The title "Think Blue, Count Two" itself is significant—it refers to the psychological triggers embedded in Veesey's mind, representing the artificial constraints placed on human behavior. The story's themes of beauty, selection, and engineered humanity also touch on eugenics and the ethics of selective breeding.

The influence extends beyond science fiction: the story's title was referenced in the 1998 anime series Serial Experiments Lain, where Lain's father uses a corrupted version of the password. This connection highlights the story's prescient exploration of psychological programming and artificial consciousness.
